## Lede

Marcus Blackwood spent the final hours of his life drugging partygoers and extracting their memories. By 1:50 AM the harvest had collapsed. By morning he was the only one who didn't wake up. Twenty people woke up in this warehouse to figure out what had happened. What we uncovered was worse than murder.

The room worked through suspects in turn. Quinn Sterling, coerced into synthesizing the drug. Sarah Blackwood, the wife poised to inherit. Jess Kane, pregnant by the deceased. Morgan Reed, who had leverage and used it to collect a bribe. Skyler Iyer, whose CyberLife deadline pushed Marcus past caution. Even Vic Kingsley's name surfaced for a moment. One by one, the accusations dissolved. The verdict the room settled on was overdose — left ambiguous, most likely accidental, safely attributable to the dead. No single killer. No lasting blame. By the time the last name was walked back, the group seemed most invested in protecting its own.

## The Story

Start with the drug. Psychotrophin-3A. An experimental compound that induces neural plasticity in large doses. Quinn Sterling buried the safety assessments years ago, ended a business collaboration with Zia Bishara and Cass to keep the research hidden. Then Marcus Blackwood came calling with knowledge of the drug he shouldn't have had. He wanted it, and he had enough on Quinn to get it.

A recovered memory from 9:59 PM captures the shape of Quinn's trap. Marcus is obsessed with PT-3A's effects on neural plasticity. He's blackmailing Quinn to continue developing it. The drug is dangerous. Quinn knows this. Marcus doesn't care. He wants another batch delivered to the bar by ten o'clock.

Dangerous."* Marcus said he could handle it. An envelope changed hands. The stranger left. Marcus popped some pills and sat at the bar. Staring vacantly. Looking scared.

## Follow the Money

Eleven shell accounts. $7.56 million in buried evidence. That's the price of silence this morning. Every dollar flowed in, with one exception: $150,000 walked out of the White account and into Alex Reeves's personal bank. We'll come back to that.

The largest account is called White. $2.45 million across ten separate transactions. Ten memories. That's not a panicked burial. The deposits arrived in three distinct bursts — a cluster at 9:41 this morning, another at 9:58, a final one at 10:01 — the rhythm of coordinated work across multiple hands. I can't tell you who walked up to Blake with which memory. I can tell you the kind of history this much silence would complicate: say, a CEO transition.

Sarah Blackwood worked the morning. I watched her disappear into a dark corner of the warehouse with Blake around 9:40, just ahead of the first White cluster. Later I watched her with Alex Reeves, negotiating what turned out to be a trade. It played out at 10:01: three deposits into White in a single minute, totaling $625,000. One minute later, $150,000 walked back out to an account bearing Alex's full name. By the time NeurAI announced Sarah as interim CEO this morning, the one person with the most credible outside claim on Marcus's company had been bought. Sarah was unavailable for comment. Alex understood the code. Sarah understood the exchange rate.

Then there's Pokerface. $1.175 million across four transactions, including the single largest burial of the entire morning — a $750,000 deposit at 9:57 AM. Four deposits, but not four hands working alone: I watched Morgan Reed and Jamie 'Volt' Woods moving in step throughout this morning's investigation, and Pokerface looks like what happens when two people share an alias and split the bill. Whoever chose the name wanted the scent thrown. Whose memories ended up here is anybody's guess. The name on the account is a lie.

And Jess. $225,000. One memory. Full name on the account. No hiding. Jess Kane was named in the accusation this morning, and the account bearing her name holds a single buried memory. Either brazen confidence or a message: I buried this, and I want you to know it.

Between W at $705,000, Rain at $600,000, and Backbone at $600,000, nearly $2 million disappeared into accounts with names that read like code words. The shape of the silence is legible from the outside. The contents stay buried.

## The Players

Quinn Sterling was the first name in the room and the first to be released. Quinn sought counsel from Ezra Sullivan about how to handle an unreasonable colleague, tried to confess to old collaborators about the research that destroyed their partnership. Ezra's recovered memory captures Quinn asking for advice about safety concerns, only to reveal the colleague in question was Marcus. Ezra's honest assessment: Marcus wouldn't listen to anyone. That's the behavior of someone trapped, not someone plotting. Quinn was coerced into supplying the drug that killed Marcus. The evidence said Quinn was the most trapped person in the room this morning. The accusation didn't survive the first round.

Sarah Blackwood was accused second. She was also, per Marcus's own final memory, one of the victims of the memory-extraction scheme — drugged that night, staring at a wall, crying. The accusation against her rested on motive: a paternity test recovered from Sarah's own files this morning, divorce proceedings Mel Nilsson had opened on the morning of the party, the CEO vacancy she stood to fill. The room could not square the victim with the beneficiary. The accusation was released. Sarah is, as of this morning, interim CEO of NeurAI. She remained unavailable for comment.

Jess Kane was accused third, and no exposed evidence explained why. What we know from the paternity test — the same document recovered this morning — is that the baby Jess is carrying is Marcus's. What we know from Jess's own recovered memory at 11:04 PM is that Marcus used her in the memory-extraction protocol under the cover of an acting-gig cover story; she was one of his test subjects long before the party. Jess had every reason to want Marcus gone and no apparent mechanism for making it happen. The accusation was released. The Jess Kane line simply refused to close.

Morgan Reed had every piece of the puzzle. Bribes, fraud, shell companies, stolen IP. Morgan confronted Marcus on the hammock at 11:22 PM with all of it and offered an ultimatum. Marcus handed Morgan a bribe and walked away. Morgan took it. Morgan's sin isn't what he did — it's what he didn't do with what he knew. I watched Morgan Reed and Jamie 'Volt' Woods working closely together throughout this morning's investigation. Jamie had witnessed the stranger in the lab coat, the pill exchange, the vacant stare. Jamie watched Marcus ingest whatever the stranger handed him and called it rich people and their top shelf habits. Between them, Morgan and Jamie had enough to name the whole supply chain. They named none of it. The accusation against Morgan was released with the others.

## What's Missing

I keep coming back to Sam Thorne. A recovered memory from 12:50 AM shows Sam at the Stanford circle meeting, still deliberating what to do about Marcus. The deliberation reached no conclusion. Marcus's body did. What did they decide, and did they act on it? The room this morning walked back every accusation it raised and never raised one against Sam. Somewhere, a police investigation may still continue. Sam may yet be asked harder questions. But the room had its chance this morning, and the room chose.

The CyberLife prototype was supposed to be programmed with memory recordings. Marcus spent his final hours extracting memories from drugged partygoers. Did he succeed in loading stolen consciousness into that prototype before he died? Does a machine exist somewhere with other people's experiences trapped inside it? The android scheme didn't die with Marcus. It may have outlived him.

[**INLINE PHOTO 8: Riley + Sam + Nat — `aln410 (8 of 9).jpg`**]
**Caption:** *Riley Torres, Sam Thorne, and Nat Francisco during the investigation.*

One question I can't shake: how did Marcus discover PT-3A in the first place? Quinn's memory says he had knowledge of the drug he shouldn't have had. Someone told Marcus about Quinn's buried research. Who? And what role did Jess Kane actually play — Marcus's mistress, his test subject, and the woman who walked into the accusation hearing this morning with $225,000 and one buried memory in an account bearing her own name. Whatever story that memory tells, Jess wanted it known it was hers.

## Closing

I watched twenty people walk through six accusations this morning and release every name. Quinn, because she was trapped. Sarah, because she was also a victim. Jess, because the evidence didn't add up. Morgan, because he took the bribe instead of the call. Skyler, because his work was all about bettering humanity. Vic, for the length of a sentence. And Sam Thorne, who made the drug that killed Marcus, because no one in the room named him at all.

Marcus Blackwood didn't die because twenty people conspired to kill him. He died because he built a system that required increasingly dangerous shortcuts to sustain its own mythology — stolen code, coerced drug supply, memory extraction from drugged partygoers, thirty-million-dollar funding asks built on demos that didn't work. And by morning, when the chance came to say out loud who had held the machine's gears in place, the room protected its own. Even Sam.

Somewhere a police investigation may still continue. Sam may yet be asked harder questions. But the room that had its chance this morning chose to protect its own, and Marcus will get the blame for all of it — the drugs he didn't synthesize, the code he didn't write, the memory protocol he couldn't even finish running. Everyone else will get to keep going. Alex Reeves, who built the technical foundation under NeurAI and walked out of the morning with $150,000 in exchange for his silence — a man who understood the code and did not understand the knife work. Morgan Reed, still in a position to write lobbying letters to Senator Burns about responsible AI innovation. Skyler Iyer, still working towards the betterment of humanity. Vic Kingsley, still making two bets in the same race. Riley Torres, still loyal to a best friend he doesn't fully know. Jamie 'Volt' Woods, still taking notes on napkins and selling them to the highest bidder. Nat Francisco and the Stanford circle still deliberating. Sam still claiming he did his best.

And Sarah Blackwood. Sarah played the morning with a precision that earned her the chair she's about to sit in. I watched her at 9:40 with Blake, and at the trade that moved $150,000 out of White and into Alex Reeves's settlement, and at the announcement that named her interim CEO before the body was cold. Whatever else happens to NeurAI from here, it will happen with Sarah at the table. Not because the system handed her anything — because she made every move a person in her position could make, and she made them well.

Marcus built a machine that ate people. The machine doesn't need Marcus anymore. It has twenty other hands on the gears, and this morning, every hand stayed exactly where it was.

We came looking for a killer. We found a room. The room is still here.
