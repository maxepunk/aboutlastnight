ABOUT LAST NIGHT | THE MARCUS BLACKWOOD FILE

Convicting Vic Kingsley Was Good Business

The room was told someone had tried to frame him. Nine hands went up anyway, in a preliminary the clock froze into a verdict. $7.865 million in memories went dark before lunch, and by the time I filed this story, NeurAI's board was already offering Marcus Blackwood's widow the CEO chair. Dethroning the kingmaker worked out for almost everyone.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews Investigative Desk · February 22, 2027 · Article NNA-071826-26

I was not in the room when the nine hands went up. Everything that reached me from that warehouse describes a verdict that finished before the deciding did: a preliminary count, a dying clock, a facilitator with nothing left to do but call it. Vic Kingsley. Nine votes. The murder of Marcus Blackwood, at Marcus Blackwood's own party.

Here is what else reached me. Blake, the man running the room, had just told everyone there was strong evidence that someone had been trying to frame Vic. He said it counted in Vic's favor. He said it out loud. The room voted Vic anyway. When a room does the exact thing it has just been warned about, you can call that justice, or you can start counting who benefits. I have been counting all evening. The number keeps going up.

THE STORY

Start with what the party was, because everything the room did this morning grew out of it. The invitations said rave. The paperwork says experiment. An email recovered from the scene shows Marcus Blackwood ordering a fresh batch of Psychotrophin-3A from Quinn Sterling four days in advance, with the date attached: "I'm having a party on the 21st." And with the leverage attached: "And you can't afford to say no, can you?" A second recovered email briefs the installation artist, Kai Andersen, on "environmental cues" that needed to "run exactly as written." A memory from the bar puts a clock under all of it: a CyberLife executive named Skyler telling Marcus he had run out of time, and should try programming the prototype with the memory recordings instead. What was the worst that could happen?

So the guests were dosed, and the guests were harvested. The last recording anyone recovered from Marcus Blackwood himself is stamped 1:50 in the morning, and what the dead man's own memory preserves is his irritation that the people he had drugged were not competent enough to help him tidy up the theft.

Now you just need these drugged zombies to LOCK them. [...] SARAH's staring at a wall, tears streaming. This is chaos. You don't think ANYONE has the right combinations anymore. Under the drug story ran a business story, and the record has it timestamped. At 9:18, Alex Reeves is rage-texting Vic Kingsley: Marcus stole the idea and design for NeurAI, cease and desist, meet me at the bar at ten. At 9:55, Vic's own recovered memory shows him steeling himself for that meeting.

ALEX is in, MARCUS is out. No more C & D. No more potential criminal conspiracy charges. You've just got to do this, quiet like and ASAP, so that NeurAI remains a viable investment."

Seven minutes later, a bartender on a smoke break heard the succession close.

Jamie 'Volt' Woods · 11:39 PM

And at 1:08, the thing they were all fighting over stopped existing. Remi Whitman carried a laptop across the party to Vic and Alex and set NeurAI's code beside its own demo.

Put Another Way, It's Worthless
"REMI approaches you and ALEX with a laptop in hand, looking like a smug son of a bitch. At first, what you see looks familiar. The demo loops perfectly. But then ALEX points out what you're not seeing. The code doesn't match. The demo's fake. ALEX snatches the laptop to investigate while you stand there, dumbfounded. [...] MARCUS's 'breakthrough AI' that he's been crowing about for years needs manual fixes for 90% of the inputs. Put another way... it's worthless."
Vic Kingsley · 1:08 AM

Thirty million dollars of Vic's money had bought a costume. By morning Marcus was dead, and eleven people woke up in the warehouse with their memories missing and spent the morning recovering them: reading some into the record, selling some to Blake to keep them dark.

The tips that reached me describe a real investigation, at least at first. Sam Thorne's first act of the morning was finding Sarah to ask if she was okay. The board the room built is an index of everything they put on the record: eight grievances, each one traceable to a recovered memory, three names circled in red. Zia. Alex. Vic.

Then the clock got involved, and the investigation became a verdict. What the accounts of the deliberation describe is not a room agreeing. It is a room running out of time in the middle of a fight.

Vic opened it on the front foot, the aggrieved party rather than the suspect: Marcus had stolen lots of code, everything he built was bogus, and Vic had sunk not only his own money but his friends' money into it. Blake went further than anyone expected. He told the room there was strong evidence someone had been trying to frame Vic, and that he counted it in Vic's support. The frame accusation found Alex within seconds, on the theory that a guilty man buries memories under someone else's name. Sarah Blackwood made the room's first formal accusation, and it was Alex too: with Marcus gone, the top of NeurAI opens up, and the cease and desist on the table demanded exactly that opening. Alex's defense was flat. Why would he murder a man he was already settling with in court? Someone called him a low-key scapegoat, and the room let him go.

Then Alex turned the accusation back on Vic. The person who had actually lost something was Vic: the man who learned at 1:08 that his investment had evaporated. Remi Whitman said he did not want to put a number on the loss, and then put one on it. Twenty million dollars. Blake observed it was far more than any account anyone had earned through their little deal with the NeurAI board of directors. Alex made his own exposure a package deal: Clearly, he asserted, if he were guilty, then Vic must be too. Or, it was Vic acting on his own. Vic's counter was that the man who discovered the code was fake was Alex, and Alex who carried the laptop away. Somebody implied Blake had been having private conversations with Vic. Blake's answer was that he had spoken to just about every single person there. He was facilitating, after all.

Blake, watching the pile-on, said it looked like a fish being beaten with a hammer. Then he named the hammer: Alex.

Sarah's own name went up in the final five seconds, as a jealous wife, a theory the room was hearing for the first time. Four hands went up, but time was running out. Blake called it: Vic Kingsley, nine votes. Then he told the room to take whatever evidence they were carrying with them so the police could not get their hands on it, and everyone ducked into the business next door while the cops came through the warehouse doors.

FOLLOW THE MONEY

While the investigation ran, so did the market. Blake's table paid cash for burials all morning: hand him a memory, name an account, and the public record loses a piece while the account gets an untraceable transfer. By the end, thirty-four memories had gone dark in exchange for a total of $7.865 million.

Nearly six point one million of the total landed in the final eleven minutes before deliberation, a stampede of sellers beating the closing bell. The account named Zia opened early and big, $750,000 at 10:32, and finished the morning on top at $2.1 million. And the entire account bearing Remi Whitman's name, $1.755 million across seven transactions, is stamped inside a single minute, 11:22. One visit. One armful. A tip says he was overheard at Blake's table making it a principle:

"Put these in my personal account. I have nothing to hide."

— Remi Whitman, overheard at Blake's table

I believe him about the account. That makes Remi's the only name on the ledger that anyone verified all morning. A man collected $1.755 million for making seven memories disappear in sixty seconds, described it as having nothing to hide, and the room's takeaway was his transparency. And the twenty million Remi attached to Vic's losses is the exact figure from his own recovered emails, two years of grievance that Vic had committed that same twenty million to Marcus while Remi's healthier company could not get the time of day.

Then there are the last entries of all. At 11:27, three minutes before the room sat down to deliberate, the market's final two transactions landed in an account named Vic Kingsley. Not Vic. Vic Kingsley, full name, as if the ledger wanted to be legible from across the room. The deliberation then opened with Blake telling everyone that somebody must be trying to frame the man given the two separate accounts.

The top of the board, Blake could not explain, and said so twice: Zia. Not in the room, he said, disappeared sometime last night. Zia was at that party. Zia's complaint that Marcus "borrowed" a storage algorithm and never gave it back is preserved in a recovered memory from 9:18 that evening. At 11:10, Zia was giving Ashe Motoko the story as an interview. Near one in the morning, Zia was circulating the room telling anyone who would listen that Ashe was the only reporter in the Valley worth talking to. The board even carries the name in red at the top of the suspect list, over the words "M stole their code." Yet Zia was gone by the time Blake arrived on the scene and if they weren't in the room, they could not have participated in Blake's little private marketplace. So who was the one burying millions of dollars worth of people's stolen memories? Who had the most to gain from NeurAI's interests?

THE PLAYERS

Blake read balances aloud. Nobody read the people.

Morgan Reed was handed a fat envelope of cash by Marcus at 11:29 last night; a recovered memory caught the handoff by the bar, and the envelope itself sat in the morning's evidence under the board's own annotation, "Bribe?" Riley Torres's voicemail, recovered with everything else, maps the machinery she built.

The Money Is Yours
"You call SARAH. You're not sure where she disappeared off to. No answer, so you leave a voice-mail: 'MARCUS worked with MORGAN to set up a slew of hidden investment accounts. We're talking cover organizations, liability shields, blood money, environmental degradation, insider trading... I've mapped the shell vehicles. The paper trail is clear. When you're ready, we blow this wide open. The money is yours. Love you!'"
Riley Torres · 11:43 PM

Morgan's own recovered papers put her in an emergency strategy meeting with Vic at eight o'clock, before the party got loud, about what those papers call "the Marcus problem." When Riley came to her in the morning offering a trade, the answer that reached me was pure crisis manager: "What do you got that I want?" At one point Sam asked the room where Morgan even was. Shell companies, a cash envelope, a Senate lobbying letter in the evidence, and a documented meeting about the victim being a problem. In the final four minutes, none of it came up. Nobody said her name once.

Remi Whitman you have already met: the man who steered the money talk, the only verified name on the ledger, the second-largest account in the building, zero votes.

Alex Reeves was examined, technically. Sarah accused him first, formally. The frame theory hit him inside the first minute. And then he argued his way from defendant to prosecutor so completely that Blake named him the hammer. He left the morning cleared, professionally promoted, and an account bearing his name got $1.28 million heavier in one two-minute visit to Blake's table. I am not saying that money is his. I am saying nobody asked whose it was, in a room that had just spent a full minute on the theory that a guilty man buries memories under someone else's name.

Quinn Sterling spent the morning inside the memories, and at least one broke through: a tip caught her mid-read, saying quietly, "omg this is bad." The record explains the dread. Marcus's recovered email orders another batch of Psychotrophin-3A from her and reminds her she "can't afford to say no." The drug that made the party possible was extorted out of its maker.

Sam Thorne checked on Sarah before she did anything else. Then she sat down with her own diary, recovered along with everything else, and it is the closest thing this case has to testimony from inside Marcus's circle. It says she made his drugs: "it's probably the shit I'm making for him." It says she found the paternity test slipped under the warehouse door and mailed it to Sarah, because her friend had a right to know. And it quotes Marcus himself, four days before he died: worried about what Vic knew, unable to remember their last conversation. The room never read from it aloud.

Taylor Chase and Ashe Motoko spent the morning as each other's stories. Ashe was fired over an exposé on Marcus's memory experiments; the kill order is in the record, in Taylor's own words, with the price attached.

"Done."
"Done. Ashe's expose on your 'memory experiments' won't see daylight. Editor thinks it needs 'more substantiation.' [...] Now about that exclusive the launch...
Taylor Chase
'Reality is negotiable.' -Tim Ferriss"
Taylor Chase to Marcus Blackwood · Jan 31, 2027

By morning the interviews ran the other way: Ashe sat Taylor down about Marcus's dealings, while Taylor worked the binary code on an old rave poster and, in the last minutes, stood at the bar holding a recovered IOU with Marcus Blackwood's signature on it. On the night itself, Ashe had also landed the interview of his career: Ezra Sullivan, forty years off the record, on the record.

Which leaves the two women Marcus apologized to with one script.

Sarah Blackwood's morning is the article nobody wrote. Her texts say she had finally kicked Marcus out and came to the party to confront him. Her divorce lawyer, Mel Nilsson, had already opened the file; the intake email, recovered with the rest, mentions a paternity test that arrived at Sarah's house anonymously. The test is in the evidence too. The mother's name on it is Jess Kane's.

In the morning, Sarah stood at the bar with Jess and Sam and compared apology cards. Marcus had sent his wife and the woman carrying his child the identical card, word for word: "I'm sorry. I love you. It's never going to happen again. She means nothing to me. Don't leave me." The photograph of that discovery shows all three women laughing. What passed between Sarah and Jess last night, the record cannot show; their side of the night never surfaced publicly. What the record does show is both of them getting the same words from the same man, and both of them still standing there in the morning, together.

Jess's own file is worse than the gossip about her. A recovered memory from 11:04 shows her explaining, flatly, that Marcus had rehearsed his extraction process on her before: called it an acting gig, talked her into the drugs, and afterward showed her the memory token he kept. She carried his child, and he beta-tested his product on her. The only deliberation theory that came anywhere near her was aimed at Sarah instead.

Because Sarah's morning has a third act. She threw the room's first formal accusation. Then, with five seconds on the clock, someone floated her as a jealous wife, and four hands went up, and there was no time to do anything about them. And then the sun went down, and an email leaked to me tonight: NeurAI's board intends to offer Sarah Blackwood the chief executive role "as soon as things settle down." From confronting her husband to succeeding him in roughly twenty-four hours. The room never got to weigh that. There was no time. There is now.

Vic Kingsley spent two decades deciding who rises in this Valley. The internship program that trained Marcus, Alex, and Sarah was his. The fund behind both NeurAI and its main rival is his. This morning a room he helped build decided he was done, and he lost in public: the protege, the thirty million, finally the vote, all of it where everyone could see. Everyone else lost quietly or won quietly, and in this economy, quiet is the whole game. The market that ran all morning does not punish taking; it pays for taking, in cash, into any name you like. What gets punished is losing where people can watch you do it.

I do not think that room held eleven villains. I think it held eleven people who each did the locally sensible thing: sell the memory, take the payout, make sure your future is safe. With the police at the door, convicting Vic was simply the efficient call. Vic is likely going to be fine. He has the resources to ensure the allegations don't stick. And a system in which betrayal is the smart play does not need villains. It needs participants, and it found eleven.

By tonight the machine had already moved on. I called Vic Kingsley's office for comment; his assistant told me he is on vacation, recovering from the trauma of last night's events, and I have written that sentence down exactly as it was said to me. The board's email promises Sarah the chair once things settle down, and things are settling the way they always settle here. The memories are bought and buried. The evidence left the building with the people who found it. The story has a designated ending. I do not think the room found Marcus Blackwood's killer this morning. I think it found the version of last night that the most people could live with, at the best available price.
