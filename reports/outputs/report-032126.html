Three Names. $7.6 Million in Silence. One Dead CEO.

Marcus Blackwood built an empire on stolen code and stolen memories. Eighteen people woke up with holes in their heads. Three of them stand accused. But the harder question is whether Marcus did this to himself, and how many people helped him get there.

Cassandra Nova | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-0321-26

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Eighteen people woke up this morning with holes where their memories should be. Three of them are accused of putting him in the ground: Zia Bishara, who hasn't been seen since the party. Jess Kane, who was carrying his child. Alex Reeves, who spent five years watching Marcus profit from stolen code.

Someone extracted those memories without permission. Someone locked them away. And someone made sure Marcus wouldn't be explaining why.

But the group couldn't agree on who. Ashe pointed at Alex. Sarah pointed at Jess. Ezra pointed at Zia. Three accusers, three suspects, zero consensus. And underneath it all, a question nobody wanted to sit with for too long: whether Marcus's own recklessness, the drugs, the crumbling empire built on stolen code, killed him before anyone else had the chance.

I was in that room this morning. I watched them try to piece together a night that had been literally ripped from their minds. This is what I found.

The Story

Start with the theft. Marcus Blackwood didn't build NeurAI. He assembled it from pieces he stole from everyone around him.

The proof came in two columns of code.

The Sloppy Thief
ZIA.4 - 12:31AM - REMI hands you the laptop. You punch in the default password for the Oracle Ledger and it works. Next, you pull up a code comparison. Left: your Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. Right: NeurAI's 'proprietary' system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. MARCUS didn't even bother changing your notes. Sloppy thief. Too bad the idiot didn't catch the massive bug that you've since fixed.
Zia Bishara

Remi handed Zia the laptop. The code comparison took seconds. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. Marcus didn't even bother changing Zia's notes when he copied the Oracle Ledger algorithm. There was a massive bug in the version Marcus stole. A bug Zia had since fixed in the original. Marcus's empire was running on broken code.

The intellectual property theft wasn't isolated. Cass discovered the same pattern with the Synesthesia Engine. A recovered memory from 12:38AM shows the code comparison: left side, the engine Cass sent Marcus for a demo. Right side, the code Marcus insisted Kai Andersen incorporate into the party installation. Identical. Marcus was using stolen code to run experiments on unsuspecting guests without signing the contract or paying a cent.

The Synesthesia Engine Theft
CASS.4 - 12:38AM - Code comparison. Left: the Synesthesia Engine code you sent MARCUS for your demo. Right: The last minute code MARCUS insisted KAI incorporate into their installation for tonight's party. Identical. MARCUS is secretly using the Synesthesia Engine before signing the contract and paying you for it. What's more, he wants KAI to run it tonight on an unsuspecting party audience. Why would he want to do that? What's his agenda here?
Cass

I overheard Cass this morning: "I only know about the science. Not Instagram. I don't like it when people steal my stuff." That's someone who just found out their life's work was deployed without their knowledge on a room full of people who are now missing their memories.

Skyler's recovered memory from 11:19PM captures the other side of the Synesthesia Engine pitch.

The Pitch That Failed
SKYLER.3 - 11:19PM - CASS starts their pitch: "The 'Synesthesia Engine' provides a creative and exploratory space for users to unlock novel levels of..." and your attention starts to drift. You notice MARCUS and MORGAN across the room, looking angry. You snap back to attention when CASS stops talking and stares at you. You stare back, not sure what else to do. CASS breaks the staring contest by exclaiming "Fuck" and storming off. You watch them leave.
Skyler

Cass is mid-presentation, explaining the engine's potential, when Skyler's attention drifts across the room to Marcus and Morgan in a tense standoff. Cass stops talking. Stares. Skyler stares back. Cass breaks the silence with a single word and storms off. The pitch that could have changed Cass's career, lost to Marcus's chaos.

Follow the thread backward. Before the code theft came the money. Riley Torres spent years building a case against Marcus. Shell companies, insider trading, environmental destruction. A recovered memory from 10:12PM shows Riley confronting Morgan Reed with the evidence. Morgan didn't even try to deny it.

Years of Research
RILEY.2 - 10:12PM - These documents represent years of research. This could have been a career-making case for you. But instead of taking your evidence to court, you're using it to bait MORGAN into giving you the info you really need. You confront them about the shell companies and insider trading they've been profiting off of with MARCUS. They don't even try to deny it. You promise to keep their name out of it if they'll help you track the money down for SARAH. MARCUS has no idea what's about to go public.
Riley Torres

Riley promised to keep Morgan's name out of it. In exchange, Morgan would help track the money down for Sarah. Marcus had no idea what was about to go public.

Except it didn't go public. Not the way Riley planned.

This morning, I watched Morgan make a beeline for Blake clutching extracted memories. Riley chased after them. "Those are mine!" The partnership that was supposed to take Marcus down had fractured in the final minutes. Someone nearby said something about possession and the law. Morgan was trying to sell Riley's evidence to the Black Market.

But by then, Riley had already gone on record. The evidence was exposed. Morgan's betrayal came too late.

Morgan's betrayal of Marcus at 12:40AM was caught on the record. Taylor Chase turned on the recorder. Morgan began to spill. Shell companies. Insider trading. Dangerous experiments on unwitting test subjects. Lying under oath.

The Betrayal
MORGAN.4 - 12:40AM - You would have told MARCUS to his face that you weren't going to play ball anymore, if he'd given you the chance. Instead, he bluffed. And you're calling. TAYLOR turns on their recorder and you begin to spill the worst things you have on MARCUS. It's a long list. Time to start with his sterling reputation…that ridiculous world leader request. MARCUS: "Merkel visited Zuckerberg. Singapore's PM flew to Cupertino for Cook. I'm building something bigger. Get me a real meeting. Someone with actual power."
Morgan Reed

Morgan had helped Marcus set up those shell companies. Had known about the insider trading for years. But Marcus bluffed, and Morgan called. The fixer became the chaos.

The Stanford Four were supposed to be friends. Nat Francisco's camera caught them once, years ago. Mel Nilsson doing a backflip off the speakers, glow paint everywhere. Sam Thorne laughing, saying they'd never sell out and work for 'the man.' Marcus grabbing Nat's shoulder: 'We're going to change the world, Nat. All four of us.'

The Footage Nat Can't Watch
NAT.1 - 8:28PM - Your camera catches MEL doing a backflip off the speakers, glow paint everywhere. SAM is laughing, saying they'll never sell out and work for 'the man.' MARCUS grabs your shoulder: 'We're going to change the world, Nat. All four of us.' You believe him. You all believe it. The footage is still on a hard drive somewhere. You can't watch it anymore. That Marcus doesn't exist.
Nat Francisco

The footage is still on a hard drive somewhere. Nat can't watch it anymore. That Marcus doesn't exist.

By 12:50AM that night, Nat, Sam, and Mel had gathered to decide what to do. They'd been trying to convince each other to stop enabling Marcus for years. But now they were learning that the AI underpinning his company didn't work. Instead of fixing it, Marcus was taking drugs and inventing techniques to steal people's memories.

The Stanford Four Intervention
NAT.4 - 12:50AM - You, NAT and SAM gather together to talk about what to do with MARCUS. You've tried to convince those two to stop enabling him for years. But at this point, he might be too far gone. Now you're learning that he's lied about the AI that underpins his company. It doesn't work. But instead of fixing it, he's taking drugs and inventing techniques to steal people's memories instead? Maybe his high pressure deal with CyberLife pushed him over the edge and into this weird, out-of-the-box thinking….
Nat Francisco

The intervention came too late. The friends who once believed they'd change the world together watched one of them steal it instead.

And this is where the self-harm theory gains weight. Marcus was consuming Psychotrophin-3A, the experimental memory drug that Quinn Sterling was being blackmailed into manufacturing for him. Sam made the party blends that kept Marcus high enough to confess his crimes to anyone who'd listen. Jess was his first memory extraction subject. She doesn't remember the experiment. Her memory was wiped in the process.

The First Subject
JESS.3 - 11:04PM - You approach your friend NAT and tell them you know why MARCUS is interested in Psychotrophin-3A. He uses it as part of his "Memory Extraction Protocol." Said it was an experimental NeurAI research he wasn't ready to go public with. You know about it because he tried it on you once. Said it was an acting gig - that you should play a butler. You wanted to help him, so you took the drugs and let him run his experiment. He was ELATED. Showed you a token. Said your memory was stored on it somehow.
Jess Kane

Marcus told Jess it was an acting gig. Play a butler, he said. She took the drugs and let him run his experiment. He was elated. Showed her a token. Said her memory was stored on it somehow. What did Marcus take from her before she ever knew she was pregnant?

The extractions worked. Marcus's own memory confirms it.

Drugged People Make Terrible Assistants
MARCUS.4 - 1:52AM - The extraction protocol is working. You've confirmed it with your own eyes. The tokens store real memories — not approximations, not summaries, but actual experiential data. The problem is dosage. Psychotrophin-3A makes subjects compliant, but it also makes them useless. Drugged people make terrible assistants. You need a way to scale this without anyone noticing. KAI's installation is the answer. Put the drug in the drinks. Run the Synesthesia Engine. Extract while they're distracted. Nobody will remember a thing.
Marcus Blackwood

Marcus built a machine for stealing consciousness. He tested it on Jess. He deployed it on his party guests using stolen code from Zia and Cass, drugs from Quinn that Sam helped manufacture, and an installation from Kai that he'd hijacked for his own purposes.

If the drugs killed him, Sam and Quinn could face questions about what they supplied. If the extraction protocol malfunctioned, everyone who contributed a piece of the machine bears some weight. The group chose to accuse Zia, Jess, and Alex. But the self-harm theory doesn't exonerate anyone. It implicates everyone who enabled him.

And then there's the bathroom at 1:36AM. A recovered memory shows Sarah finding Jess, crying. The wife finding the woman carrying her husband's child.

The Bathroom Confession
SARAH.4 - 1:36AM - You find JESS in the bathroom, crying, and suddenly put it together. You squeak out between unexpected tears: "MARCUS's baby is yours?" She's sobbing so hard now that she can't even speak, so she nods. You thought you'd be outraged, but seeing her here in front of you, you just feel resigned. You say: "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."
Sarah Blackwood

Sarah thought she'd be outraged. Instead, she felt resigned. "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."

Sarah, Jess, and Mel found each other in the drug lab this morning. They discovered the betrayals. They discovered they'd all slept with Marcus. And somehow, in the wreckage of his manipulations, they chose solidarity over blame. The sisterhood survives. Marcus doesn't.

Alex's case against Marcus goes back five years. The texts to Vic Kingsley at 9:18PM: "M stole the idea & design for NeurAI from me. Just look at the code base architecture! Everyone knows Marcus can't code for shit."

The Cease and Desist
ALEX.1 - 9:18PM - ALEX texts VIC: Look, you don't want a lawsuit coming after NeurAI. This is the last chance to make things right / Cease & Desist. Don't want lawyers involved / M stole the idea & design for NeurAI from me. just look at the code base architecture! Everyone knows MARCUS can't code for shit / Still, he profits, while I'm rage-texting you like a crazy person / I just want some credit and MARCUS gone from from NeurAI / VIC texts : Okay, let's talk. Meet me at the bar at 10:00?
Alex Reeves

Vic's response: "Okay, let's talk. Meet me at the bar at 10:00?"

NeurAI is now considering Alex as their new CEO. The person Marcus stole from might inherit the empire built on that theft.

Follow the Money

The Black Market ledger tells its own story. $7.6 million in buried memories. Eleven shell accounts. And every dollar traces back to someone in that room.

Cobrakai leads the pack. $2.08 million across nine transactions, starting at 9:05AM and running straight through to shutdown.

Elonisshit collected $1.53 million across five transactions. A name that broadcasts contempt for what it's hiding.

Then there's Motherofmen. $1.3 million. Five transactions. The account appeared on the roster and immediately started earning. I watched Morgan make a beeline for Blake clutching memories just before shutdown. I watched Morgan introduce themselves to Sam using someone else's name. A pattern of false identities. A pattern of rapid movement toward Blake. I can't prove who controls Motherofmen. But I watch people, and some patterns are hard to ignore.

Laverne moved first. Seven transactions starting at 8:22AM, totaling just over a million. First to sell, and kept selling throughout the investigation.

$125,000 went to an account bearing Remi's name. Whether Remi set it up or someone else directed funds there, that's a real identity attached to real money. $30,000 went to an account called "Jamie volt." Same question. An account bearing a real name doesn't tell you who walked up to Blake with the token. It tells you where Blake sent the payment.

Zia vanished. But someone stayed to sell. $7.6 million in buried memories, and Zia's name appears nowhere in the ledger. If the accused are guilty, where's their payday?

The Players

Nat, Ashe, and Taylor went on record publicly. I watched them work throughout the investigation, piecing together evidence that could have earned them silence money instead. Nat and Ashe worked together frequently, unlocking memories that weren't theirs anymore. Taylor was collaborative, working through locked stashes with multiple investigators.

Some partnerships surprised everyone. Flip and Taylor, working side by side to crack open a locked stash. Jamie and Quinn, the bartender and the chemist, finding common ground nobody expected. The investigation made allies of strangers and strangers of allies.

Riley built the case. Years of research into shell companies and insider trading. Career-making material, handed over to Mel to protect Sarah. When Morgan tried to sell that evidence to Blake, Riley chased them across the venue. Built the case, watched it nearly disappear, fought to keep it on the record.

Morgan flipped on Marcus, then tried to profit from the chaos. This morning, Morgan sought out Sam and introduced themselves as "Phil Kowalski" to obtain drugs. Phil Kowalski. That's Flip's real name. His closest-held secret, the construction worker identity he keeps hidden behind the "party architect" persona because of deep class anxiety. Morgan didn't just use a fake name. Morgan stole the identity of someone who trusted Marcus enough to share it. The same pattern Morgan showed all morning: take what's useful, discard what's inconvenient, move on before anyone notices.

Vic Kingsley spotted the secret entrance to Marcus's hidden bachelor pad and workshop. That discovery opened a door the investigation hadn't known existed. Behind another closed door, Ashe, Skyler, and Vic met privately. What three investigators needed to discuss away from the group is its own question.

Jamie saw much more than they indicated throughout the party and investigation. Some people watch. Some people talk. Some people do both and charge for the privilege.

Quinn confessed to Ezra at midnight. Three times Quinn had to explain the situation before Ezra was willing to take off their rose-colored glasses. This morning, Quinn and Cass had a conversation I overheard: "Wait, why didn't you tell me you were making the drugs?" The people Marcus exploited were only now discovering how their work connected.

Ezra wanted to throw Zia under the bus since they weren't around to defend themselves. I watched that play out this morning. The absent suspect is always the convenient one.

Sarah's voice, from the bathroom at 1:36AM: "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do." Sarah, Jess, and Mel found each other in the wreckage. The sisterhood survives.

What's Missing

Zia hasn't been found since the party. Fled the country, or removed as part of the operation that killed Marcus? The accusation assumes guilt. The absence suggests something worse. Or something simpler: Zia was wronged by Marcus, knew it, and ran before anyone could frame them for what happened next.

The self-harm theory was weighed and set aside. Drug use. Business failure. A man whose stolen code was breaking, whose drug consumption was escalating, whose company was built on nothing. The group decided Zia should be investigated further. But if Marcus's death was self-inflicted, the questions don't go away. They multiply. Sam supplied the drugs. Quinn manufactured the compound under duress. The Stanford Four watched for years and didn't intervene. If this was an overdose, police will want to know who put the pills in his hand.

Cobrakai. Nine transactions. $2.08 million. The biggest account and the most aggressive timeline. I can tell you the shape of that silence. Someone who saw a lot, and who went unseen. But I can't tell you what's inside it.

What happened between Morgan and Riley in those final minutes before Blake's network shut down? Morgan was clutching memories. Riley was chasing. An unidentified investigator said something about possession and the law. The partnership ended in pursuit.

What did Marcus need the Synesthesia Engine for, beyond what we've seen? Vic's discovery of the secret entrance led to Marcus's hidden bachelor pad and workshop — a space full of extracted memories locked in boxes and sealed behind doors. How many more are in there? What were Ashe, Skyler, and Vic discussing behind closed doors after Vic found the way in?

And where is Zia now?

Three names on an accusation. Three accusers who couldn't agree on which one. And underneath it all, a possibility the group explored and then backed away from: that Marcus Blackwood, high on his own supply, running a company built on stolen code that was already breaking, experimenting on himself with drugs he'd blackmailed a chemist into making, might have done this to himself.

Zia was the convenient answer. Not here to defend themselves. Easy to blame. But if the self-harm theory holds, then the question isn't who pulled the trigger. It's who loaded the gun. Sam made the drugs. Quinn was coerced into refining them. The Stanford Four watched Marcus spiral for twenty years and chose loyalty over intervention. Vic kept funding a company built on fraud. Morgan kept spinning the narrative until it was more profitable to stop. Everyone in that room had a hand in building the machine that consumed Marcus Blackwood.

But here's what keeps me up tonight. Every person in that room made small, reasonable choices. Sam looked the other way when Marcus took too much. Quinn kept manufacturing under duress rather than face exposure. Mel knew about the financial fraud and chose to build a divorce case instead of blowing the whistle. Morgan translated Marcus's chaos into talking points for Washington. Each act, in isolation, was survivable. Understandable, even. But stacked together, they built a system that extracted memories from eighteen people without consent and left one man dead.

That's the real danger. Not one villain. A room full of people who each decided it wasn't their problem.

Marcus Blackwood built a machine that steals human memory. He tested it on a woman who thought she was helping her lover. He deployed it on a party full of people who trusted him. And he did it all because every person who could have stopped him chose not to. If that machine survives him, and someone scales it beyond one warehouse party, it won't be because of a single bad actor. It will be because a thousand people made the same small choice: to look the other way.

Sarah, Jess, and Mel found each other in a drug lab and chose solidarity over blame. Riley built a case for years and fought to keep it on the record when Morgan tried to sell it. Flip discovered that Morgan had been wearing his identity like a costume. Eighteen people sat in a circle this morning, pointed in three different directions, and chose to look away from the mirror.

I was in that room. I watched them decide. And I'm telling you: the three names on that accusation are the least interesting part of this story. The interesting part is what everyone else chose not to see.
