The Party That Stole Your Mind

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Five people woke up with holes where their memories should be. And somewhere in that warehouse, someone had already started buying silence.

Cassandra Nova · February 22, 2027 · Article NNA-123025

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Five people woke up that morning with holes where their memories should be. The rest of the partygoers? Gone. Marcus's body? Missing. And somewhere in that warehouse, someone had already started buying silence.

My sources shared a video of the moment when the first memory played back on that screen. I watched Sarah Blackwood see an account of her own past like a letter from a stranger's life. Whatever happened the night before, she didn't remember living through it. None of them did. That's what psychotrophin3b does. Derek Thorne's little chemistry experiment. The drug that turns your experiences into property someone else can own.

This is the story of a corporate coup disguised as a party. A husband who built a machine to steal memories. A chemist who wanted his formulation back. And a room full of people who had to decide whether the truth was worth more than the money being offered to bury it.

The Story

Sarah Blackwood spent most of the investigation looking for a key. Her purse was locked. Her memories were gone. Her husband was dead. She started with the practical problem because the others were too big to face.

What she found instead was a duplicate apology letter. Marcus had written to Jessicah Kane the same words he'd written to Sarah. Same handwriting. Same regrets. Different woman.

My source reported back about watching Sarah's face when Jessicah's memory played back. A memory of the both of them crying in a bathroom at 3:36 AM. Jessicah asking about the baby. Sarah responding with words she doesn't remember saying anymore.

Both His Victims
JEK002 - 3:36AM - Bathroom. Both crying. SARAH knows. You know she knows. She says: 'The baby's his?' You nod. Expected rage. Get: 'You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do.' You're not enemies. You're both his victims.
Jessicah Kane

That baby deserves better. We both do. Sarah said that. Then someone extracted the memory, and now she has to watch herself being gracious on a screen without actually feeling the grace. That's the violation no one talks about. It's not just losing what happened. It's losing who you were when it happened.

But here's the thing I kept coming back to: Derek Thorne had been pressuring Sarah for months. Convince Marcus to keep throwing parties, he'd said. Marcus wants me at NeurAI. The parties are how we get there. Sarah thought she was being used as leverage. She was right. She just didn't know what kind.

Victoria Kingsley's fund was bleeding. Skyler Iyer's message made that clear: $163 million in withdrawal threats if the Alex Reeves situation wasn't resolved. Marcus had stolen Alex's algorithm to build NeurAI. Alex knew it. Victoria knew it. And someone was threatening to pull everything if they didn't pick a side.

Records of texts between Skyler Iyer and Victoria Kingsley

Victoria helped with locks and puzzles during the investigation. Quiet. Efficient. Almost invisible. I kept losing track of her in the room. When you're holding $163 million in leverage, you don't need to speak. You just need to watch.

Alex Reeves worked the case harder than anyone except Ashe Motoko. Everyone watched them cross-reference memories, build timelines, connect dots. Alex, the engineer whose life's work got stolen. Who'd sent a cease and desist letter to NeurAI demanding credit and termination of the theft. Who now was being offered something interesting.

Then, Black Market Holdings approached Alex during the investigation. The company that now owned NeurAI as of this morning when Marcus died. The offer was simple: Marcus's old job in exchange for burying certain evidence. The IP theft victim invited to become the accomplice.

I don't know if Alex took the deal. The SAM account received two transactions that night. $250,000 and $225,000. The timing lines up suspiciously with Alex's interactions at the Valet station. But Alex also kept working the case. Kept exposing evidence. Which version of Alex showed up that night? Maybe both.

Then James Whitman ran to the Valet.

My source was across the room when it happened. James had been gathering memory evidence, pooling it with the group. Years of professional rejection by Victoria. A $3 million allocation he'd questioned in writing.

And suddenly he was at Blake's station with an armful of extracted memories, shouting.I WANT TO BURY ALL OF THESE.

Moments later, the memory network went dark. Cacophony, then silence. With the police on their way, whatever James was trying to hide was valuable. Valuable enough that someone decided that despite the risk, it was worth keeping the memory network live long enough to process every last transaction.

And that's when all the threads started pulling together. Derek Thorne's panic texts to Marcus. Victoria's fund crisis creating the vacuum. Alex's stolen algorithm. The parties that weren't parties. The drugs that weren't recreational.

Derek had sent those texts days before the party. Victoria and James could go to jail. We need to talk. Urgently. At your party. He wanted his formulation back. Marcus had been profiting from psychotrophin3b without giving Derek his cut. And someone had offered Derek a path forward: remove Blackwood, take his position.

Marcus's own memory tells the rest. 3:52 AM, trying to get drugged partygoers to lock memory containers with combinations only he knew. Derek wandering off mid-command. Sarah staring at a wall, tears streaming. Chaos everywhere. And THEY would be back if he didn't call soon.

They. The people Marcus was working for. The ones who wanted the memories destroyed, not returned. The ones who built the business model that turns human experience into currency.

The group made their accusation. Marcus and Derek, distributing unstable drugs at a party and orchestrating a plot to remove Blackwood and install Derek in his place. Derek wanted his chemistry back. Marcus wanted a unicorn valuation. They both got chaos. And now Marcus is dead, his body missing, and five people are trying to remember a night that was stolen from them.

Follow the Money

Three shell accounts. Over $5.7 million buried. And every dollar traces back to someone in that warehouse.

Anonymous 2 is the monster. $4.12 million across twenty-four transactions in rapid succession. I got the account of James Whitman running to the Valet with that armful of memories. I can almost hear him shout. The timing aligns too cleanly to be coincidence. Whatever was in those extracted memories, someone decided they were worth more buried than in the hands of the authorities.

SAM received $475,000 across two transactions. The timing correlates suspiciously with Alex Reeves's interactions at the Black Market station. Two messages came through the memory network that night: first offering a $50,000 bonus for buried memories, then an increase to $150,000. The second message hit right before SAM's first transaction. The bribery offer made tangible.

Then there's Anon. $1.175 million across three transactions earlier in the evening. Whoever started this wanted anonymity from the beginning. The first burial hit Anon at 8:58 PM, before the chaos peaked. Someone knew what was coming and started buying silence early.

Victoria Kingsley's silence seems deafening in this context. The ghost who helped with locks but never APPEARED to interact with the Valet. When you're holding $163 million in leverage, maybe you learn the true meaning of discretion. You lean to wait for everyone else to make their moves while quietly taking care of your own business.

The Players

I want to talk about the people who brought evidence to light instead of burying it. They had every reason to take the money. To protect themselves. To let the system win.

Ashe Motoko took meticulous notes all night. Group scribe. Systematic analyst. Consummate journalist. While memories disappeared into shell accounts around her, Ashe was building the timeline that let us see the pattern. The journalist who documented everything while the evidence vanished.

Alex Reeves appeared to be working the case constantly, partnered with Ashe on the heavy analytical lifting. The IP theft victim who kept exposing evidence even while Black Market Holdings was offering him the dead man's job. Whatever Alex decided about that offer, the evidence he brought forward is the reason we know about the algorithm theft at all. But perhaps even that was a part of his calculations.

Sarah Blackwood found more than she was looking for. She started searching for a key to her purse. She found the duplicate apology letter, the proof of the affair, the truth about the man she married. Not the kind of discovery anyone prepares for.

"The baby's his? You nod. Expected rage. Get: 'You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do.' You're not enemies. You're both his victims."

— From Jessicah Kane's extracted memory

And then there's James Whitman. I received an audio recording of him rushing to the Valet with most of the group's memory evidence. I heard the commotion from the other 4, the desperation in his voice. Years of professional rejection by Victoria. A $3 million allocation to Marcus's company that he'd questioned in writing. He shouted about burying everything, the memory network went dark moments later.

I'm not going to pretend I don't understand why. You wake up with gaps in your memory, someone offers you cash to keep those gaps private. That's not greed. That's survival. The system made the choice hard. Don't blame people for making it.

Victoria Kingsley remained a ghost throughout. Helpful with puzzles. Quiet otherwise. Her $163 million fund crisis explains the silence. When you're calculating leverage that large, every word is a liability.

What's Missing

Derek Thorne's gym bag contained psychotrophin3b. The drug that makes memory extraction possible. But was it him who distributed it at the party? Marcus's chaos memory shows drugged guests wandering, unable to follow simple instructions. It doesn't show who drugged them.

James buried most of the group's extracted memories in that final rush. What was in them? He shouted about burying all of these. But whose memories? What did they show? The Anonymous 2 account holds $4.12 million in silence. That's a lot of truth that someone wanted gone.

Alex was offered Marcus's job in exchange for burying evidence. The SAM account timing suggests maybe he took the deal. His investigation work suggests maybe he didn't. Both things can be true. People are complicated. Furthermore, James turned out to be of much greater service to NeurAI's post-Marcus goals. So, who knows if the offer is even still on the table.

And the questions that haunt me most: Where are the missing partygoers? Five people woke up. The rest vanished. Where is Marcus's body? The investigation happened in the warehouse where he died. No body. Who shut down the memory network? The cacophony, then silence. Someone had the power to kill the whole system. They used it right after James's outburst.

The buried evidence creates the shape of a conspiracy we can see but not prove. Every shell account is a locked door. Every transaction is someone deciding silence was worth more than truth.

Marcus is dead. Maybe Derek did it. Maybe someone we haven't found yet. But the 5 who woke up with gaps in their memories are a united front, and that's the story that goes on the record: Marcus and Derek ran a drug distribution scheme while one of them was orchestrating a corporate coup. Derek wanted his formulation back. Marcus wanted a unicorn. They both got chaos.

But here's what I keep thinking about: five people woke up with their memories stolen. Not metaphorically. Actually extracted. And the first thing that happened? Someone started buying those memories. Paying for silence. Turning human experience into a transaction.

That's not a murder mystery. That's a business model. And Marcus Blackwood didn't invent it. First they wanted your clicks. Then your conversations. Now they want what it felt like to be you. To know your deepest secrets in a way that only you can understand them.

Five people woke up with holes where their memories should be. The rest of the victims: missing. And somewhere in the wreckage of that party, someone is counting their money.
