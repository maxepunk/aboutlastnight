Nobody Killed Marcus Blackwood

Twenty of his victims, every one with a motive, voted that he faked his own death — then blamed the outsider who ran his memory market while selling the evidence for $6 million. The story isn't the verdict. It's who walked out richer.

Casper Nova | Senior Investigative Reporter · February 22, 2027 · Article NNA-053026-26

Twenty people spent four hours building the case against Marcus Blackwood. Code he stole. People he drugged. Memories he pulled out of their heads. The proof was overwhelming, and the tally on the whiteboard was strange: eleven votes for the theory that Marcus was not dead at all, under a line in red marker reading WHERE'S THE BODY? Nine more votes went to Blake, the one person there with no grudge against Marcus at all.

While that argument ran, the room quietly turned each other's stolen memories into $6.07 million of cash. The outsider took the blame. The architect walked, maybe literally. I was in that room. But the vote isn't the story. The story is who walked out richer — and I'm still counting.

THE STORY

I start at the whiteboard, because that is where the room ended. WHERE'S THE BODY? Robotic Prototype? The 11 beside MARCUS FAKED HIS DEATH was inked over twice, the way you press a pen when you want a thing to be true. The theory: Marcus loaded his own memories into a CyberLife android and walked out a side door as data, leaving no corpse to convict anyone over. It sounds insane until you read what surfaced on the public record that night. The theory had a blueprint, and the blueprint was a conversation Marcus tried to talk his way out of.

The Prototype Conversation
You missed our deadline. ... Tonight we were supposed to do a test run. But you say your AI's still buggy. So let's try programming my prototype with your memory recordings instead. What's the worst that could happen? Marcus: Skyler, it's untested technology. What if something goes wrong...
Jamie 'Volt' Woods

That is Skyler Iyer, at midnight, pushing Marcus toward the exact move the room voted had happened: load the memories into the robot. Marcus, pale, said it was untested. And he already knew how to pull a memory out of a person, because he spent the night doing it to the people around him.

The Extraction
The extractions worked. The instructions were clear: Your memories are now in the tokens. Secure them. Combinations only you know. Protect them however you can. Simple. Now you just need these drugged zombies to lock them. Sam—take this token. Lock the— They wander off mid-command. Sarah's staring at a wall, tears streaming. This is chaos. You don't think anyone has the right combinations anymore. Drugged people make terrible assistants.
Marcus Blackwood

Marcus drugged the party and harvested their memories. He had also been self-experimenting with Quinn's Psychotrophin, the same line of chemistry, scared and unsteady as he dosed himself. That fact cut two ways, and the room took both. Maybe Marcus had simply overdosed — the chemistry was lethal, Quinn had warned him, and an accident meant no one in the warehouse had to be guilty. Or maybe a man who knew how to pull a memory out of a person, with Skyler's prototype on offer, had used that same expertise to fake a death and leave as a file. Quinn floated the second read, and the room latched on. Vic Kingsley had already decided to replace Marcus with Alex Reeves, so Marcus had reason to vanish before he was pushed out. The theories came from motive, and motive was everywhere.

Marcus stole from three people three different ways, and all three were standing there. Zia Bishara's Oracle Ledger algorithm went into NeurAI unchanged. Alex Reeves did the real work on the BizAI algorithm Marcus sold and took the credit for, then got cut out. Cass Zhang's Synesthesia Engine was slipped into Kai Andersen's party installation, set to run on an unsuspecting crowd. The most damning page came from Remi Whitman, who put two columns of code side by side for Vic and Alex to read.

Three thefts, three people with every reason to want Marcus gone. The room voted on Cass and Zia first, three votes each.

Then Quinn put the faked-death theory on the table — and it rebounded onto him. He was the chemist who made the Psychotrophin Marcus had been dosing himself with, the one man who knew the lethal dose; the room floated that he could have killed Marcus and then taken a sub-lethal hit himself to pass as another victim. Three votes landed on Quinn. Then the room, which had every reason to name a peer, reached for the exit ramp instead. Eleven votes for faked death. The motive was clear to anyone standing there. Whether anyone had acted on it was the one question the room chose not to answer.

FOLLOW THE MONEY

While the whiteboard filled with murder theories, a second ledger ran a quieter story. The memories Marcus had pulled out of his guests were worth money, and over the course of the morning the room turned them into $6.07 million. It did not pool. It spread thin across 13 accounts — breadth, not a single whale — the four biggest being an account called Aaron at $1.055 million, then Sasha at $925,000, Simpson at $900,000, and Advent at $650,000. An account called Nunya made the first sale of the morning and took an extra $50,000 for it.

Almost no one put a face to the cash. The exception was an account named Jamie: Jamie 'Volt' Woods made no secret that it was his, and told Blake to wire its proceeds straight to his personal bank. One other thread was worth following — Advent took in just over a million dollars, then moved $400,000 of it to Sasha, which had already collected a $150,000 bonus with a note simply stating 'negotiated'. You're going to want to remember that note.

That is the story the money tells. A room full of people who had every reason to want Marcus exposed spent the morning doing the opposite — feeding the evidence of his crimes, and their own, into a market built to make it disappear. The verdict upstairs said no one in the room was guilty. The ledger said everyone had something to sell.

THE PLAYERS

In a room that size, what you notice is not who did the most, but who chose to do anything at all. Nearly everyone had a reason to stay quiet; nearly everyone spoke anyway.

Sarah Blackwood came to confront a man who would not answer his calls. Marcus, Sarah's spouse, had drained their shared account after Sarah asked for a divorce, talked her out of a career years earlier, and fathered a child with Jess Kane — a paternity test, mailed anonymously to Sarah, put that beyond doubt. Across the room, those two damaged people collided. Sarah's challenge was not a verdict on either of them. Both were genuinely wronged. The moment asked a harder question: is victimhood measured in suffering, or in dollars?

"Nah dude, you're not a victim! Do you have the money?!"

Sarah Blackwood, to Jess Kane

Jess's answer was that she had none. It was a question the room never stopped asking — who was a victim, and who had quietly come out ahead. The ledger wouldn't answer that. What people did once the doors opened might.

Quinn Sterling walked out with three votes against him, and the names stung: Ezra Sullivan, Zia Bishara, and Cass Zhang, the people he had confided in and thought were his allies. His shout to the room read like a man trying to break a frame that had already closed.

Others made their own noise. Sam Thorne got Marcus high enough to confess the stolen BizAI work, then mailed Sarah the paternity test himself, enabler and whistleblower in the same body. Morgan Reed cornered Marcus with proof of his insider trading and walked away with a bribe instead. Earlier I watched Morgan steer Vic Kingsley aside — "maybe we should go somewhere quiet and have a chat" — before the two dropped out of earshot over "negotiations between October and February." Riley Torres mapped the entire shell-company network into a voicemail for Sarah. Mel Nilsson gathered the old Stanford crowd around the hidden accounts, and Nat Francisco — a documentary producer who had been handed the unpublished expose that got Ashe fired — helped them assemble the worst of it: proof that Marcus's AI had never worked at all.

The rest moved at the edges. Taylor Chase had once gotten Ashe Motoko's expose on Marcus's memory experiments spiked for an exclusive — an email recovered from the party lays it out — and this morning I overheard her tell Vic she "had a special relationship with Marcus," the man whose story she had buried. Kai Andersen had folded Cass's stolen code into her own installation without knowing whose it was, the unwitting instrument of the worst theft in the building. Vic Kingsley came in with the verdict already written, and the memory shows him ending the night on the sidewalk, his designer scarf pressed to Marcus's bleeding nose; Alex Reeves came in with a cease-and-desist already filed. Skyler Iyer had built the prototype that may have carried Marcus out. Flip turned up under a name that was not his — Phil Kowalski, a contractor — and, per his bookie's texts, a $30,000 gambling debt due that night.

And then there was the one act of grace in a mercenary night. Ezra Sullivan walked up to Ashe, the journalist Marcus had silenced, held out a memory that was hers, and gave it back.

"I think this memory belongs to you."

Ezra Sullivan, to Ashe Motoko

WHAT'S MISSING

Not everything in that warehouse made it onto a record. The whole point of Blake's market was to make memories disappear, and the ones sold into it are gone from anything the public will ever see.

Two of the people Marcus damaged most — his estranged spouse Sarah Blackwood, and Jess Kane, who carried his child — are nearly absent from the public record. Almost nothing they knew ever surfaced on the board the room could read. Whatever was in the memories they never put on it — the ones too private or too dangerous or too painful — stayed with them.

And the whiteboard still reads WHERE'S THE BODY? I stood in that warehouse, and there is no obvious answer. If Marcus loaded himself into a CyberLife android and walked out a side door, that machine is somewhere right now. If Blake knows where, they did not say. The faked-death theory held eleven votes because it was the most comfortable available truth: nobody had to be guilty, nobody had to be next. I am not comfortable with comfortable truths. Whatever Marcus has become, that story is not over.

CLOSING

Here is the part I keep turning over. When the faked-death theory started to look thin for whatever investigation came next, the room turned, in the final seconds, on Blake, the NeurAI operator who ran the memory market all night. Nine votes, almost at once. Blake was the only person in the warehouse without a personal stake in Marcus, the only outsider. Convicting Blake required no one to betray a peer or revisit a single one of their own choices.

"Marcus has no hold over us!"

Quinn Sterling, shouted to the room before the final vote

Quinn had just shouted that, and then the votes flew to the person who had been buying their memories for cash all night. The room found it easier to name the manager than the architect. And the fallout of our little investigation together is even more fascinating. I have been receiving some relevant tips and anonymous leaks all day: Alex Reeves, one of the people Marcus stole from, is now in line to run the company. The leaked offer email commended Alex on her negotiation skills. Jess Kane is leaving the country to recover despite her assertions during the investigation that she had no money. And Phil Kowalski walked off his construction job this afternoon despite his gambling debts. Our investigation is concluded. But we are just beginning to see who really profited from that investigation based on what they do after.
