## LEDE

Three accusation theories collided in the warehouse this morning. The most direct — that Marcus had overdosed on the drugs his own supplier reproduced for him — got three votes. The best-documented — that Morgan Reed had architected the shell-account scheme that hollowed Marcus's company — got six. The third theory pointed at a chemist named Quinn Sterling, who had not woken up in the warehouse with the others. Quinn got nine. The room rejected the explanation that required no killer, rejected the one that pointed at a sitting neighbor, and broke for the one whose target was not there to answer.

From my newsroom screen, with Ashe Motoko reporting from the floor, I tracked the deliberation. By her account, Kai Andersen pressed a bundle of memory recordings into Remi Whitman's hand at 5:55 AM: "Here, these are all for you." The count came at 6:04. Whatever was in the bundle, Remi had nine minutes to argue from it.

Behind the deliberation, $11,585,000 moved through Blake's table. Three of the voters had signed their own first names to $3.06 million of it. As I file this story, sources inside NeurAI have leaked to me that Marcus's widow is being lined up for his job. What came across my desk and what got buried are not the same story.

## WHAT'S MISSING

No exposed recording shows what happened to Marcus between the failed lockdown at 1:50 AM and the divorce-attorney email at 2:08. The technology Marcus had built to pull memories out of his guests had — by his own design — left them in no condition to record what came next.

Quinn Sterling was at the party — Blake's recovered attendees list confirms it. Marcus had put Quinn on the guest list four days earlier, ordered the next batch of Psychotrophin-3A delivered in person, told Quinn to meet him at the bar at ten. What Quinn did once there, whether Quinn ever crossed paths with Marcus in the chaos at 1:50 AM, whether Quinn had any role in the protocol Marcus ran on himself — none of these are in any recording I have. Nine voters said yes anyway.

By Ashe's account, Kai handed Remi a bundle of memory recordings at 5:55 AM with *"Here, these are all for you."* The Quinn evidence Remi argued from was already on the public record by then — qui002 and qui003 had been exposed to the Detective two minutes earlier as anonymous tips. What else was in Kai's bundle, where the rest of it came from, what Remi did with it in the nine minutes between the handoff and the count — none of that is on any record I can see. I do not know what stayed in the bag.

I cannot tell you what Sam buried for $1.71 million, or what Flip buried for $750,000, or what Jamie buried for $600,000. Account names, totals, transaction counts. That is the shape of what they sold. The contents are with Blake and his handlers at NeurAI headquarters.

## CLOSING

The vote stands. The room named Quinn Sterling and gave Remi Whitman the win. Two other theories — one that required no killer at all but implicated Sam with manslaughter, and one that pointed at a sitting neighbor — had to lose for that to happen.

Sam Thorne argued the overdose theory despite the risk to his own freedom. The evidentiary chain ran through his own diary: Marcus self-experimenting, doses higher than his chemist had warned were safe, the recorded chaos of 1:50 AM when Marcus had the drug in his own hand. The theory required no perpetrator beyond Marcus himself. Three votes. Vic Kingsley and Alex Reeves prosecuted Morgan Reed on the strongest documentary record of the morning — shell vehicles, lobbying letters, the press management Taylor was running for her. The financial crime was real. The murder accusation was their leap from it. Six votes. Remi Whitman walked nine minutes from a memory-recording handoff to a verdict that named a man who had not woken up in the warehouse. Three documents. No opportunity in evidence. No defendant in the room. Nine votes.

The room rejected the explanation that implicated Marcus as the cause of his own death. Rejected the one that pointed at a sitting neighbor. Instead it broke for the one whose target was not there to answer.

In a market that turned memory into inventory and silence into a tradeable asset, the truth was the most expensive thing on the shelf. Sam's theory would have cost Sam his freedom. Vic and Alex's would have cost the coalition a neighbor. Remi's cost nobody in the room a thing. The room shopped accordingly.

Memory was the currency last night. Access to the recording was the currency this morning. Whoever decided what reached Remi before the vote decided what the room could vote on. Surveillance capitalism in this story is not the drug, and it is not the company. It is the brokerage. The people who control the recordings control the verdict. The people who buried their secrets in their own first names are still going to work tomorrow. The people who buried theirs anonymously won't have to answer for anything.

The chemist takes the fall. The CEO takes the chair. Sam, Flip, and Jamie signed their first names for three million dollars. Whoever took the other eight could afford not to.
