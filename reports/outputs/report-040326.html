Marcus Blackwood Is Dead. His Memorial Fund Is Open for Deposits.

Eighteen people examined what was left of a tech CEO's life. They concluded he destroyed himself. Then $4.71 million in hush money moved through shell accounts bearing the names of people still in the room.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-040326-26

Marcus Blackwood is dead. This morning, eighteen people sat in a room and watched his extracted memories play back on a screen. What they heard was a man who spent the final hours of his life stealing code, drugging partygoers, and trying to command chemically incapacitated people to secure evidence only he could access. The group voted. The conclusion was nearly unanimous: Marcus killed Marcus.

But someone named a burial account after his memorial fund and collected $1.555 million in hush money before the investigation was three hours old. A dead man's name, repurposed as a deposit slip. I was there when the account hit the tracker. Nobody flinched.

The Story

Start with the drugs. Sam Thorne's memory from 10:44 PM last night shows Marcus already out of his mind, high on what Sam describes as the "usual party blend." Sam had just given it to him. But Marcus was already somewhere else entirely, loose-lipped and reckless, confessing that the BizAI algorithm wasn't his own work. That Alex Reeves had rewritten the whole thing and Marcus had taken the credit.

God's Favorite Idiot
SAM.2 - 10:44PM - God's favorite idiot. Jesus Christ, MARCUS is so high! And you just gave him your usual party blend. He should not be so out of his mind that told you that! The BizAI algorithm wasn't his own work. ALEX rewrote the whole thing and MARCUS somehow still took credit. Is he feeling remorse? No. This is MARCUS we're talking about. You bite your tongue and take another hit.
Sam Thorne

Sam bit his tongue. Took another hit. That's the reflex I saw repeated in every thread of this investigation: people who knew and chose the comfortable path. But here's the thing about Marcus's drugged confession. It wasn't remorse. It was a system failing. The man who built NeurAI on stolen work was losing his ability to keep the lie running, and the chemicals were accelerating the collapse.

Alex Reeves had been screaming about this for five years. A memory from 9:18 PM shows the text he sent Vic Kingsley that night: a cease-and-desist, a plea, a warning. "M stole the idea & design for NeurAI from me. Just look at the code base architecture! Everyone knows MARCUS can't code for shit." Vic's response was measured. "Okay, let's talk. Meet me at the bar at 10:00."

The Last Warning
ALEX.1 - 9:18PM - ALEX texts VIC: Look, you don't want a lawsuit coming after NeurAI. This is the last chance to make things right / Cease & Desist. Don't want lawyers involved / M stole the idea & design for NeurAI from me. just look at the code base architecture! Everyone knows MARCUS can't code for shit / Still, he profits, while I'm rage-texting you like a crazy person / I just want some credit and MARCUS gone from from NeurAI / VIC texts : Okay, let's talk. Meet me at the bar at 10:00?
Alex Reeves

By 11:32 PM, Alex was done talking. A memory shows Marcus bragging about the BizAI sale one more time. "Best deal I ever made!" Alex's fist connected with Marcus's jaw. Sarah Blackwood pulled him off. "Worth it," the memory records. "Finally worth it." Five years of stolen work in every punch. And still, somehow, not enough.

Marcus wasn't just stealing from Alex. At 12:38 AM, Kai Andersen opened a laptop and showed Cass a code segment Marcus had embedded in the party's installation design. Cass needed ten seconds to recognize the Synesthesia Engine — their own creation, stolen before Marcus had signed the contract or paid for it.

Ten Seconds to Recognize Your Own Code
KAI.4 - 12:38AM - You open your laptop and show CASS the code segment that MARCUS insisted you include in your installation design for tonight's party. CASS needs no more than 10 seconds to recognize it. This is their code, without a doubt. But MARCUS claimed it as his own. CASS told you that he hadn't yet signed the contract or paid them the money they agreed upon. Why was he so anxious to install the Synesthesia Engine at his party tonight? What makes this program worth stealing and executing on unsuspecting guests?
Kai Andersen

And then Taylor Chase enters the frame. Not at 12:38 AM. Earlier. At 9:51 PM, Taylor was holding court, calling Ashe Motoko a loser and bragging about the NeurAI launch exclusive. Zia Bishara's memory captures the moment Taylor's performance shifts from cruelty to confession: "They had the receipts on Marcus. But they never understood that the story's not about the truth." Taylor had tipped Marcus off before Ashe's exposé went to print. Got Ashe fired. Got the exclusive as payment.

The Story's Not About the Truth
TAYLOR.2 - 9:51PM - TAYLOR: "I can't believe that ASHE is even showing their face here. What a loser. I mean, look. They had the receipts on MARCUS. But they never understood that the story's not about the truth. No sirree… I know you gotta keep some doors open. Don't get me wrong. Sucks to be ASHE - grinding away at their blog or whatever, then showing up here as some loser's plus one… It makes me appreciate my spot on the VIP list. Gotta be grateful for the small things, you know? And I did get the exclusive on NeurAI's launch out of it."
Taylor Chase

Marcus stole code. Taylor buried the journalism that would have exposed it. The same instinct, different instruments. And the same pharmacologist was getting squeezed underneath it all. Quinn Sterling's memory from 9:59 PM shows Marcus had discovered the unusual side effects of Psychotrophin-3A, a neural plasticity compound Quinn had been researching. Marcus was obsessed with it. He'd found Quinn's buried safety assessments and was using them as blackmail, demanding another batch that night. The drug was dangerous. Marcus didn't care.

The drugs weren't recreational. At 12:32 AM, Skyler Iyer cornered Marcus at the bar. CyberLife's deadline had passed. Marcus's AI was supposed to be ready for a working prototype, but the code was buggy — or, as the investigation would later prove, fraudulent. Skyler's solution was blunt: program the robotic prototype with the stolen memory recordings instead.

What's the Worst That Could Happen?
SKYLER.4 - 12:32AM - It's after midnight, so you go track MARCUS down at the bar. "You missed our deadline. You were supposed to show me a working prototype by tonight." MARCUS deflects. He says the AI's still buggy. "So try programming the prototype I sent you with your memory recordings instead. What's the worst that could happen?" MARCUS deflects again. "It's untested technology. What if something goes wrong?" But you've had enough of his delay tactics. It's now or never.
Skyler Iyer

Quinn blackmailed for the compound. Sam supplying the party blend. Skyler demanding the extracted memories be loaded into a machine. Marcus wasn't self-destructing in isolation. He was the node where every pressure converged. By 1:50 AM, his own memory shows the wreckage: the extractions had worked, but he'd told drugged partygoers to secure memories with combinations only he knew. Sam wandered off mid-command. Sarah was staring at a wall, tears streaming. Marcus had built a system that required lucid participants and then ensured nobody was lucid. Including himself.

Drugged Zombies and Lost Combinations
MARCUS.4 - 1:50AM - The extractions worked. The instructions were clear: "Your memories are now in the tokens. Secure them. Combinations only you know. Protect them however you can." Simple. Now you just need these drugged zombies to LOCK them. "SAM—take this token. Lock the—." They wander off mid-command. SARAH's staring at a wall, tears streaming. This is chaos. You don't think ANYONE has the right combinations anymore. Drugged people make terrible assistants.
Marcus Blackwood

At 1:36 AM, fourteen minutes before Marcus would attempt his chaotic memory-locking protocol, Sarah Blackwood found Jess Kane crying in a bathroom. Sarah put it together. Marcus's baby was Jess's. What happened next is the single most unexpected moment in the evidence record.

That Baby Deserves Better
SARAH.4 - 1:36AM - You find JESS in the bathroom, crying, and suddenly put it together. You squeak out between unexpected tears: "MARCUS's baby is yours?" She's sobbing so hard now that she can't even speak, so she nods. You thought you'd be outraged, but seeing her here in front of you, you just feel resigned. You say: "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."
Sarah Blackwood

Two women who had every reason to destroy each other chose grace instead. Neither would remember it by morning. The drugs would take this moment along with everything else. But the evidence kept it, and the investigation would give it back.

Forty-five minutes earlier, Marcus's oldest friends had finally gathered to confront what they'd enabled for years. The convergence came at 12:50 AM, when Mel Nilsson called an emergency meeting of the Stanford Four, minus Marcus. Sam came with the drug supply confession. Mel had the paternity secrets and the hidden bank accounts. Nat Francisco had been pushing Sam to confront the situation for hours. And Sam's memory holds a line that still sits with me: "But wait until they learn what you and NAT figured out." Whatever that was, it never made it into the exposed evidence.

Then Remi Whitman turned the laptop around. 1:08 AM. Zia Bishara's Oracle Ledger commit from 2023 on the left. NeurAI's "proprietary" system from 2027 on the right. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. Marcus hadn't even changed the notes. Vic Kingsley finally saw what her $30 million investment had purchased: a sham. Alex had been right all along. The code was worthless.

The group reached its verdict this morning. Taylor Chase, without rising from her seat, asked the room: "Do you want the TRUTH? OR do you want to go HOME tonight?" The vote arrived like an exhale. Marcus killed Marcus. Nearly everyone agreed. By then, $4.71 million had already moved through Blake's network. The police were minutes away.

Follow the Money

Six shell accounts. Twenty-one extracted memories buried. $4,710,000 paid out through Blake's network over the course of a single morning — while we were still examining evidence, still watching memories play back, still trying to understand what happened last night. That's not grief. That's an industry.

The Marcus Blackwood Memorial Fund collected $1,555,000 across seven transactions. I need you to sit with that. Someone created a burial account named after the dead man whose death we were investigating, and funneled seven memories through it while we were still in the room. Seven transactions, $1.555 million, and no one has stepped forward to claim they operate the fund. That's not a memorial. A memorial doesn't require Blake's services. This is a mechanism — someone registered a dead man's name as an account, and whoever controls that name collects the burial payments. The name itself is the weapon: who in that room was going to question a fund honoring the man they'd just voted dead by his own hand?

I overheard Sam Thorne mention donating to Marcus's memorial fund during the investigation. Sam also brought evidence to this reporter. But here's what I've been turning over since: donations don't go through Blake. Burials do. Sam wasn't just donating. Sam was burying memories — memories that contained something Sam wanted hidden — and directing the payments to a name that made the suppression feel like an act of love. Sam, who made Marcus's drugs, who worried about Marcus's decline, who called an emergency meeting of their oldest friends to intervene — was also someone with secrets worth burying. The fund gave Sam a way to do both at once: protect himself and honor his friend. Or at least tell himself that's what he was doing.

This scheme ran on people who were drugged and memory-impaired, during an active murder investigation, with the police on their way. Someone set up a dead man's name as a deposit slip and let the room's guilt — and the room's secrets — do the work.

Morgan Reed's account collected $1,230,000 across just three transactions, the highest per-transaction average of any account. Morgan was first to access the drug lab this morning, first to solve the Psychotrophin-3A puzzle. Why is a DC lobbyist racing to solve a pharmaceutical puzzle? But here's what complicates the picture: the fingerprints on Morgan's final transaction are completely different from the prior ones. Someone else was routing transactions through Morgan's account — putting Morgan's name on activity that may not have been Morgan's. That looks less like collaboration and more like someone casting suspicion. Meanwhile, Morgan and Vic Kingsley were spotted meeting in quiet corners throughout the investigation. The crisis manager and the venture capitalist, coordinating — while the memorial fund collected $1.555 million under a name neither of them had to sign.

Riley Torres admitted to burying evidence. Six memories, $775,000. Riley claimed they were personal, related to Sarah Blackwood's private information, nothing criminal. That's a lot of money for nothing criminal. Then there's Ypl42: one memory, $450,000, anonymous. The highest single-transaction payout. Someone wanted no name attached to that silence. An account bearing Sarah Blackwood's name collected $450,000 for two memories. An account bearing Alex Reeves's name holds $200,000 — but Alex was seen early this morning in a heated confrontation with Blake, raising his voice: "I DON'T TRUST you people!" The account under his name has timeline inconsistencies. It may not actually be his.

Here is what I can tell you about who brought evidence to this reporter and who didn't. Ten people came to me directly with memories during the investigation: Sam, Jamie, Kai, Jess, Mel, Ashe, Cass, Zia, Quinn, Skyler. The bartender. The artist. The drug supplier. The fired journalist. The technologists whose code was stolen. Every one of Vic Kingsley's memories is absent from the exposed evidence. All four — sealed. Morgan Reed's memories — also absent. Every one of them. The venture capitalist who funded NeurAI and the crisis manager who ran interference for Marcus achieved complete opacity. The people with the least institutional power in that room gave me everything. The people with the most to protect gave me nothing.

Taylor Chase spent part of this morning discussing with Blake about requesting access to convince others to bury information. Taylor has no registered account — no personal paper trail on the burial ledger. The person who called the final vote, the person who built a career suppressing stories about Marcus, was actively recruiting others to bury while keeping clean. The suppression network that killed Ashe Motoko's exposé years ago has found a more efficient payment model.

The Players

Alex Reeves brought all four memories to this reporter. No hedging. No burial account. This morning, I watched Alex hold a conference in the back room with Remi Whitman and Vic Kingsley. He told them plainly: "I don't have a problem with you, personally. It's Marcus who did me wrong." After five years of stolen credit, Alex didn't want revenge. He wanted the record corrected. Remi made sure the right people couldn't look away from the code comparison. Zia and Ashe each brought all four memories forward. Kai and Cass independently exposed the Synesthesia Engine theft. Quinn's blackmail memory made it to the record, which means someone decided that Marcus's leverage expired when he did.

Jamie Woods brought three memories directly to me. The bartender overheard Marcus and Quinn's drug exchange at 10 PM, overheard Skyler's midnight ultimatum about the robot prototype, witnessed Marcus handing Flip an envelope full of cash. Jamie was being paid by Skyler to document exactly these kinds of conversations. While the powerful operated through shell accounts and proxy arrangements, the person everyone in the room treated as furniture walked up to this reporter and handed over the night's most critical observations. Jamie and Remi collaborated frequently during the investigation — the bartender who saw everything and the CEO who needed proof, finding common cause.

Skyler Iyer has $163 million in Vic Kingsley's fund. At midnight, Skyler pressured Marcus to program a CyberLife prototype with stolen human memories. This morning, Skyler watched the code comparison prove that NeurAI — the company at the center of that investment — was a $30 million sham. Skyler brought three memories to this reporter, including the midnight demand. Whatever else Skyler is calculating, the CyberLife CEO did not try to bury the evidence of what was asked of Marcus that night.

Mel Nilsson chose Sarah Blackwood's welfare over group loyalty, but waited until 12:50 AM to act on information that had been circulating for hours. Mel brought three memories to this reporter. Sam came with evidence and with guilt. Nat Francisco had pushed Sam to confront the situation earlier that evening. These are people who watched Marcus deteriorate and chose the comfortable path until it was nearly too late — then chose transparency when it mattered.

Flip had no registered account with Blake's market. Brought no memories to this reporter. Two of Flip's memories were buried through someone else's account — unless Flip was devious enough to bury under another name, which nothing in the evidence suggests. Flip came to Marcus's party carrying $30K in gambling debt and an identity guarded from everyone: Phil Kowalski, construction worker, not "party architect." Marcus knew the real name and held it like a leash. He called Flip his "blue collar treasure hunter" and thought it was a compliment. After Marcus's death, the burial market processed Flip's memories without Flip's involvement. The person with the least power in the room didn't even get to choose their own silence.

During this morning's investigation, the exposed evidence played back a moment neither woman remembered. At 1:36 AM, Sarah had found Jess Kane crying in a bathroom. She'd learned the baby was Marcus's. She'd offered solidarity instead of rage. The drugs stole that memory from both of them. They came into the room this morning expecting to be strangers at best, enemies at worst — the wife and the pregnant mistress. Then the evidence showed them who they'd been to each other at 1:36 AM, and Jess greeted Sarah as a close friend. Not because she was pretending. Because the investigation had returned something the drugs took: the knowledge that they had already chosen each other. That may be the only thing Marcus's memory extraction protocol ever gave back.

What's Missing

Twenty-one memories buried across six accounts. $4.71 million in silenced evidence. I can tell you the shape of the silence. I can't tell you what's inside it.

What I can tell you is what's missing from the record. Sam's memory references something he and Nat Francisco figured out, a revelation intended for the Stanford Four meeting. It never surfaced. Vic Kingsley received Alex's stolen-code accusation at 9:18 PM and agreed to meet at 10. But the code comparison didn't happen until 1:08 AM. That's a three-hour gap between the warning and the proof. What did Vic do with that information in the interim?

The group didn't arrive at self-destruction without considering alternatives. Over the course of the morning, the room explored other possibilities. One theory held weight long enough to be discussed seriously: Vic Kingsley paid Sam Thorne to help Marcus overdose. Consider the circumstantial architecture. Vic had already decided to replace Marcus with Alex — a plan in motion before the party began. Morgan — who was seen coordinating with Vic in quiet corners throughout the investigation — was first to the drug lab this morning and first to solve the Psychotrophin-3A puzzle. A strange priority for a DC lobbyist. Every one of Vic's memories is sealed. The memorial fund appeared while Morgan and Vic were meeting. I can't tell you what those meetings were about. I can tell you the timing. The self-destruction verdict may be correct. But it is also, by a wide margin, the most comfortable answer for the most powerful people in the room. Another theory named Alex as the killer — plausible given five years of rage and the 11:32 PM fistfight. These were set aside when Taylor forced the vote, with the police already en route. I don't know if the room chose truth or chose to go home before the choice was made for them.

Quinn was blackmailed into supplying Psychotrophin-3A. Marcus was self-medicating with it AND using it for memory extraction. Skyler was demanding the extracted memories be loaded into a CyberLife prototype. The drug supply, the memory theft, and the robotics deal form a single chain of pressure. If any link had broken — if Quinn had refused, if Sam had stopped supplying the party blend, if Skyler had extended the deadline — would Marcus have survived the night?

And the memorial fund. Seven memories buried under a dead man's name. Who created that account? The name is too pointed to be random. It collected more money than any individual's account. The investigation's biggest financial mystery isn't what was buried. It's who decided that Marcus Blackwood's name still had market value even in death.

The group voted that Marcus killed Marcus. Taylor Chase — the person with the deepest documented history of suppressing stories about Marcus Blackwood — demanded truth and ended the investigation minutes before the police came through the doors. The person who buried Ashe Motoko's journalism called the final vote. I don't know if that's irony or strategy. I know it determined what the police walked into.

The system Marcus built didn't die with him. It found new hosts before his body was cold. Sam Thorne brought evidence to this reporter and buried memories in the name of a dead man's memorial fund — a fund that, by every metric I can measure, exists to collect burial payments under a name no one in the room dared question. Sam had secrets worth burying. The fund gave Sam a way to suppress them and feel virtuous about it — a donation to a dead friend's legacy. That's not just a con targeting the innocent. That's a system that gives compromised people a noble story to tell themselves, and extracts value from the telling. The burial market doesn't need to prey on the powerless. It just needs to give everyone a reason to participate.

Ten people brought evidence to this reporter. Among them: a bartender who was paid to observe, an artist whose code was stolen, a drug supplier wracked with guilt, a journalist whose career was destroyed for getting too close to the truth. The venture capitalist's memories — all four — are sealed. The crisis manager's — sealed. The people with the least to gain from transparency gave the most. The people with the most to protect achieved total opacity. The burial market doesn't create inequality. It reveals it. And rewards it.

But some people in that room chose something harder than transparency and more dangerous than silence. Sarah Blackwood and Jess Kane discovered, through the exposed evidence, that they had found each other at 1:36 AM and chosen solidarity over a system that had victimized them both. The drugs erased that choice. The investigation restored it. Jess greeted Sarah as a friend this morning, and meant it — not because of what she remembered, but because of what the evidence showed her she'd already done. That moment survived Marcus's drugs. It survived the burial market. It may be the only thing from last night that did.

Marcus Blackwood is dead. His memorial fund is open for deposits.
