# A Murder, Put to a Vote
### *(Pass 1 — fact-corrected draft. Copy-edit is Pass 2.)*

*Twenty guests spent the night deciding which of them killed Marcus Blackwood. By a single vote, they named Vic Kingsley, the most powerful person in the room and the hardest to control. A NovaNews investigation into a verdict that was never really about the evidence.*

**Cassandra Nova** · Senior Investigative Reporter · February 22, 2027

› *HERO — photo-8: "Vic Kingsley, an hour before twenty people made her their answer."*

## LEDE
Marcus Blackwood is dead. The twenty guests from last night's party spent this morning arguing over which of them killed him, and every one of them had a reason. By the time the arguing burned down to a vote, it was nine to eight, and the name on top was Vic Kingsley: the wealthiest person in the room, the investor everyone needed and nobody could steer, the one person there who answered to no one else present.

I was in that room. I watched it narrow twenty suspects to one by a single ballot. What I did not watch was an investigation. The person who stood to gain the most from Marcus being gone walked out of that room without a target on his back. I will get to him. First, understand what the night actually had too much of: motive.

## THE STORY
Start with the theft, because almost everyone in that room had a piece of it. Marcus Blackwood built NeurAI, a company he told investors would transform memory capture, on code he did not write. Zia Bishara wrote it. She built a storage algorithm called the Oracle Ledger back in Ezra Sullivan's incubator program, let other students prototype with it, and watched Marcus quietly fold it into NeurAI's so-called proprietary system. Remi Whitman ran the forensic comparison and put it on a laptop screen in front of the two people who mattered most. The builders had become the investigators.

> **EVIDENCE — Zia Bishara, recovered memory, 12:31 AM:** "Left: your Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. Right: NeurAI's 'proprietary' system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. Marcus didn't even bother changing your notes. Sloppy thief. Too bad the idiot didn't catch the massive bug that you've since fixed."

Read that again. Same variable names. Same comments. He did not steal carefully. He stole lazily, and he left in a bug Zia had already fixed in her own version. That is the company a roomful of investors lined up to fund. And Zia was not alone. Cass Zhang pitched Marcus a tool she calls the Synesthesia Engine, demoed it, watched him grin and promise her fifty thousand dollars, then never sign and never pay. Skyler Iyer caught Marcus fishing for CyberLife's neural protocols he had no business knowing. A police report from October 2024 tells the oldest piece of it: Ezra Sullivan's office at the Electronic Frontier Foundation was burglarized, and the only things taken were the files from a 2020 incubator program. The program that trained Cass, Quinn, Zia, and Skyler. The pattern was years old before anyone got to the party.

› *PHOTO — photo-3: "Cass and Zia at the evidence board, the two builders whose work Marcus is accused of stealing, building the case against him."*

Then there is what Marcus did with all that stolen science. The Synesthesia Engine, the tool Cass never got paid for, was running through Kai Andersen's party installation the whole night. Kai did not know. Marcus handed him a code segment, told him to run it exactly as written, and Kai only connected the dots at 12:38 in the morning, when Cass needed ten seconds to recognize her own work. The question Kai wrote into his own memory is the one nobody could answer yet: why was Marcus so anxious to run it on unsuspecting guests?

> **EVIDENCE — Marcus Blackwood, recovered memory, 1:50 AM:** "The extractions worked. The instructions were clear: 'Your memories are now in the tokens. Secure them. Combinations only you know. Protect them however you can.' Simple. Now you just need these drugged zombies to lock them. 'Sam, take this token. Lock the...' They wander off mid-command. Sarah's staring at a wall, tears streaming. This is chaos. You don't think anyone has the right combinations anymore. Drugged people make terrible assistants."

That is Marcus's own recovered memory. I surfaced it myself. It is a confession. He drugged his own guests, ran a memory extraction on them, and stood there watching them wander the rooms in a chemical fog. The compound came from Quinn Sterling, whose research into a substance called Psychotrophin-3A Marcus found out about and used to blackmail Quinn into producing more. And Nat Francisco, sitting with Sam Thorne and the rest of the old Stanford circle, had figured out the rotten center of all of it: the AI underpinning NeurAI does not work. It never worked. Marcus did not fix the fraud. He started stealing memories instead.

› *PHOTO — photo-5: "I caught Sarah near the case board while the room still argued competing theories. Behind her, the board had already rendered its verdict on Marcus."*

And then there is the human cost. Jess Kane was pregnant with Marcus's child. Sarah Blackwood, his wife, had finally thrown him out and came to the party to confront him. Marcus had sent each of them the same apology card, the same rose on the front, the same line inside: she means nothing to me, don't leave me. To his wife, the throwaway "she" was the mistress. To his mistress, it was his wife. Sam Thorne had found the paternity test and mailed it anonymously to Sarah so she would know. Everything in those rooms pointed toward two women becoming enemies. Instead, at 1:36 in the morning, in a bathroom, they found something else.

> **"You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."** — Sarah Blackwood, in Jess Kane's recovered memory

So the night did not lack for people with reasons to want Marcus gone. The thieves he robbed. The artist he used. The researcher he blackmailed. The investors he defrauded. The women he wrecked. Motive was the cheapest thing in that house. So the question was never who had a motive. The question was: who did the room choose, and why? The room's stated answer was money. That is where my reporting and the vote part ways.

## FOLLOW THE MONEY
The room built its case against Vic on money. The theory: Morgan Reed was tied to shell accounts sheltering Marcus's cash, and if Marcus died, Vic would inherit the lot. The only problem is that the ledger refuses to tell it. Across the black market that ran all night, $4.36 million in memories moved through named accounts.

> **FINANCIAL TRACKER — $4,360,000 total:** Ft $1,155,000 · L (collector, buried nothing) $1,125,000 · Xoxo $900,000 · Phil (swept to L, net $0) $750,000 · X $475,000 · Sam $450,000 · Vic (swept to L, net $0) $375,000 · Ceo $225,000 · Taylor $30,000

An account labeled Vic took in $375,000 and then swept every dollar of it out to a collector that goes only by the letter L. An account labeled Phil, the construction-worker alias the recovered memories tie to Flip, did the same with $750,000. Both emptied into L. And L buried nothing of its own, produced no memories, and still ended the night holding $1.125 million. There is no account named Morgan anywhere in this ledger. The room's entire premise has no support in the only record that could have backed it.

› *PHOTO — photo-6: "Ashe and Flip tear through the couch cushions, the same cushions Flip's recovered memory showed Marcus used to hide cash drops for a gambling debt he held over her."*

A name on a black-market account proves nothing. An account that says Vic and then hands all its money to an anonymous letter is exactly what it would look like if someone wanted the room to read Vic's name as a motive. What I can tell you is the shape of the money: the account bearing the accused's name kept zero. The question the room never asked, the one that would take apart its whole theory, is the simplest on the page. Who is L? I cannot tell you. I can only tell you the room did not want to know.

## THE PLAYERS
Here is the comparison the room would not make. Nobody who cast a vote was confused. They were choosing. So look at the choice through the one candidate the room let off the hook, and start with what was already in motion before Marcus ever hit the floor.

> **EVIDENCE — Vic Kingsley, recovered memory, 9:55 PM:** "Alex is in, Marcus is out. No more C & D. No more potential criminal conspiracy charges. You've just got to do this, quiet like and ASAP, so that NeurAI remains a viable investment."

Vic was running a transition. In her own recovered memory she has already decided it: Alex in, Marcus out, before midnight. The deal was done. And someone saw it happen. Jamie 'Volt' Woods stepped outside for a smoke and overheard the man who had just punched Marcus in a panicked huddle with a VC.

> **"He's out. You're in. Trust me. It's done. Please just come inside."** — overheard by Jamie 'Volt' Woods

› *PHOTO — photo-2: "Alex spent the morning building the alliances that would save him. Remi, who worked alongside him all session, was the one who surfaced the proof that Marcus built NeurAI on stolen code."*

So look at Alex Reeves, who had the oldest grievance in the room. His cease-and-desist letter, recovered from the night, lays it out: he and Marcus co-founded a company called BizAI, where Alex did all the engineering while Marcus worked the investors, and when Marcus sold it for three hundred million dollars, Alex was handed a "technical consultation fee" instead of his share of the equity. Now Alex says NeurAI runs on that same stolen code, and his lawyers had given the board until the end of February to make him whole. He threw the punch that night. And by 9:55 he was being installed in Marcus's chair. Motive, opportunity, and the prize, all stacked on one man. He got eight votes. He made one more friend than Vic lost. The director's own read is that the vote broke toward Vic instead of Alex because of the people in that room invested in Alex's success.

That is the asymmetry, and it is the whole story. The room blamed the most powerful person present and protected the person with the most to gain. Nine to eight is not an evidentiary verdict. It is the price of being liked. I am not telling you Alex killed Marcus. I have no evidence that he did, and I will not pretend otherwise. I am telling you that the man with the strongest case against him was the safest person in the room.

Everyone else made choices too. Zia and Cass put the theft on the record. Quinn surfaced the blackmail used against them. Nat named the fraud at the company's core. Jess and Sarah refused to become each other's enemies. Ashe Motoko kept reporting even after Taylor Chase had helped bury his earlier story, and landed a rare on-record interview with Ezra Sullivan. Skyler guarded what Marcus tried to steal. Flip got dragged into Marcus's orbit by a debt. And then the two who haunt the edges: Morgan, the ghost at the center of the financial theory the room built and then refused to point at, and Taylor, a journalist who once buried the truth about Marcus for access.

## WHAT'S MISSING

› *PHOTO — photo-4: "The board named the victim and his crime before the room asked who profited. Taylor, Jamie, and Riley work the same wall."*

Start with Morgan Reed, because the room did not. Morgan and Vic went into that party as allies. Morgan, a crisis manager whose own reputation was bolted to Marcus's, had been meeting with Vic to deal with what the two of them called the Marcus problem. Then the room built its entire money theory on Morgan, the lobbyist supposedly sheltering Marcus's cash, and would not turn to look at Morgan. Morgan is the one who handed Taylor the interview that could have ended Marcus: shell companies, insider trading, non-consensual experiments, lying under oath. And there is no Morgan account anywhere in the ledger. The room kept the hinge of its own argument, and Vic's own partner in managing Marcus, standing in the room all night and never pointed.

There is another silence. Riley Torres came to that party for one reason: Sarah. The two have been best friends since law school, and Riley, an environmental lawyer who spends her days protecting people from exploitation, kept a card of reminders of Sarah's worth for the day Sarah was ready to leave him. Whatever Riley saw that night, her recovered memory was buried, and I cannot read it. I do not know what was in it. I know it went dark. And I know the room let $4.36 million in memories vanish into accounts it never bothered to trace, while the one name at the center of its own theory stood untouched in the room all night. I know which questions never got asked.

## CLOSING
Marcus Blackwood built a company on stolen code, blackmailed a researcher into making a dangerous drug, ran a non-consensual memory extraction on his own guests, and turned their stolen experiences into something you could trade on a ledger. The room spent the night fighting over those extracted memories and ended by naming the most powerful woman in it, by a single vote. Meanwhile the most protected person in the room, the one with the most to gain, collects the prize. At the time of writing, an anonymous source on the NeurAI board confirmed it: Alex will be offered the interim CEO role once this blows over. That is how power actually moved. Not in the vote, but in who was safe to protect.

Marcus made memory a commodity. The room made the verdict a referendum on who was safe to blame. And somewhere in that ledger, an account called L is sitting on $1.125 million with no name attached to it. Nobody in that room asked who it belongs to. That question is still open. So is the case.
