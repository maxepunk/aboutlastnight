He Took From Everyone. Then Someone Stopped Looking Away.

Marcus Blackwood stole code, exploited trust, and extracted memories from his own guests. Nineteen people enabled him for years. One night in a warehouse, they all stopped at once. Inside the investigation into what happened next.

Tiresias Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-032826-26

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Twenty people were in that warehouse last night, and by morning, only nineteen were left to sort through the wreckage. I was there when the extracted memories started playing back. Stolen code from at least three separate creators, all passed off as Marcus's innovation. Shell companies layered like nesting dolls. An experimental drug pipeline built on blackmail. A coordinated takedown planned by the dead man's oldest friends. And one name, written in plain text on a $530,000 burial account, that nobody can explain.

The group voted six to five. They pointed at Morgan Reed. I was there when the hands went up. Five people in that room looked at the same evidence and pointed at Sarah Blackwood instead. Sarah, who had spent the investigation working closely with Jess Kane, the woman carrying Marcus's unborn child. The crisis manager stands accused by a margin of one. That's not a conviction. That's a coin flip with a body attached.

But this story isn't about who killed Marcus Blackwood. It's about what he built, who he took from, and the nineteen people who looked the other way until the night they didn't.

The Story

Start with Morgan Reed. He built shell companies for Marcus Blackwood the way an architect builds houses — professionally, discreetly, with his name on every paper trail. When Riley Torres showed up last night brandishing the receipts, Morgan didn't run. He traded.

Morgan's mood shifted from friendly to cornered in seconds. And then he negotiated. Told Riley how to trace the money, in exchange for keeping his name out of the accusations. A professional deal, struck in the middle of a party, over documents that could end careers.

The Trade
MORGAN.2 - 10:12PM - When RILEY approaches, your mood lifts. They're always a friendly face in the crowd. Except for this time. They come in hot, brandishing a sheaf of papers documenting all the shell companies you set up for MARCUS. They've done their homework. Your name is all over the paper trail. You've seen what happens when this kind of news gets out. You'll have to figure out some way to get ahead of it. But for now, you're beat. You tell RILEY how to trace the money, in exchange for keeping your name out of their accusations.
Morgan Reed

But the shell companies were scaffolding. Underneath was something worse.

The Stanford Four. Sam Thorne, Mel Nilsson, Nat Francisco, and Marcus himself. Three of them arrived at that party last night with a plan. Sam had already mailed a paternity test to Sarah Blackwood before the first drink was poured. Mel brought divorce ammunition. But Sam knew something the others didn't. Sam and Nat had discovered something worse than infidelity or hidden bank accounts. Something about the code. Something about the drugs. Something about the memories.

Riley Torres delivered the ammunition. Mel's memory captures the handoff — years of research into Marcus's hidden accounts and insider trading, enough to build a career on. Riley gave it all to Mel to protect Sarah. That's love, not strategy. And it armed the Stanford Four with what they needed.

The Stanford Four Convene
SAM.4 - 12:50AM - MEL calls a meeting of The Stanford 4 sans MARCUS. You've all turned a blind eye and enabled him for too many years. If you don't intervene now, who know what else MARCUS is likely to do next. MEL rolls in with the paternity news and some secret bank accounts, like that's what's going to take Marcus down a peg. You were the one who mailed the paternity test to SARAH in the first place. You thought she should know. But wait until they learn what you and NAT figured out...
Sam Thorne

"But wait until they learn what you and Nat figured out." The memory cuts there. Sam's teaser hangs in the air like smoke. What did Sam and Nat discover that made paternity tests and bank accounts look like small change? The answer was already in the warehouse, on a thumb drive that Remi Whitman had taken from Marcus's secret living quarters.

Remi didn't wait for permission. A recovered memory from 9:37 last night shows her following Marcus through a secret door into what turned out to be a hidden living space inside the warehouse. Marcus was so high he could barely form sentences. He smiled when he saw her. Welcomed her in. Told her to help herself to anything. So she did. She grabbed a thumb drive and left to find Alex Reeves.

The Secret Room
REMI.2 - 9:37PM - You saw him go through the secret door, and you didn't think. You just followed him. It looks like he's taken over the other half of this warehouse to live in. You're not sure why he's not at home with his wife, and don't care. You need to talk to him about the funding discrepancy. But the man is SO HIGH that he can barely string sentences together. He smiles when he sees you in his space. Welcomes you in. Tells you to help yourself to anything. So you do. You grab a thumb drive and leave. Now you need to find Alex.
Remi Whitman

What Remi found on that drive is what Sam and Nat had been building toward. The code comparison was damning. On the left: Zia Bishara's Oracle Ledger, committed in 2023. On the right: NeurAI's "proprietary" system from 2027. Identical. Marcus hadn't even changed the variable names. He stole an algorithm and forgot to file off the serial numbers. Remi showed Vic Kingsley and Alex Reeves the comparison on Sam Thorne's laptop. Maybe now Vic would stop prioritizing NeurAI and give Remi the time of day. Or maybe Vic saw something else entirely: an exit.

But Zia's code wasn't the only thing Marcus stole. Cass Zhang had built something called the Synesthesia Engine, a neural stimulation platform for cross-sensory artistic experiences. Marcus promised Cass $50,000 for full deployment. Never signed the contract. Never paid. Instead — and Kai's install brief from Marcus confirms this — he had Kai Andersen embed specific code specs into an art installation at the party. "It's crucial that there are no problems with the environmental cues," Marcus wrote. "I need them to run exactly as written." Kai didn't know the specs were Cass's stolen code, designed to run on unsuspecting guests without their knowledge or consent. Kai and Cass discovered the theft together when they compared code at 12:38 last night. Marcus hadn't just stolen Cass's technology. He'd weaponized Kai's art to deploy it.

The Stolen Engine
CASS.4 - 12:38AM - Code comparison. Left: the Synesthesia Engine code you sent MARCUS for your demo. Right: The last minute code MARCUS insisted KAI incorporate into their installation for tonight's party. Identical. MARCUS is secretly using the Synesthesia Engine before signing the contract and paying you for it. What's more, he wants KAI to run it tonight on an unsuspecting party audience. Why would he want to do that? What's his agenda here?
Cass Zhang

And then there was the drug.

An email recovered from Quinn Sterling's files shows Marcus blackmailing Quinn about an experimental compound called Psychotrophin-3A: "I need you to bring me another batch. And you can't afford to say no, can you?" Sam Thorne's diary, found in the warehouse, confirms he manufactured it — "when you pay someone an ungodly amount of money off the books, you buy yourself a certain degree of acquiescence." Baggies of the drug were recovered from the scene, labeled with different formulations.

Jess overheard Sam and Nat discussing the compound during the party and confirmed Marcus was experimenting on himself with it. Jamie watched him take pills at the bar after a hushed conversation about "ingesting extracts — untested, dangerous." The drug was half of the machine. Cass's stolen Synesthesia Engine was the other half. Marcus ran both through Kai's installation to capture human memories on tokens he could lock and trade.

Three creators robbed. A drug pipeline built on blackmail. A memory extraction system run on stolen code and unwitting subjects. And somewhere in the middle of all this, Skyler Iyer was pressuring Marcus to go further. Text messages recovered between Skyler and Jamie show that Skyler was paying the bartender $100 per recorded interaction between Marcus's "tech savvy guests" — intelligence gathering for CyberLife. And Jamie's recovered memory from 12:32 last night captures the worst of it: Skyler insisting Marcus program a robotic prototype with the captured memories. "What's the worst that could happen?" Marcus hesitated. For once, even he understood what the technology was becoming.

The Prototype
JAMIE.4 - 12:32AM - You're busy restocking when a stranger walks in. They have the wrong vibe for a MARCUS party. "You missed our deadline." MARCUS goes pale. "Tonight we were supposed to do a test run. But you say you're AI's still buggy. So let's try programming my prototype with your memory recordings instead. What's the worst that could happen?" MARCUS: "SKYLER, it's untested technology. What if something goes wrong..." No response. You freeze. Don't breathe. Don't move. BE Invisible. It works. They walk past you like you're furniture.
Jamie 'Volt' Woods

None of these people coordinated. That's the part I keep coming back to. The Stanford Four had their plan. Remi had her thumb drive. Cass and Kai had their code comparison. Riley had the shell company documents. They all arrived at the same conclusion independently, from different angles, on the same night. And Marcus was stumbling around his secret bachelor pad, too intoxicated to understand that nineteen separate reckonings were converging on him at once.

And then there's Flip. Five extracted memories buried under his own name. $530,000. In a room where every other account hid behind pseudonyms or borrowed identities, Flip signed the ledger like a personal check. This morning, he admitted to making the burials. Used his personal account. I watched the room absorb that admission. Nobody quite knew what to do with it. Jamie's recovered memory from earlier in the evening shows Marcus handing Flip a fat envelope of cash. Penny-wise with friends, ultra generous with "friends." Whatever Flip's relationship to Marcus was, it predated this party and it involved money moving in directions nobody was tracking.

The Envelope
JAMIE.1 - 8:07PM- These motherfuckers think they run the world. Look at MARCUS and that construction worker he's been hanging around lately. MARCUS just handed over a big fat envelope. You know that's loaded full of Franklins. And MARCUS told you to skip the top shelf drinks tonight. Penny-wise when it comes to his friends, and ultra generous when it comes to his "friends." Whatever. You're definitely noting this interaction down.
Jamie 'Volt' Woods

But the most human moment in the entire evidence record didn't come from corporate espionage or financial fraud. It came from a bathroom. A recovered memory from 1:36 last night shows Sarah Blackwood finding Jess Kane crying. Putting it together. Squeezing out between unexpected tears: "Marcus's baby is yours?" Jess couldn't speak. She just nodded. Sarah had expected to feel outraged. Instead she felt resigned.

The Bathroom
SARAH.4 - 1:36AM - You find JESS in the bathroom, crying, and suddenly put it together. You squeak out between unexpected tears: "MARCUS's baby is yours?" She's sobbing so hard now that she can't even speak, so she nods. You thought you'd be outraged, but seeing her here in front of you, you just feel resigned. You say: "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."
Sarah Blackwood

"You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."

They chose each other. Sarah and Jess worked together through every hour of the investigation that followed, a partnership forged in that bathroom. Sarah was heard telling Flip, "I DON'T want you to be able to pay your debt." Then, after the warehouse was evacuated moments before a police raid, they booked a private charter to the Bahamas. Gone before this article was drafted.

Morgan, meanwhile, had already played his second hand. At 12:40 last night, he offered Taylor Chase everything needed to destroy Marcus publicly. Taylor called it journalistic gold. Morgan called it getting ahead of the story. Trading secrets to Riley for protection with one hand, feeding Taylor the kill shot with the other.

Journalistic Gold
TAYLOR.4 - 12:40AM - You offered MORGAN a chance to spill about anything, as long as it was newsworthy. They want your help taking MARCUS down. His reputation? In shambles. His business? Ruined. And what they've told you so far is journalistic gold. Hiding business income in shell companies, soliciting insider trading tips, performing dangerous experiments on unwitting test subjects, lying under oath… It's too good to pass up. You're ready to put your journalistic reputation on the line to get this incredible story out there.
Taylor Chase

So Morgan had motive. Morgan had access. And his client was self-destructing in a secret room, too high to function, while every secret he'd ever kept was being excavated by the people he trusted most.

Follow the Money

Five shell accounts. $2,635,000 in buried evidence. Seventeen extracted memories purchased and removed from the record this morning.

Daniel. $650,000. Three transactions. The largest account and the most anonymous — not a name on the roster, not a known alias. A ghost with the biggest balance.

Then Marcus. $600,000 under the dead man's own name. Two transactions. A clever investigator, I was told, trying to throw off suspicion. Or a tribute. Or a taunt. Vic Kingsley was calculating this morning, not panicking. I watched him take in the evidence with the composure of someone who'd already made his decisions. Whether the Marcus account was misdirection or memorial, someone with a VC's instinct for optics chose to put the victim's name on a ledger he could no longer object to.

KB. $555,000. Three transactions. Two women who arrived with nothing left the country on a charter flight. Sarah was in hushed conversations with Blake all morning. I can't confirm who controls the account. I can tell you the behavioral pattern, and I can tell you the initials.

Flip. $530,000. Five transactions. The only account that isn't hiding behind a pseudonym. Flip admitted to the burials this morning. Used his own name. Five hundred thirty thousand dollars is a lot of money to spend on a confession. It's even more to spend on a frame job. The system doesn't record what's inside those memories. It only records the name on the check.

And Pikachu. $300,000. Four transactions behind a cartoon character's name. The most deliberately absurd pseudonym on the ledger. Someone wanted distance. Not just anonymity, but mockery of the system itself.

I watched Morgan Reed, Alex Reeves, Vic Kingsley, and Sarah Blackwood all in hushed conversations with Blake during the investigation. I can't tell you which conversations led to which accounts. I can tell you the conversations happened. Alex was announced as the next CEO of NeurAI before the vote was even called. Someone spent $650,000 to make the Daniel account the largest on the ledger. And the person with the most to gain from demonstrating both loyalty to NeurAI and the business instincts of a CEO was already being fitted for the corner office.

The Players

Some people brought the truth. Some people bought the silence. Most did both before the morning was over.

Sam Thorne watched Marcus get so high that he confessed the BizAI algorithm was Alex Reeves's work. Bit his tongue and took another hit. The Stanford Four's coordinated exposure was years overdue, and Sam was the one who finally triggered it — carrying the drug knowledge, waiting for Mel and Nat to catch up to what he already knew.

Nat Francisco brought something the others lacked: clarity about why the enabling had to end. Nat's recovered memory from the Stanford Four meeting captures it plainly. Marcus lied about the AI that underpins his company. It doesn't work. Instead of fixing it, he's taking drugs and inventing techniques to steal people's memories. Nat had been trying to convince Sam and Mel to intervene for years. This was the night they finally listened.

The Reckoning
NAT.4 - 12:50AM - You, NAT and SAM gather together to talk about what to do with MARCUS. You've tried to convince those two to stop enabling him for years. But at this point, he might be too far gone. Now you're learning that he's lied about the AI that underpins his company. It doesn't work. But instead of fixing it, he's taking drugs and inventing techniques to steal people's memories instead? Maybe his high pressure deal with CyberLife pushed him over the edge and into this weird, out-of-the-box thinking….
Nat Francisco

Quinn Sterling was under Marcus's thumb. The threatening email in Quinn's files makes the dynamic plain: Marcus needed the drug, and Quinn couldn't walk away without risking exposure. Quinn carried that weight through the entire party. Ezra Sullivan, who had spent decades mentoring the next generation, was forced to reckon with what his mentees had become. A rare on-the-record interview with Ashe Motoko captured Ezra at their rawest: if the younger generation — Cass, Quinn, Skyler, Zia — loses the belief that change is possible, Ezra doesn't know what hope looks like anymore.

Ashe Motoko had been waiting for this night. A recovered email between Taylor Chase and Marcus proves the conspiracy: "Ashe's expose on your 'memory experiments' won't see daylight. Editor thinks it needs 'more substantiation.' She'll be reassigned to lifestyle by Monday. Now about that exclusive..." Taylor got Ashe fired from the Silicon Valley Business Journal to curry favor with Marcus. But Zia Bishara found Ashe at the party and handed over a scoop: Marcus had stolen Zia's algorithm. Ashe turned that lead into something bigger — landing the Ezra Sullivan interview that no one else had ever gotten. Taylor, meanwhile, took Morgan's leaks and prepared to publish. Two journalists, one grudge, and a dead man's secrets flowing in both directions.

Skyler Iyer ran with his crew all evening — Quinn, Ezra, Cass — while a partnership between NeurAI and Skyler's CyberLife Robotics proceeded as planned. With Alex as CEO and the stolen code on the record, the question is who that partnership benefits now. Kai Andersen spent the party searching for Ashe and discovering that his art had been weaponized. Jamie kept notes from behind the bar, invisible enough to catch what everyone else missed.

And Sarah Blackwood. She already knew about Marcus's infidelity before the party — the paternity test arrived in the mail. What she discovered that night was the identity: Jess Kane, crying in a bathroom. Five people in that room thought Sarah was the killer. She and Jess were wheels-up to the Bahamas before the vote was finished.

What's Missing

What was on the rest of that thumb drive? Remi grabbed an entire drive from Marcus's secret living quarters. The code comparison was the headline. But a man who was running a memory extraction lab, a stolen drug pipeline, and a secret living space inside a warehouse almost certainly had more on that drive than one algorithm. The footnotes may change the story.

Morgan was accused, not convicted. And the margin between those two words is evidence of something this investigation hasn't resolved: whether the people who dismantled Marcus's empire did so because they wanted justice, or because they wanted it for themselves.

Morgan Reed stands accused by a margin of one. The man who built the financial architecture, traded secrets for protection, and armed Taylor Chase with a career-ending story. The vote says guilty. The margin says the room wasn't sure. This is the one crisis Morgan couldn't manage.

But the harder question isn't who killed Marcus Blackwood. It's why it took so long. The Stanford Four enabled him for twenty years. Vic bankrolled a company built on stolen code. Quinn kept providing a drug that was being used on human subjects. Sam bit his tongue and took another hit while Marcus confessed to theft. Everyone in that warehouse had a reason to want Marcus gone. Almost everyone had a reason to have acted sooner. They didn't, because Marcus was making money for everyone who kept quiet.

Here is what Marcus Blackwood built: he took Alex's code from BizAI and called it his own. He took Zia's Oracle Ledger and didn't even change the variable names. He took Cass's Synesthesia Engine without signing the contract. He blackmailed Quinn for the drug and recruited Sam to manufacture it. He drugged his own guests, ran stolen software through Kai's art installation, and extracted their memories onto tokens he could lock and trade. Then Skyler showed up demanding he load those memories into a robot. Each theft enabled the next. Each person who looked the other way made the next theft possible. That's not one man's crime. That's an ecosystem.

And when the ecosystem collapsed, nobody dismantled it. They used it. The same memory market that Marcus built to exploit people became a marketplace for the people who took him down. Alex got the CEO title and, if my read of the ledger is right, the corporate instincts to justify it. Vic walked out calculating. Morgan's name is on a verdict, not an indictment. And Sarah and Jess — who found each other in a bathroom and chose solidarity over rage — left the country with resources they didn't arrive with. I don't know whether that's justice or just the system working exactly as designed.

Somewhere, Skyler's prototype is still waiting for its memories. The investigation shut down the party. It didn't shut down the technology. Marcus is dead, but the infrastructure he built — stolen code, weaponized art, a drug pipeline, a memory market — survived him. It survived because everyone who helped tear it down also helped themselves to a piece of it on the way out. The machine doesn't need Marcus. Based on what I saw this morning, it never did.
