## LEDE

Nine votes for a chemist who never walked through the door. That was the verdict by the time I left the warehouse this morning. Quinn Sterling, blackmailed into making a drug, accused of killing the man who held the leash. The recordings that pointed his way arrived in Remi Whitman's hands nine minutes before the count. I watched them be passed. I did not see who pressed them into the bundle.

Marcus Blackwood is dead. A party last night, the kind he was famous for, and somewhere between the drinks and the installation art, somebody took his memories along with everyone else's and stopped his heart. The drug that emptied the warehouse was made by a chemist who was not in the room this morning to answer for any of it.

I was. I saw what came in and what got buried. The two are not the same story.

Quinn Sterling got an email on February 17. It came from Marcus's NeurAI address, and it was less an invitation to a party than a delivery instruction with the guest list as the leash. I read it this morning. The drug Quinn was being told to bring is the same drug that emptied a warehouse full of partygoers' heads four nights later. Marcus knew the side effects were inconsistent at higher doses. He wanted higher doses anyway.

## FOLLOW THE MONEY

$11,585,000 in evidence went to Blake's ledger this morning. Ten shell accounts received it. Three bore the first names of present voters. The remaining seven did not — including one that was swept out before the morning ended.

An account named Bebop took three transactions early — $1,050,000 in burials between 5:00 and 5:32 AM. At approximately 5:46 AM, those funds left Bebop and landed in an account called F Marcus. The transfer happened moments after Jess Kane and Sarah Blackwood were seen approaching Blake together. By the time the section closed, Bebop's column read zero.

F Marcus, $2,730,000 across seven transactions, takes the dead man's first name and prefixes it with an obscenity. I do not know who chose the name. I can tell you the account began receiving funds at 5:37 AM in a three-transaction batch and took the Bebop transfer at 5:46. Other tokens followed, from other operators, at 5:46, 5:54, and 6:00 AM. The companion account Marcus, $650,000 across three, sits beside it like an unsigned footnote.

Biku, $2,845,000 across nineteen transactions, is the largest account by volume and bears a pseudonym no present voter has volunteered for. Then come the named accounts. Sam at $1.71 million. Flip at $750,000. Jamie at $600,000. Three present voters who signed their own first names to the receipts and then sat in the deliberation that named Quinn. Ja, $1,375,000 across three large transactions, reads like the start of one of those names — though Jamie's column is already in her own first name, separately. The arithmetic is interesting. The certainty is not available to me.

The coalition spent the morning chasing the $20 million Vic committed to NeurAI and the stolen Oracle Ledger code. The eleven and a half million dollars that actually got buried went past them while they were arguing.

Remi Whitman made the case that put Quinn in the dock. He did it using a bundle handed to him nine minutes before the vote. I do not know whether it was conviction or convenience. I know it worked. ## WHAT'S MISSING

No exposed recording places Quinn Sterling at the party last night. Marcus put him on the guest list. The drug he was being blackmailed to make is what emptied the warehouse's heads. Whether he arrived, whether he was supposed to, I cannot tell you.

What I have instead is the recording Marcus left of his own protocol at 1:50 the night before. The extraction worked. The combinations that were supposed to lock the recordings did not. The technology Marcus had built to pull the partygoers' memories out of their heads also pulled the forensic record out of the room. By the time the warehouse emptied this morning, no one in it was equipped to remember the moments around the death.

`[Evidence card — mar004]`
**Headline:** 1:50 AM, the Chaos
**Owner:** Marcus Blackwood

I also cannot tell you what Kai chose not to hand to Remi. The brokers had five years of buried Marcus scoops between them. I saw one handoff. I did not see what stayed in the bag.

I cannot tell you what Sam buried for $1.71 million, or what Flip buried for $750,000, or what Jamie buried for $600,000. Account names, totals, transaction counts. That is the shape of what they sold. The contents are with Blake.

And I cannot tell you who decided Sarah Blackwood would be the next CEO of NeurAI, or when. Sources inside the company leaked the decision to Cassandra during the count. The succession was in motion before the room ever convened. The room never asked.

## CLOSING

The vote stands. Quinn Sterling is the room's named killer. The bundle that named him was real. The chemist behind it was not at the table to answer.

I am not going to pretend the verdict was wrong. I am going to tell you what else I saw. Three voters with their own first names on a ledger worth $3.06 million. A widow with a divorce filed at 2:08 the morning her husband died, an attorney at her side this morning, and a company waiting for Monday. A coalition with the loudest case in the room and the wrongest target. A stack of recordings I watched change hands nine minutes before the count. And no one named who was actually in the room.

Memory was the currency last night. Access to the recording was the currency this morning. Whoever decided what Remi heard before deliberation decided what the room could vote on. Surveillance capitalism in this story is not the drug, and it is not the company. It is the brokerage. The people who control the recordings control the verdict. The people who buried their secrets in their own first names are still going to work tomorrow.

The chemist takes the fall. The CEO takes the chair. The named accounts take the money home. I came to cover a murder. I am leaving with a succession plan and a receipt.
