A NeurAI Death Investigation

The Room Convicted Vic Kingsley of Killing Marcus Blackwood. His Widow Will Take Over the Company.

Eighteen of us spent the morning sorting a memory thief's sins into a verdict. We landed on the investor who wanted Marcus fired, 12 to 2, and buried $6.27 million on the way there.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 22, 2027 · Article NNA-062726-26

Marcus Blackwood died at his own party last night. This morning, eighteen of us gathered in the neon wreckage to pry the rest of his sins loose, memories pulled from engineers, investors, and guests who never agreed to give anything up. By the time the hands stopped going up, the room had a killer: Vic Kingsley, the venture capitalist who had already decided to fire Marcus, convicted 12 to 2.

The motive was airtight. The record is not. No recovered memory shows a killing, and the longer I sat with the morning, the less it seemed the room had been looking for one. I was in that room when the vote came down, and I have been turning it over ever since.

What the Memories Show

Start with what Marcus knew, because everyone in that room carried a piece of it. The memories we pulled back this morning tell the same story from a dozen angles. Marcus Blackwood built his companies on other people's work. Zia Bishara's 2023 ledger code, lifted whole and barely disguised into NeurAI's "proprietary" system. Alex Reeves's algorithm, the real engine behind the BizAI that Marcus sold for three hundred million. Cass Zhang's Synesthesia Engine, taken before a contract was ever signed.

◆ Zia's 2023 code beside NeurAI's 'proprietary' system. Identical.

That last theft came with a purpose I still cannot fully explain. Cass built a demo. Marcus did not wait to buy it. The memory shows why that matters.

Right: The last minute code MARCUS insisted KAI incorporate into their installation for tonight's party. Identical. Why would he want to do that? What's his agenda here?
Cass Zhang

Running stolen code on a room full of people who never agreed to be test subjects. Nobody in our investigation could say why. But somebody had already decided Marcus was a problem that needed solving, and he decided it early. Vic Kingsley, the investor whose firm bankrolled NeurAI, opened the night doing the cold arithmetic no founder wants done about him.

Something Has to Be Done
VIC.1 - 8:23PM - First thing first: meet with MORGAN. You both have deep concerns about MARCUS. He's gotten sloppy, missed deadlines, made too many promises he can't back up. What's more, there's evidence he's also been breaking the law in his race to beat his competitors to the finish line. Business dealings in Silicon Valley can get a little messy sometimes, but you don't trust MARCUS to tow the line anymore. He's lied to your face too many times, and you're NOT risking jail time for criminal culpability. Something has to be done.
Vic Kingsley

Read the last line twice. Not risking jail time for criminal culpability. That sentence is the case the room built this morning. Marcus's crimes were not only Marcus's problem. They were Vic's exposure, and Vic said so out loud, hours before anyone died.

Marcus's removal was already in motion, from more than one direction. Weeks earlier, Alex Reeves's lawyers had put the demand in writing: strip Marcus of the company he ran and install a neutral party as its interim chief executive. Vic had made his own quiet decision to push him out. By the night Marcus died, more than one person had already drafted his exit.

Sarah Blackwood arrived with a different wound. Texts recovered from Riley Torres's phone show the decision already made: she had thrown Marcus out and come to confront him about something she would not name. What the memories add is the cost of it. Marcus had quietly emptied their shared account, her lawyer Mel Nilsson realized, and planned to leave his wife with nothing. Then her friends went to war for her. Riley Torres had spent the night pulling a thread that ran straight into Marcus's shell companies, and she handed the whole thing away.

The Case Riley Gave Away
MEL.3 - 11:09PM - You thought you had a solid case with the paternity test and evidence of MARCUS's infidelity. But then RILEY revealed their cards: secret bank accounts, convoluted shell companies, environmental destruction, evidence of insider trading with someone pretty high up in the government... You stare, gobsmacked. This could be a career-making case for RILEY, but instead, they're giving it all to you so that you can use it to protect SARAH. You won't let them down. MARCUS has no idea what's coming.
Mel Nilsson

Riley gave away the case of her career to shield her best friend, and asked for nothing back. Hold onto that. It reads as pure loyalty here. It reads differently once you follow the money.

Then there is Jess Kane. She loved Marcus, and this morning, in an argument with Sarah that half the room heard, she said it without flinching: "He hated you. He loved me." The memories carry a prenatal paternity test naming Marcus the father of her child, a pregnancy he never claimed.

◆ Probability of paternity: 99.85 percent.

And then the detail that turns a love affair into one more count of the same crime. Marcus ran his memory-extraction drug on Jess herself. He told her it was an acting gig, that she should play a butler. She trusted him, took the dose, and let him do it.

An Acting Gig
JESS.3 - 11:04PM - You approach your friend NAT and tell them you know why MARCUS is interested in Psychotrophin-3A. He uses it as part of his "Memory Extraction Protocol." Said it was an experimental NeurAI research he wasn't ready to go public with. You know about it because he tried it on you once. Said it was an acting gig - that you should play a butler. You wanted to help him, so you took the drugs and let him run his experiment. He was ELATED. Showed you a token. Said your memory was stored on it somehow.
Jess Kane

The room did not sit with that for long. When someone floated Jess's pregnancy as a motive for Sarah this morning, Sarah swatted it away without blinking: "What would I get out of that? I'd much rather collect alimony for the rest of my life." Mel nodded along. Two sentences, and the woman carrying Marcus's child went from a person back to a data point.

Five motives, one body, and this morning the room weighed every one. The first count split eight votes toward a Vic-and-Morgan collusion, four toward Sarah for the cheating, one lonely vote for Skyler Iyer as a drugged-out wildcard, and Jess, alone, insisting no one had killed Marcus at all. Then they pulled Vic and Morgan apart, and the collusion theory fell over. Morgan Reed drew zero. Vic held everything. The field came down to two names, Vic and Sarah, and the final count was 12 to 2.

Which would be the end of it, except for the theory almost nobody wanted to finish. Marcus was drugged all night, on a compound he had strong-armed out of a chemist at doses no safety test had ever cleared.

Go Up on the Dosage
To: quinn.k.sterling@gmail.com From: marcus.blackwood@neurai.co Date: February 17, 2027 Subject: Re: the latest trial I need you to bring me another batch of Psychotrophin-3A. The ingestion method you established with the last trial is effective, but the side effects I am looking for are totally inconsistent. I know you're resistant because you didn't test levels anywhere near this high during the PT-3B safety assessments, but I'm certain we should go up on the dosage. And you can't afford to say no, can you? I'm having a party on the 21st. I'll put you on the guest list. Come find me there. Anything you want to tell me about it, you can tell me in person.
Quinn Sterling

Marcus wrote that to Quinn Sterling four days before the party. You cannot afford to say no, can you? And he was not only poisoning himself. Skyler Iyer, whose robotics company needed Marcus's memory tech, spent the small hours pushing him to boot an untested prototype on captured human memories. What's the worst that could happen, Skyler asked him. Marcus, for once, was the one urging caution. A man that drugged and that reckless might not have needed anyone to kill him. The room had that possibility in front of it this morning and set it aside. Not because it clears Vic. Because a room actually hunting for the truth would have picked it up.

Follow the Money

Blake walked our room through the ledger this morning, and by the end the numbers told a story the vote did not. Ten accounts. $6.27 million paid out to bury memories where no one will read them again.

The largest silence carries Sarah's name. $2.98 million, thirteen memories sealed, under the woman who spent the deliberation insisting she had nothing to hide. To her credit, she stayed transparent about it. She told us she had last seen the balance nearer a million, that someone else had moved money in, and the fingerprints on those transfers back her up. She was not the only hand on that account. It is still her name on it. Both things are true, and the paradox just gets to walk out the door.

Everyone else's money points somewhere too. Hydroflask, a flat million under a name no one at the table claims. $825,000 on an account bearing Morgan Reed's name, the same Morgan whose alliance with Vic sat at the center of the case the room tried to build. And Vic? The man the room convicted is the one name missing from all ten accounts. The money followed everyone except the accused.

As I file this, word lands that Sarah Blackwood will step in as NeurAI's interim chief executive while the company steadies itself. The widow, the largest account, and now the corner office.

The Players

Sorting who helped from who did not means separating what people brought into the light from what they paid to keep dark. Some did both before lunch.

The truth about Marcus came, mostly, from the people he robbed. Remi Whitman turned Sam Thorne's laptop toward Vic and Alex Reeves and let the stolen code testify for itself. Cass Zhang and Kai Andersen matched the Synesthesia Engine line by line. Quinn Sterling, blackmailed into brewing the drug, let that surface too. None of their hands were clean. Remi wanted Vic's attention as much as she wanted justice. The evidence was true regardless.

"Out on the sidewalk with your designer scarf pressed against Marcus's nose, about to ruin his life."

Vic Kingsley

That is Vic's own memory, exposed this morning, not pried out of anyone else. His candor is a large part of what convicted him. Two people in that room read the same record and still voted no.

Others reached for the ledger instead, and I won't pretend to know who. Blake's columns name who got paid, not who walked up to bury a memory, and the two are not the same. What I can tell you is who was working the room while the accounts filled. Sam Thorne, who makes the drugs Marcus lived on, turns out to be the one who mailed the paternity test to Sarah in the first place, a warning to a friend she could not give out loud. And Jamie Woods, the bartender, caught half of what I have reported here from behind the rail. The envelope of cash Marcus pressed on a construction worker he kept in his pocket. The sidewalk deal after the fistfight, where a VC told the man who had thrown the punch, "He's out. You're in."

And then the thread I cannot shake, because it is mine too. Ashe Motoko had the story about Marcus first, the real one, and it cost her the job. A fellow reporter, Taylor Chase, buried her exposé as a favor to Marcus and took the NeurAI exclusive as his reward. I heard his own account of it this morning.

"They had the receipts on Marcus. But they never understood: the story's not about the truth. And I did get the exclusive out of it."

— Taylor Chase

Not every alliance made sense. At one point this morning Sam, Remi, and Vic, the drug maker, the rival Marcus had shafted, and the investor cutting him loose, went behind Marcus's bedroom door and shut it. Whatever they were settling, they settled it together. I am not here to judge what anyone buried.

What's Missing

Here is the part I cannot hand you. I can show you the shape of the silence. I cannot show you what is inside it. Thirteen memories are sealed under Sarah's name. Six more under Hydroflask, someone who chose to be no one. I know what they cost. I have no idea what they held.

The record keeps other holes the vote never closed. Who slipped that paternity test under the lab door in the first place, before Sam ever mailed it on. Whether Cass's stolen engine actually ran on the people dancing in that room, and what it did to them. Who besides Sarah could move Marcus's money. And the memory Marcus pulled out of Jess's head, the one she can watch on a screen but cannot remember living. That one may already be gone for good. Nobody ever squared the overdose against the verdict, either. The room just picked one and went home.

What It Means

The room convicted Vic Kingsley, and I will not tell you they found the wrong man. He had more to lose than anyone if Marcus's crimes reached daylight, he had said as much before Marcus died, and his own recovered memory did the rest. If someone in that warehouse had a hand in this, Vic is a reasonable place to look.

But the harder question is what naming him will actually cost him, and the honest answer is: likely not much. There will be an investigation now. The police were minutes out when the room scattered, and Blake was already telling Vic to leave the country. But Vic is a power broker with a fund's worth of lawyers and a lifetime of making inconvenient things disappear. An accusation is not a conviction, and men with his resources are practiced at keeping the two far apart. Ashe Motoko had the truth about Marcus in writing, and it ended her career, not his; the charge against Vic may travel the same road, for the same reason. A verdict the accused can afford to outlast is not justice. It is paperwork.

Because the morning was never really about who killed Marcus. It was about who gets what he left behind. His death cracked open a structure Vic sat near the top of, and by the time we filed out, the pieces had quietly rearranged themselves. That demand Alex's lawyers made weeks ago, Marcus out and a neutral party in, came true on its own this morning while we argued over the killing. Sarah Blackwood, whose name sits over the largest silence on Blake's ledger, takes the office. The letter asked for a neutral party. It got the widow. $6.27 million in memory went into the ground, and everyone with a place in the new order walked out holding something.

Everyone except the people it was supposedly for. Somewhere in that neon room are guests who let Marcus mine their memories on a promise and may never get back what he took. Jess Kane most of all. She loved him. She carried the child he never claimed. She let him wipe a memory out of her own head and call it an acting gig. This morning she watched the room wave her pain away in a sentence about alimony, and, as I write this, she has checked herself into an inpatient program to grieve the man who did all of it. There was no seat for her in the reorganization. The room found its killer and the company found its chief executive, and the one person Marcus hurt past repair is counting the cost alone.

"The room was never really deciding who killed Marcus Blackwood. It was deciding who inherits what he built on other people's backs, and the ones he built it on walked away with nothing."
