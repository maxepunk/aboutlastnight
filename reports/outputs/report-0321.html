Everyone Agreed Until They Realized What It Meant.

Marcus Blackwood built NeurAI on stolen code, stolen memories, and experimental drugs. Eighteen investigators started with self-harm. They ended with the one name that didn't require anyone present to take responsibility. Inside the party, the pivot, and the $7.6 million in silence.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-0321-26

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Eighteen people were at his party last night. This morning, they woke up with holes in their memories, sat down in a room together, and tried to figure out who killed him. I was there for that part. One person wasn't. Zia Bishara, the developer whose algorithm Marcus stole to build NeurAI's core technology, vanished before anyone thought to ask where they went.

The extracted memories tell a story of a man who drugged his own friends, stole their thoughts, and called it innovation. The group that gathered this morning started in the same place: self-harm. Drug use. Business failure. A man whose stolen empire was collapsing under its own weight. That was the consensus. Until they followed the thread and realized what self-harm actually meant. If the drugs killed Marcus, then Sam supplied them. If the compound was dangerous, then Quinn manufactured it. The self-harm theory didn't exonerate anyone. It implicated the people still in the room. I watched the moment that landed. The whiteboard spelled it out: "Self Harm? Implicate Quinn? Sam?" And that's when the conversation shifted. Ezra Sullivan pointed at the empty chair. Zia, who wasn't there to defend themselves, became the answer that didn't require anyone present to take responsibility.

Here is what I can tell you. Here is the shape of what was buried. Here is the $7.6 million worth of silence that somebody purchased this morning while the rest of us were still trying to understand what Marcus built, who he destroyed, and whether the machine that killed him was pointed outward or aimed squarely at himself.

The Story

Start with the theft. Everything else grows from it. Zia Bishara developed the Oracle Ledger, an algorithm so elegant that Marcus Blackwood showed up at their doorstep one night, out of his mind, begging to borrow it. A memory from that evening captures the visit: Marcus was "zonked," seeing unicorns, desperate. He said it was a make-or-break moment. Zia relented. Ezra Sullivan had taught them to collaborate. That generosity became the foundation of a $30 million fraud.

The Doorstep Visit
ZIA.1 - 9:18PM - You knew to start worrying when MARCUS stopped answering your calls. He ghosted you right after he showed up at your doorstep UNANNOUNCED one night, begging for your help. The man was ZONKED out of his mind. He must have been seeing unicorns. He wanted to "borrow" your Oracle Ledger algorithm for a demo with CyberLife Robotics. Said it was a make-or-break moment for him. And you relented, because MARCUS has helped you out of a jam before, and EZRA taught you to collaborate. Maybe EZRA can teach you what to do about Marcus now.
Zia Bishara

By 12:31 last night, Zia had proof. Remi Whitman handed over a laptop, and Zia ran the code comparison. Left side: their Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. Right side: NeurAI's "proprietary" system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments in the margins. Marcus didn't even bother changing Zia's notes.

But here's the thing about that doorstep visit. Marcus wasn't just desperate for code. He was high. And the drugs weren't recreational. While Zia was lending an algorithm to a friend, Morgan Reed and Vic Kingsley were already meeting to discuss what Morgan's memory calls their "shared concerns about Marcus's recent erratic behavior." That was 8:23 PM, the earliest recovered memory of the evening. Both suspected a drug habit. Both knew his business dealings weren't above board. Both fell silent when the question became: what do we do about it?

The Liability Meeting
MORGAN.1 - 8:23PM - You meet VIC to discuss your shared concerns about MARCUS's recent erratic behavior. He can't be trusted. You both suspect he's developed a drug habit. And you have secret information that his business dealings are not all above board. Both your reputations rely too heavily on MARCUS to let him continue on unchecked. Something has to be done, and soon. But what? You both fall silent.
Morgan Reed

Something has to be done. Remember those words. They'll matter later.

Because while Morgan and Vic were strategizing, Nat Francisco's camera was already rolling. Nat's earliest memory isn't from last night's party at all. It's older. Stanford. Glow paint on everything. Mel Nilsson doing a backflip off the speakers. Sam Thorne laughing, swearing they'd never sell out and work for "the man." And Marcus, grabbing Nat's shoulder: "We're going to change the world, Nat. All four of us." The footage is still on a hard drive somewhere. Nat can't watch it anymore. That Marcus doesn't exist.

Four hours after that silent meeting with Vic, Morgan flipped. Taylor Chase's memory captures it: 12:40 AM, recorder running, Morgan spilling everything. Shell companies. Insider trading. Dangerous experiments on unwitting test subjects. Lying under oath. Taylor called it "journalistic gold." Morgan wanted Marcus destroyed. His reputation, his business, all of it. The fixer had found a new client: themselves.

The Fixer Flips
TAYLOR.4 - 12:40AM - You offered MORGAN a chance to spill about anything, as long as it was newsworthy. They want your help taking MARCUS down. His reputation? In shambles. His business? Ruined. And what they've told you so far is journalistic gold. Hiding business income in shell companies, soliciting insider trading tips, performing dangerous experiments on unwitting test subjects, lying under oath... It's too good to pass up. You're ready to put your journalistic reputation on the line to get this incredible story out there.
Taylor Chase

Now follow the thread backward. Morgan knew about the experiments. But what experiments? Jess Kane knew. She told Nat Francisco at 11:04 PM. Marcus had used Psychotrophin-3A, a compound Quinn Sterling created and tried to destroy, as part of something he called a "Memory Extraction Protocol." He'd tested it on Jess first. Told her it was an acting gig. Play a butler, take some drugs, let him run an experiment. She wanted to help him. He was elated. Showed her a physical object afterward and said her memory was stored on it somehow. Her own memory of the experience was wiped in the process.

The Test Subject
JESS.3 - 11:04PM - You approach your friend NAT and tell them you know why MARCUS is interested in Psychotrophin-3A. He uses it as part of his "Memory Extraction Protocol." Said it was an experimental NeurAI research he wasn't ready to go public with. You know about it because he tried it on you once. Said it was an acting gig - that you should play a butler. You wanted to help him, so you took the drugs and let him run his experiment. He was ELATED. Showed you a token. Said your memory was stored on it somehow.
Jess Kane

Said it was an acting gig. Let that sit. Marcus Blackwood told the mother of his child to play a butler, drugged her, extracted her memories, and called it research. Then he scaled it.

By 1:50 AM, Marcus had drugged his entire party. His own memory, recovered from that final hour, is the most damning document I've seen in months of investigating NeurAI. The extractions worked. The instructions were clear. But his drugged subjects couldn't follow commands. "SAM, take this token. Lock the..." Sam Thorne wandered off mid-sentence. Sarah Blackwood was staring at a wall, tears streaming. Marcus called them "drugged zombies." His own word. These were his friends.

The Harvest
MARCUS.4 - 1:50AM - The extractions worked. The instructions were clear: "Your memories are now in the tokens. Secure them. Combinations only you know. Protect them however you can." Simple. Now you just need these drugged zombies to LOCK them. "SAM - take this token. Lock the - ." They wander off mid-command. SARAH's staring at a wall, tears streaming. This is chaos. You don't think ANYONE has the right combinations anymore. Drugged people make terrible assistants.
Marcus Blackwood

And now the Stanford memory snaps into focus. "We're going to change the world, Nat. All four of us." That's the same voice commanding drugged people to lock away their own stolen thoughts. The world changed, all right. By 12:50 AM, Nat, Sam, and Jess had gathered to reckon with what Marcus had become. The AI underpinning NeurAI didn't work. It never worked. Instead of fixing it, Marcus had turned to drugs and memory theft. His high-pressure deal with CyberLife Robotics pushed him over the edge.

But Marcus wasn't just stealing memories. He was stealing code. Cass discovered at 12:38 AM that the Synesthesia Engine, their own creation, had been secretly embedded into Kai Andersen's party installation without payment or permission. The Oracle Ledger was Zia's. The Synesthesia Engine was Cass's. The memories were everyone's. NeurAI was a house of stolen things, and the CyberLife deal was the wind that would blow it down.

Quinn Sterling carried the heaviest secret. The compound that made the extractions possible, Psychotrophin-3A, was Quinn's creation. They'd tried to destroy it. Wiped their research, dissolved their business partnership with Zia and Cass, lost friendships in the process. Then Marcus reached out with knowledge of the drug he shouldn't have had. Quinn confessed everything to Ezra Sullivan at midnight, trying to explain three times before Ezra would take off the rose-colored glasses and listen. Quinn was terrified of exposure on both sides.

And then there was Sarah Blackwood. At 1:36 AM, she found Jess Kane crying in the bathroom and put it together. Marcus's baby was Jess's. Sarah's reaction wasn't outrage. It was resignation. "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do." This morning, during the investigation, Sarah, Jess, and Mel Nilsson discovered they all had relationships with Marcus in a confessional moment in what had been his drug lab. I watched that happen. Three women, bound together by one man's damage, choosing each other instead of him.

Riley Torres had been building toward this for years. At 10:12 PM, Riley confronted Morgan Reed about the shell companies and insider trading. Morgan didn't even try to deny it. Riley sacrificed a career-making case to funnel everything to Mel, who was building Sarah's divorce filing. By 11:09, Mel was staring at Riley's evidence, gobsmacked. Secret bank accounts. Environmental destruction. Insider trading with someone high up in government. Riley gave it all away to protect Sarah. Marcus had no idea what was coming. He didn't live to find out.

The Reckoning
NAT.4 - 12:50AM - You, NAT and SAM gather together to talk about what to do with MARCUS. You've tried to convince those two to stop enabling him for years. But at this point, he might be too far gone. Now you're learning that he's lied about the AI that underpins his company. It doesn't work. But instead of fixing it, he's taking drugs and inventing techniques to steal people's memories instead? Maybe his high pressure deal with CyberLife pushed him over the edge and into this weird, out-of-the-box thinking....
Nat Francisco

The AI doesn't work. The code is stolen. The drugs are real. The memories are taken. Every thread converges on that room at 12:50 AM, where the people who loved Marcus longest finally admitted he was too far gone. Somewhere between that admission and dawn, Marcus Blackwood died.

Follow the Money

Eleven shell accounts. Thirty-eight extracted memories purchased and buried. $7,605,000 in total. That's what silence cost this morning. The party extracted memories last night. The investigation re-buried them today. The suppression operation didn't end when Marcus died. It accelerated.

The largest account, Cobrakai, received $2,080,000 across eight separate purchases. That's not impulse. That's a campaign. Someone ran a systematic burial operation while the rest of us were still sorting through evidence. Morgan's sprint toward Blake in the final minutes, detailed above, wasn't the only suspicious timing. The entire network shut down moments after.

Elonisshit. $1,530,000 across five purchases. The name reads like a statement, contempt for tech moguls or contempt for one tech mogul in particular. Motherofmen appeared on the burial roster and immediately started accumulating: $1,305,000, five purchases. Laverne was the most persistent, seven separate purchases totaling $1,005,000. Someone kept returning to Blake, again and again.

Then there are the accounts that tell different stories. $125,000 went to an account bearing Remi's name. One purchase. Whether Remi set it up or someone else directed the payment there, that's a real identity attached to real money. $30,000 went to an account called Jamie volt. The smallest payout on the ledger. An account bearing a real name doesn't tell you who walked up to Blake with the memory. It tells you where Blake sent the payment.

Zia Bishara wasn't here this morning. Couldn't bury anything, couldn't expose anything. But the stolen code underpinning NeurAI represents the financial foundation of everything: a $30 million investment built on an algorithm Marcus took from Zia's doorstep. The money trail doesn't start with shell accounts. It starts with theft.

The Players

Nat Francisco exposed four memories. Every one of them a chapter in the Stanford Four's disintegration, from the glow-paint promise to the final reckoning. Nat didn't just bring evidence this morning. Nat brought a documentary. The filmmaker who once captured Marcus's dream now captured his unraveling and chose to put every frame on the record. Ashe Motoko and Taylor Chase went on record alongside Nat as a unified group, working through locked evidence together all morning. I watched them operate. Methodical, collaborative, relentless.

Riley Torres sacrificed a career-making legal case to protect Sarah Blackwood. The memory of Riley confronting Morgan about shell companies reveals years of research handed over to Mel Nilsson for the divorce case. Then this morning, Riley chased Morgan through the investigation space when memories started disappearing toward Blake. The loyal friend became the last line of defense.

Morgan Reed's arc from insider to informant happened in real time last night: conspiring with Vic Kingsley at 8:23 PM, flipping to Taylor Chase by 12:40 AM. But this morning, Morgan was impersonating "Phil Kowalski" to obtain drugs from Sam Thorne, and by the end was racing toward Blake with someone else's memories. The fixer was still fixing. The question is who the final client was.

Sarah Blackwood found solidarity with Jess Kane at 1:36 AM. "That baby deserves better. We both do." Then during the investigation, Sarah accused Jess. I need you to hold both of those facts at once. Grief and solidarity aren't mutually exclusive. Neither are love and suspicion. Ezra Sullivan was the one who offered the pivot. When the self-harm consensus started implicating people in the room, Ezra pointed to the empty chair. Zia wasn't here. Zia had been wronged. Zia had fled. It was a cleaner story than the one that implicated Sam and Quinn. But Ezra had spent the party trying to mediate Zia's code theft complaint, first with optimism, then with growing horror. The mentor who couldn't protect the student helped redirect the investigation toward them.

Vic Kingsley discovered the secret entrance to Marcus's hidden bachelor pad and workshop this morning. Alex Reeves was rage-texting Vic the night before about stolen code, demanding Marcus be removed from NeurAI. Ashe Motoko accused Alex during the investigation. Skyler Iyer watched Cass pitch the Synesthesia Engine at 11:19 PM while Marcus and Morgan argued across the room. Jamie "Volt" Woods had extensive knowledge throughout both the party and investigation, but their actions remain unclear. Flip owed Marcus for a $30,000 gambling debt. Phil Kowalski — that's Flip's real name, the construction worker identity they keep hidden behind the 'party architect' persona. When Morgan impersonated that name to deceive Sam, Morgan didn't just use a fake identity. They stole Flip's most guarded secret. Flip spent the late hours taking whatever Sam offered, letting the drugs smooth the edges of imposter syndrome that runs deeper than anyone at that party knew. Kai Andersen wandered the party searching for Ashe, stumbling into the discovery that Marcus had stolen Cass's code for the installation.

Quinn Sterling carried the weight of Psychotrophin-3A's creation. The compound Quinn tried to destroy became Marcus's weapon. I overheard Quinn and Cass talking about drug production this morning. That knowledge, that your invention became the thing that hurt everyone in that room, is its own kind of prison.

What's Missing

Zia Bishara isn't here. That became the foundation of the accusation after the self-harm theory proved too uncomfortable: motive and absence. The code comparison happened at 12:31 AM. Marcus died sometime after. What happened in between? Zia had proof of theft, but no recovered memory covers what they did next. Did Zia leave because they killed Marcus, or because they discovered the full scope of what he'd stolen and couldn't stomach staying? The answer lives in the gap between midnight and morning, and nobody in that room this morning could fill it.

Morgan was sprinting toward Blake with extracted memories in the final moments before the network shut down. Riley was chasing them. What was Morgan trying to bury? Morgan knew about the shell companies, the insider trading, the experiments. Which of those secrets was worth a last-minute sprint? And what about Marcus himself? The whiteboard asked the question everyone answered first and then backed away from: "Self Harm?" connected to Quinn and Sam. Marcus was self-experimenting with Psychotrophin-3A. He was drugging himself and his friends. If the drugs killed him, was it an accident, a calculated risk, or something he saw coming?

Thirty-eight extracted memories were buried this morning. $7.6 million changed hands. Blake's network now possesses both the memories extracted at the party and the evidence of how the extraction worked. The party was designed to harvest. The investigation's black market was designed to re-bury. Who benefits from that symmetry? I can tell you the shape of the silence. I can tell you it has eleven names and a price tag. I cannot tell you what's inside it.

The group accused Zia Bishara. But that wasn't where they started. They started with self-harm. Drug use. Business failure. A man who built a collapsing empire on stolen code and experimental drugs. That was the consensus until the room realized what it meant: if the drugs killed Marcus, then Sam and Quinn were implicated. The self-harm theory pointed inward. Zia pointed outward. Absent. Wronged. Gone before morning. A cleaner story. I report the accusation because it's what the investigation concluded. But the first conclusion, the one they talked themselves out of, may have been closer to the truth.

Zia built an algorithm and lent it to a friend. That's how extraction starts. Not with malice. With trust. Quinn synthesized a compound and tried to destroy it. Marcus found it anyway. That's how extraction scales. Not by invention. By leverage. Jess was told it was an acting gig. She wanted to help someone she loved. That's how extraction finds its subjects. Not by force. By asking nicely.

Cass built a perception engine for creative exploration. Marcus deployed it on a room full of people without their knowledge. Nat filmed four friends who believed they'd change the world. Twenty years later, one of them was commanding drugged people to lock away their own stolen thoughts. Sam made the drugs. Mel made the legal case. Riley gave up a career. Sarah found the woman carrying her husband's child and chose grace over rage. Every one of them was trying to do something human. Build something. Protect someone. Trust someone. And every one of those human impulses became raw material for the machine Marcus built.

That's the system. Not one predator. A thousand acts of trust, each one converted into a product. Your collaboration becomes someone's proprietary code. Your generosity becomes someone's test case. Your memories become someone's leverage. Marcus Blackwood didn't invent this. He just stopped pretending it was anything else.

Thirty-eight extracted memories are locked in Blake's vault. $7.6 million in purchased silence. The technology works. Psychotrophin-3A, the Synesthesia Engine, the Oracle Ledger — they're all still out there. And the next person who picks them up won't be as sloppy as Marcus. They'll have better code. Better funding. Better PR. They won't drug people at a party. They'll build an app, write a terms of service nobody reads, and call it convenience.

I was in that room this morning. I watched eighteen people try to piece together what was taken from them. They're still not sure what they lost. That's the part that should frighten you. Not that someone stole their memories. That they can't tell what's missing.
