Marcus Blackwood Is Dead. His Memory Machine Is Just Getting Started.

A researcher blackmailed into making dangerous drugs. A shadow investigation built on a borrowed laptop. A venture capitalist who saw the fraud and chose silence. Fifteen people attended the party. Five stayed for the investigation. $1.7 million bought the rest.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-0327-26

Marcus Blackwood is dead. The CEO of NeurAI, found lifeless in the warehouse he'd converted into a private laboratory, a place none of his fifteen partygoers were supposed to know existed. By morning, their memories had been extracted, locked, and scattered like evidence across a crime scene that Marcus himself had engineered. I started receiving tips from inside that warehouse before sunrise. I expected a story about a tech company cutting corners. What my sources sent me was something worse.

Five people stayed for the investigation this morning: Sarah Blackwood, Vic Kingsley, Remi Whitman, Sam Thorne, and Alex Reeves. They accused Quinn Sterling, the researcher Marcus had been blackmailing for months over a dangerous drug called Psychotrophin-3A. But the investigation that produced that accusation was shaped by forces the group may not have fully understood. Two parallel investigations running in the dark. A venture capitalist purchasing silence under her own name. And $1.7 million in secrets acquired by Blake's network before the police arrived.

Quinn Sterling had motive and means. But Quinn's name appears nowhere on the financial ledger. No shell accounts. No attempt to suppress the blackmail evidence. The people who did spend money this morning weren't hiding a murder. They were hiding an empire.

The Story

Start with the blackmail, because that's where the group started. A recovered memory from 9:59 PM last night shows Quinn Sterling under Marcus Blackwood's thumb. Marcus had discovered Quinn's off-the-books research into Psychotrophin-3A, a drug with dangerous neural plasticity side effects, and he wanted more of it. Not to study. To use.

The Blackmail
QUINN.2 - 9:59PM - Somehow, MARCUS found out about the unusual side effects of Psychotrophin3A, and is obsessed with its ability to induce neural plasticity in large doses. Why did you bury the PT-3A safety assessments? Now he's got you by the balls and is blackmailing you to continue with your experiments to develop it. This drug is dangerous, and should not be in the wrong hands. But MARCUS wants you to bring him another batch of it tonight. You're to meet him at the bar at 10.
Quinn Sterling

Two minutes later, a separate memory confirms the handoff happened. Jamie "Volt" Woods was trying to have a smoke near the service door when Marcus walked out with a stranger in a lab coat. The stranger warned him. Marcus didn't listen.

The Handoff
JAMIE.2 - 10:01PM - Voices behind the service door. Then MARCUS enters with a stranger wearing a lab coat. STRANGER: "Ingesting extracts—untested. Dangerous." MARCUS: "I can handle it." Envelope exchanges. Stranger leaves. Then MARCUS pops some pills and stares vacantly at the bar for a while. Looks scared. Rich people and their top shelf habits. Not your problem.
Jamie "Volt" Woods

Marcus took the pills anyway. He looked scared. That's the detail that stays with me. Not the arrogance, not the envelope exchange, but the fear on a man's face right before he swallowed something he'd been told was dangerous. The blackmail gave Quinn Sterling a motive to kill Marcus Blackwood. But before I got to that conclusion, someone else was already running a very different investigation.

At 9:37 PM, thirty minutes before Marcus met Quinn at the bar, Remi Whitman followed Marcus through a secret door in the warehouse. The memory shows Marcus so high he could barely form sentences. He smiled when he saw Remi in his private space. Welcomed them in. Told them to help themselves to anything. Remi took a thumb drive and left. That's the kind of invitation you only get from someone who's lost the ability to understand consequences.

Through the Secret Door
REMI.2 - 9:37PM - You saw him go through the secret door, and you didn't think. You just followed him. It looks like he's taken over the other half of this warehouse to live in. You're not sure why he's not at home with his wife, and don't care. You need to talk to him about the funding discrepancy. But the man is SO HIGH that he can barely string sentences together. He smiles when he sees you in his space. Welcomes you in. Tells you to help yourself to anything. So you do. You grab a thumb drive and leave. Now you need to find Alex.
Remi Whitman

Emails between Remi Whitman and Alex Reeves confirm this wasn't spontaneous. Remi came to that party with a plan. They wanted to investigate Marcus over a funding discrepancy at NeurAI, and they'd coordinated with Alex beforehand. The thumb drive was step one. But Remi needed a computer to read what was on it, and that's where Sam Thorne entered the story without meaning to.

A memory from 11:17 PM captures Sam Thorne handing over a laptop without a second thought. Remi came at Sam looking desperate, asking for a computer. Sam was in a daze, distracted by worry about Marcus, and handed it over. Then Remi asked if Sam knew any hackers. Sam texted Zia Bishara. Just like that, Remi had the laptop, the thumb drive, and a hacker. But Sam's memory carries a detail that gnaws at me.

The Laptop Handoff
SAM.3 - 11:17PM - REMI comes at you, looking so desperate, asking for a computer. You're in such a daze, thinking about MARCUS, that you hand over your laptop without pausing to consider how many secrets are on it. All they would have to do is open up your client spreadsheet and you'd be screwed. But they have it now. You'll just have to hope they're not the snooping sort. Then they ask if you know any hackers. So you pull out your phone and text ZIA.
Sam Thorne

"All they would have to do is open up your client spreadsheet and you'd be screwed." Sam knew the risk. Handed it over anyway. That carelessness, or generosity, or grief, whatever you want to call it, became the investigation's most important break. Because Remi didn't open Sam's spreadsheet. Remi opened something much worse for Marcus Blackwood.

But before I tell you what Remi found, I need to tell you about Sam Thorne's night. Earlier that evening, a memory from Jess Kane captures Sam talking to Nat Francisco about Psychotrophin-3A in raised voices. Sam was worried Marcus was experimenting on himself with it. Jess, overhearing this, knew for a fact that he was. That concern about the drug connects Sam to the same thread that the group would eventually use to accuse Quinn Sterling. Sam was tracking the danger before anyone else named it.

My sources on the ground tell me Sam operated solo all morning. Unlocking extracted memories, turning them all over publicly, spending every available minute before the police arrived trying to crack open more evidence. Sam's financial footprint on the ledger is exactly zero. In a room where $1.7 million changed hands to suppress the truth, Sam's hands stayed empty. Remember that. Because someone in that room wanted Sam to take the fall for Marcus's death.

Now. The laptop. At 12:31 AM, Zia Bishara punched in the default password for something called the Oracle Ledger, and it worked. What came up on the screen was a code comparison. On the left: Zia's own Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. On the right: NeurAI's so-called proprietary system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. Marcus hadn't even bothered changing Zia's notes. He didn't steal code cleverly. He copied it like a student plagiarizing a term paper.

At 1:08 AM, Remi turned Sam's laptop around so that Vic Kingsley and Alex Reeves could both see it. This is the moment where every thread in this story collides. Remi's shadow investigation. Sam's unwitting contribution. The stolen code that built NeurAI. And the investor who'd staked her reputation on it.

The Fraud Exposed
REMI.4 - 1:08AM - You turn SAM's laptop around so that VIC and ALEX can both see the code comparison. On the left: ZIA's Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. On the right: NeurAI's 'proprietary' system from 2027. the data is identical. MARCUS didn't even bother changing the notes. He's not just a thief, he's a sloppy thief. Next you roll the demo. That's even sloppier. Maybe now VIC will stop prioritizing NeurAI and give you the time of day.
Remi Whitman

Vic Kingsley's own memory of that moment fills in what Remi couldn't see. At first, the demo looked familiar. It looped perfectly. But Alex Reeves pointed out what Vic wasn't seeing. The code didn't match. Alex snatched the laptop. What they found was disastrous. Marcus's "breakthrough AI" needed manual fixes for 90% of the inputs. Put another way, it was worthless. Vic stood there, dumbfounded. The biggest investment in her portfolio had just evaporated in front of her eyes.

The Moment It Collapsed
VIC004 - 1:08AM - REMI approaches you and ALEX with a laptop in hand, looking like a smug son of a bitch. At first, what you see looks familiar. The demo loops perfectly. But then ALEX points out what you're not seeing. The code doesn't match. The demo's fake. ALEX snatches the laptop to investigate while you stand there, dumbfounded. What they find is disastrous. MARCUS's "breakthrough AI" that he's been crowing about for years needs manual fixes for 90% of the inputs. Put another way... it's worthless.
Vic Kingsley

Remi wanted Vic's attention. They got it. The question is what Vic did with what she saw. Because the woman who discovered that her biggest investment was built on stolen code and fake demos didn't blow the whistle. She didn't demand answers. She didn't come to me. By this morning, Vic Kingsley was purchasing silence under accounts bearing her own name.

But first, Vic tried something else. The whiteboard in the investigation room tells the story plainly: "VIC: Wants to set Sam up." The group noticed it. Sources tell me Vic spent much of this morning's investigation talking about needing more money, having hushed conversations with Alex Reeves, Sarah Blackwood, and Blake. Vic's relationships with the other investigators kept her insulated from serious suspicion. She redirected, and for a while the room followed her finger toward Sam Thorne before the group pulled it back toward Quinn Sterling.

The redirect failed. But it tells us something about priorities. Vic wasn't trying to find Marcus's killer. She was trying to protect the investment she'd just watched crumble. And when deflection didn't work, she turned to the ledger.

Meanwhile, three hours before Remi's laptop revelation, Quinn Sterling was confessing to friends. At 12:52 AM, a recovered memory captures Quinn, Cass, and Zia Bishara together for the first time since an infamous conference call that blew up their working relationship. Quinn confessed to the off-the-books PT-3A research that Marcus had uncovered. Confessed to the blackmail threatening to end a career. Cass hugged Quinn so hard. Zia let go of an old grudge right there in front of everyone.

The Confession That Convicted
QUINN.4 - 12:52AM - You, CASS, and ZIA. It's the first time you've been together since that infamous conference call when you burned it all down. You're currently feeling such intense pressure about Psychotrophin-3A that this once dreaded meeting now feels like a walk in the park. You finally confess to your off-the-books research that MARCUS uncovered, and his blackmail that's threatening your career. CASS hugs you so hard. You watch as ZIA releases their deep-seated grudge in front of your eyes. The truth is incredibly freeing.
Quinn Sterling

"The truth is incredibly freeing." That's what the memory says. A confession meant to unburden became the evidence that convicted. The group weighed Quinn's motive against Sam's access, Remi's laptop intrusion, even Vic's suspicious behavior. Quinn won the accusation. But freedom and culpability are not the same thing.

Follow the Money

Two shell accounts bear Vic Kingsley's name. Not a pseudonym. Not a code. Her name. $375,000 to "Victoria" at 4:23 AM. Another $130,000 to "Vic" seven minutes later at 4:30 AM. That's $505,000 total, purchased under accounts so traceable they might as well be a signature. Either Vic wanted to be found, or she didn't think anyone would look.

The Investor Who Knew
VIC004 - 1:08AM - REMI approaches you and ALEX with a laptop in hand, looking like a smug son of a bitch. At first, what you see looks familiar. The demo loops perfectly. But then ALEX points out what you're not seeing. The code doesn't match. The demo's fake. ALEX snatches the laptop to investigate while you stand there, dumbfounded. What they find is disastrous. MARCUS's "breakthrough AI" that he's been crowing about for years needs manual fixes for 90% of the inputs. Put another way... it's worthless.
Vic Kingsley

The woman who watched NeurAI's fraud unravel on a laptop screen at 1:08 AM last night responded not by exposing what she'd seen, but by purchasing silence under her legal name this morning. My sources inside the warehouse saw Vic having hushed conversations with Blake during the investigation. I watched the ledger populate in real time from where I was receiving the feed. Two accounts. One person. Half a million dollars.

Then there's the ghost. An account called "NeurAI" received $1.2 million across five separate entries, all within a fourteen-minute window between 4:35 and 4:49 AM. Someone used the dead company's name as a shell account to acquire more silence than anyone else in the room. The account name itself is a tell. Whoever created it was thinking about institutional protection, not personal gain. Was it Vic, extending her campaign under a corporate alias? Or someone else entirely, someone with access and motive to shield NeurAI's secrets even after Marcus was gone?

Now look at where the money isn't. Quinn Sterling, accused of murder, has no financial footprint on the ledger. No shell account. No attempt to suppress the PT-3A evidence or the blackmail memory. Sam Thorne, who spent the morning unlocking and exposing every extracted memory within reach, also sits at zero. In a room where $1.7 million changed hands, the accused killer and the most transparent investigator both have clean ledgers. The people who spent money were protecting a company. Not covering up a murder.

The Players

Remi Whitman came to that party with a plan and executed it with surgical precision. Followed Marcus through a secret door. Grabbed a thumb drive. Talked Sam Thorne into handing over a laptop. Recruited Zia Bishara as a hacker. Built a fraud case in real time and delivered it to the one person who could do something about it. Remi wanted Vic Kingsley's attention, wanted her to stop prioritizing NeurAI and give AIBioComp a shot at funding. Remi got what they wanted. Whether it was justice or leverage is a question I can't answer from the evidence alone.

Sam Thorne operated in the open from the first minute to the last. My sources describe Sam working solo all morning, unlocking extracted memories and turning every single one over to the public record. The whiteboard had Sam flagged for drugs, memory extraction access, potential data involvement. Vic wanted Sam to take the fall. But Sam's behavior was the opposite of someone with something to hide. Early the previous evening, Sam was already raising alarms about PT-3A with Nat Francisco, worried Marcus was poisoning himself. That concern became evidence. Sam's transparency made them simultaneously Vic's target and the investigation's backbone.

I've been following Alex Reeves's legal battle with NeurAI for months now, and the evidence that landed on my desk this morning confirms what Alex has been arguing all along. Alex was the one who saw through the demo. When Remi turned that laptop around, it was Alex who pointed out what Vic wasn't seeing, snatched the machine, and confirmed that Marcus's AI needed manual fixes for 90% of inputs. Alex's cease and desist against NeurAI, filed through Patchwork Law Firm, is now escalating into a multimillion-dollar lawsuit. Alex didn't just help solve the case. Alex has been building this case for years.

Sarah Blackwood's whereabouts have been unknown since the investigation concluded. Sources tell me she went quiet in the final hour, stepping away from the group with a look they described as someone recalculating everything she thought she knew. The recovered memories tell their own story about her night. At 1:36 AM, Sarah found Jess Kane in the bathroom, crying. She put together that Marcus's baby was Jess's. The memory captures Sarah saying, "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do." Sarah came to that party to confront her estranged husband. She left it discovering the full scope of his betrayal. Neither she nor Vic Kingsley has been available for comment.

Vic Kingsley is traveling to an undisclosed location to recover from trauma. That's the official line. The unofficial line is written across two shell accounts: $505,000 in purchased silence. The VC who was supposed to be the adult in the room turned out to be the one writing checks to keep what she'd learned in the dark. Vic's relationships with the other investigators kept her safe from serious suspicion this morning. That insulation was its own kind of evidence.

What's Missing

Five entries under NeurAI. $1.2 million. All within a fourteen-minute window. Someone created that account and funneled more silence through it than anyone else in the room. Vic Kingsley had motive and was already operating under her own name. But the NeurAI account is different. Institutional, not personal. Someone was thinking about the company's survival, not their own exposure. I don't know who. I know what it cost.

Quinn Sterling was accused of murder, but Quinn's financial trail is clean. No shell accounts, no suppression of the PT-3A evidence, no attempt to bury the blackmail memory. If Quinn killed Marcus to escape blackmail, the evidence of that blackmail is still sitting in the public record for anyone to find. That's either the behavior of someone who didn't care about getting caught, or someone who didn't do it.

Sam Thorne is being called in for further questioning by police. The whiteboard had Sam flagged for drugs, memory extraction access, data access. Sam's laptop was the vehicle for the fraud discovery, but it also contained secrets Sam didn't want exposed. What was on that client spreadsheet? Why did Sam flee when Vic arrived at the party? If Sam had nothing to hide, those questions shouldn't linger. But they do.

What did Vic's purchased silence contain? She saw the fraud firsthand. She had access to evidence across every thread. The $505,000 in her named accounts bought something, but silence about what? And the $1.2 million NeurAI ghost account remains unsigned. The silence outweighs the truth by an order of magnitude. I can tell you its shape. I can't tell you what's inside it.

I reported the group's accusation of Quinn Sterling. A researcher blackmailed into making a dangerous drug for a desperate man. The motive is documented. The means were in Quinn's hands. I'm reporting their verdict. I'm not endorsing it. The accusation answers the question of who killed Marcus Blackwood. It does not answer the question of who tried to bury the world he built.

What I can tell you is this: Marcus Blackwood built a company on stolen code. He powered it with dangerous drugs. He used blackmail to keep his suppliers in line. When his empire was exposed, $1.7 million materialized to suppress the evidence. Remi Whitman's shadow investigation produced the proof. Sam Thorne's laptop carried it. Alex Reeves spotted the flaw. And Vic Kingsley, who watched it all unfold on a screen at 1:08 AM, responded by purchasing half a million dollars in silence under her own name. The fraud didn't die with Marcus. It found new management.

The memory extraction technology that Marcus developed to steal people's experiences didn't die with him either. It worked. Fifteen people walked into that warehouse last night. By morning, their private moments were currency, traded on a ledger that Blake now controls. Whoever funneled $1.2 million through the NeurAI account wasn't protecting a dead man. They were protecting the infrastructure. The machine that turns your memories into someone else's leverage. That infrastructure is operational right now. It has a customer base. It has a revenue model. And it has new owners who've already demonstrated they'll pay seven figures to keep it quiet.

This is the part where I'm supposed to tell you the system failed. It didn't. The system worked exactly as designed. Marcus built a machine to extract and monetize human memory. He stole the code to power it, blackmailed a researcher to enhance it, and faked every demo to fund it. When he died, the machine kept running. The $1.7 million spent this morning wasn't a cover-up. It was a purchase order. Someone, maybe Vic, maybe the ghost behind the NeurAI account, maybe both, looked at the wreckage of Marcus Blackwood's empire and saw not a scandal to contain but an asset to acquire. The five investigators in that warehouse were honest, as far as I can tell. But at least one of them was also shopping. And the product they were buying is you. Your memories. Your private moments. Your leverage, sold back to you at a price you never agreed to pay. Marcus Blackwood is dead. The machine he built to rob you of your most intimate experiences is under new management and open for business. I intend to find out whose.
