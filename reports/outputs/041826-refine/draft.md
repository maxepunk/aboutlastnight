## HEADLINE

**The Room Named a Fist. The Board Named a Widow.**

## DECK

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Eight votes pointed at Alex Reeves. Next week, the company that built the technology to extract memories from unwilling subjects will be run by the person most completely erased by it.

## BYLINE

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent
February 22, 2027

---

## LEDE

Marcus Blackwood died last night. This morning, seventeen people stood in a warehouse and voted on who killed him. Eight of them pointed at Alex Reeves, the man whose algorithm Marcus stole, whose career Marcus hollowed out, whose fist had met Marcus's jaw only hours before the CEO stopped breathing.

The math was clean. The motive was cinematic. Alex even brought the recording of his own punch to the investigation and set it on the table like a dare. But the room he brought it into was full of people with reasons, an experimental drug nobody wanted traced, and a set of shell accounts wearing other people's names like costumes.

I was there. I watched the votes shift and shift again. And after the vote finally landed, they all ran.

## FOLLOW THE MONEY

$6,265,000 worth of memories got buried during the investigation this morning. Seven shell accounts. And Blake was there for every transaction.

With Marcus gone, Blake works for the NeurAI board. I've been covering this company for months. The man who spent the morning turning memories into money was an institutional operator, there to make sure the company survived whatever the room figured out.

Here's what I can read and what I can't. Blake's books show me the shape: account, amount, moment. He doesn't show me which memory went into which account. For that I'm reading pattern against absence — who had evidence that didn't reach the exposed record, who was overheard at his station, whose accounts grew when. What follows are hypotheses. The shapes are real. The names inside them, I'm inferring.

Start with the frames. Three of the seven accounts carry names that match specific people: **Taylor. Zia. Mell.** Naming a laundering vehicle after yourself is a stupid move if you're trying to stay hidden. It makes more sense if someone wants those particular names attached to the money.

The Mell account took six memories in one batch at 9:48 AM — $1,350,000 in a single moment, the largest single-moment dump of the night. Morgan Reed has no exposed memory on the record. We know from Taylor Chase's 12:40 AM deal memory that Morgan had the evidence on Marcus: shell companies, insider trading, dangerous experiments, lying under oath — the kind of institutional-exposure material NeurAI's board would need to disappear before any of it could surface. None of it reached the Detective. Mel Nilsson also has no memory on the record, despite giving a legal defense of Sarah during the accusation. The Mell account carries her name with a typo. Either someone weaponized the frame for deniability, or one of her oldest friends has been quietly moving money around Marcus longer than last night.

The Taylor account took one memory for $125,000 at 9:29 AM. Taylor closed the Morgan deal at 12:40 AM, put her journalistic reputation on the line, and put the evidence of that deal on the Detective's desk this morning. I saw Kai Andersen near Blake shortly before the Taylor-named account first hit the board. Make of that what you will. I won't pretend I can tell you whose memory it was.

The Zia account took one memory for $30,000. An insult-priced frame, sized small enough to look suspicious without being plausible. Zia Bishara wasn't even in the warehouse this morning. Her memory of the code comparison reached me despite her absence — and the cheapest shell on the ledger carries her name. Someone wanted NeurAI's stolen-code problem to become Zia's reputation problem.

Three frames, three deflections. Each one routes heat away from a specific NeurAI-threatening vector: Morgan's lobbyist-knowledge problem, Zia's IP-theft problem, Taylor's paid-favor problem. Frames don't work on people who are already running toward the light — Taylor put her own evidence on the table this morning; the source who exposed Zia's memory did the same. But a frame doesn't have to work to do its job. It just has to get looked at.

Now the anonymous accounts. Nancy is the largest on the ledger: $1,830,000, six memories, three separate visits between 9:46 and 10:15 AM. Whoever funded Nancy wasn't panicking — they were building. Most accounts took one or two visits. Nancy took three, each larger than the last. Remi Whitman, overheard at Blake's station this morning, said she had lots of employees to feed, that she couldn't take care of others if she couldn't take care of herself. Remi's own memory of the evening isn't on the exposed record. She was also the one who handed Zia the laptop that proved Marcus's code theft — the kind of proof Blake's employer would pay almost anything to keep from surfacing. I can't confirm Nancy is Remi's. But the accumulating profile fits the woman I heard talking about payroll.

Ladybug took four memories for $1,525,000 — three in one visit at 9:44, one more seven minutes later. Jamie Woods tended bar at the party and has no exposed memory on the record. She had a side hustle I've heard about: invisible workers who sell what they overhear to the highest bidder on both sides. Some people will pay handsomely for the tidbit; others will pay handsomely to make sure the tidbit never surfaces. If Ladybug is Jamie's, she witnessed the party, and she sold what she saw.

Mushroom took four memories for $605,000 in a single sitting between 10:07 and 10:08 — someone who arrived with a ready stack and cleared it fast. Flip has no exposed memory on the record. But she did have a secret I know about, her real-life identity she's been hiding from her own social circle for years, one Marcus used as leverage.

Elephant took three memories for $800,000, plus a $50,000 first-burial bonus. Sarah Blackwood, during the investigation this morning, was overheard telling Blake there was an elephant in the room. The first burial of the entire night — at 9:26 AM, before anyone else had a chance to get their bearings — was $450,000 into that account. Sarah named the thing nobody else would say out loud, and somebody paid first-burial money to make sure nobody would be able to. I'll get back to her.

The accused has no account. If Alex killed Marcus in a rage over stolen code, his financial fingerprint should be on this ledger somewhere. It isn't. The money bent around him, not toward him.

## THE PLAYERS

Count who brought receipts. Alex exposed his own punch. The accused walked into the room with the most damning footage of himself and put it on the table. Sam Thorne's memories talk about the drug supply — the usual party blend, the 10:44 PM confession that Marcus was God's favorite idiot, the 12:50 AM Stanford meeting. Complicity rendered as testimony. Nat Francisco matched her, memory for memory. Mel Nilsson brought the reckoning. If the Stanford Four wanted a cover-up, they ran the worst one I've ever seen. What they ran looked more like a confession broken into three voices.

Quinn Sterling exposed the blackmail. The memory shows Marcus had her over a barrel on PT-3A. She put it in the room anyway. Vic Kingsley exposed the replacement plan and the image of himself on the sidewalk with a designer scarf pressed against Marcus's bleeding nose. Taylor exposed the Morgan deal, the one that could make or break her career, then handed me the evidence of it.

Now count the invisible. Not the buriers specifically — the people Marcus's world treated as scenery, who turn out to be the ones who saw it best.

Jamie "Volt" Woods tended bar at the party last night. She watches every one of these parties for a living. Her character sheet describes a side hustle I've heard about in five different cities now — invisible workers who sell what they overhear to the highest bidder on both sides. Some people will pay handsomely for the tidbit; others will pay handsomely to make sure the tidbit never surfaces. Jamie's own memories of last night are not on the exposed record but a text message exchange between Jamie and Skyler about exchanges of information for money that were found in the warehouse support the theory about her side hustle.

Flip was at the party under an assumed identity — a real name I won't publish because she's been trying to keep it hidden from her own social circle for years. Marcus knew it. Marcus held it over her. Flip's own memories of last night are not on the exposed record either. Her identity was being leveraged by Marcus long before the extraction technology got involved.

Kai Andersen was treated as furniture by his own boss. "I hired you to decorate, not think." Kai is also the person who installed the code that would have run Cass Zhang's stolen Synesthesia Engine on Marcus's guests last night. The artist-as-vendor treatment isn't incidental. It's how the stolen code got into the walls of the party. Invisibility is useful to the person it's imposed on, and it's useful to the person imposing it. The visible hire the invisible, and then act surprised when the invisible see.

And Blake. The valet. Present at every moment anyone needed to launder a memory. The institution's fixer. The invisible worker whose job is turning other people's evidence into silence on behalf of the people who can afford to pay him.

Then there's who held their cards. Morgan Reed, Riley Torres, Jamie, Flip. Four people, not a word on the exposed record between them. We know Morgan made the deal with Taylor to blow up Marcus's reputation, but Morgan's own memories of doing it stayed dark. Riley is Sarah Blackwood's best friend and, per her character sheet, an environmental lawyer who'd been gathering evidence on Marcus's business dealings for months.

Jess Kane is on the roster. Her name isn't on anything else. Not a single exposed memory places her anywhere last night. Sam — Marcus's old Stanford friend and the man's drug supplier — mailed her the paternity test that brought her to that party to begin with. Whatever happened to her after she arrived is entirely outside the record.

Remi Whitman's overheard conversation with Blake is the exact shape this operation takes for someone who's not a frame and not a witness — just a person who needs money and has something worth selling. Her words were about feeding her employees. Whatever she had to trade, Blake works for the company whose valuation depended on her silence. The market functioned.

What I watched this morning wasn't a room trying to solve a murder together. It was eight or nine groups of one or two, each arranging their own pieces, looking up only long enough to vote.

## THE ACCUSATION

The accusation wasn't one theory. It was five, and each one fell apart differently.

Sarah started it. Sam had been feeding Marcus drugs. Alex had motive — stolen code, public humiliation. A collusion theory: the two of them had drugged Marcus to death. Sam pushed back. She'd been worried about Marcus, she said, trying to help him, not kill him. She had her diary to corroborate it, and she opened it on the table. I watched Alex and Sam work closely together all morning, helping each other recover lost memories, and the drug-collusion accusation dissolved without a single vote landing against either of them directly.

Then the theory turned on Sarah herself. Marcus had cleaned out her bank account. Marcus was fathering someone else's child. Motive-rich. Mel Nilsson — Sarah's divorce attorney and one of the Stanford Four — pushed back. Sarah was handling Marcus through legal channels. Mel had been helping her do it. The case against Sarah didn't take.

Morgan Reed's name came up next. The shell companies, the insider trading, the jail she was trying to avoid. Morgan ended the vote with seven against her — one vote shy of the accusation.

Zia Bishara, who wasn't even in the room, got two votes for her stolen code. The whiteboard held a fifth theory I kept coming back to: *Marcus was drugged to death.* Somebody in that room thought the punch wasn't even how he went.

I had overheard Alex this morning, late in the investigation, speaking uncertainly about his own behavior last night — a man genuinely disoriented by what had happened to him. But when it came to accusations, he pushed back that punching someone and killing them weren't the same thing. Eight people disagreed. 

The vote landed. They all gave statements. They all ran.

## WHAT'S MISSING

Morgan Reed's own memories of making the Taylor deal never reached me. Taylor's did — she put her deal on the record herself. From Morgan's end, there is silence. Somebody bought it out of existence before I got there.

[PHOTO: Nat and Alex this morning]

Four people whose memories are nowhere on the record: Morgan, Riley, Flip, and Jamie. That's the shape of people who came to the warehouse with tokens and decided nobody should see what was on them.

But there's another shape, and it's the one that's been bothering me all morning.

Five people in that warehouse cannot tell you where they were last night. Sarah Blackwood. Jess Kane. Riley Torres. Mel Nilsson. Quinn Sterling. Their memories of the evening either sit locked in tokens nobody found or they were buried. Marcus's wife. His pregnant mistress. His wife's best friend. His wife's lawyer. The researcher he was coercing. The five people with the clearest stake in Marcus's downfall are also the five we know the least about.

That's not a coincidence. Memory extraction is a technology. Technologies have patterns. This one's pattern is that the people with the most reason to turn on a NeurAI founder are the people least able to account for themselves the morning after. The vote went 8-7-2 partly because Alex was the only candidate the room could fully reconstruct. His punch was on the record. His motive was legible. Morgan was more culpable and the room knew it — seven of them voted for her. But Morgan's own evening? Evidence was so sparse. Either by coincidence or by design, there was just not enough evidence to point the finger at Morgan. And my vote is that this was by design. 

Nancy is the largest account on the ledger at $1.83 million across six transactions. Ladybug, Mushroom, Elephant all carry the same grammar — opaque names, likely chosen by their actual owners as private codes nobody else at that station would read. $6.27 million of memories gone, never to be recovered, purchased by NeurAI one token at a time.

And the simplest gap of all: Marcus was being replaced by Vic, resisted by Quinn, punched by Alex, dosed by Sam, exposed by the owner of a memory Remi handed over, undercut by Taylor and Morgan, and reckoned with by his oldest friends, all inside the same hundred-and-eighty-minute window. Any one of those events could have been the fatal one. The room voted on the loudest. I can see the shape of the silence around the others. I can't tell you what's inside it.

## CLOSING

Eight votes. A documented punch. A man who brought his own fist to the record. The accusation stands, but it stands on a floor with holes in it.

Marcus Blackwood built an empire on Zia Bishara's code, Quinn Sterling's chemistry, Kai Andersen's unpaid creative work, Alex Reeves's erased authorship, and Sam Thorne's ongoing willingness to keep him dosed and functional. The Stanford Four called their 12:50 AM meeting nine years too late. Vic decided to fire him the night someone else decided to do something more permanent. Taylor cut the deal to destroy his reputation nine minutes after the code comparison proved he didn't have one. Everyone in that room had been looking away for a decade, and most looked away even this morning when a murder charge was on the line.

Three real names sit on shell accounts this morning. Zia, Taylor, Mell. Whoever designed that, designed it to be seen. A frame only works if somebody looks at it, and the people who built these wanted them looked at. That isn't the fingerprint of a killer trying to get away. That's the fingerprint of an institution shaping which story gets told.

Here's what I know that the room didn't. As I write this, a source I trust has confirmed that NeurAI will name Sarah Blackwood as interim CEO next week.

Sarah cannot remember her evening. Her entire record of last night is gone. She'll take the chair anyway. Marcus's widow, handed the company built on stolen code she didn't write, a broken AI she didn't build, and a memory-extraction protocol that erased her own account of the last twelve hours of her marriage. Yet, with her in the driver's seat NeurAI gets to maintain some level of consistency in leadership. 

Vic Kingsley wanted Alex in that seat. Vic is the primary investor on the board. Vic will almost certainly have signed off on this. At 9:55 PM last night, the plan was to install the man who'd been wronged. Eight hours and a fist later, the plan is to install the woman who was wronged harder, who also happens to be next of kin, and who also happens to have no memory of the night anyone could have asked her about.

I can't prove Sarah Blackwood made a deal with Blake. What I can observe: she was about to walk away from Marcus with nothing. Marcus emptied their accounts and planned the divorce to leave her destitute. That was her life up until yesterday. Now Marcus is dead, and next week the company is hers, and her first known act this morning — before the rest of us had finished arriving — was a conversation with Blake about an elephant in the room. The first $450,000 paid into silence that morning went to an account called Elephant. Her own memory of the evening is nowhere on the exposed record. Blake works for the board. The board needed clean books. The widow needed something to walk away with. If that account is hers, the first thing Blake bought off the ledger this morning was whatever she had seen.

That's a shape. I can't tell you what's inside it.

The machine that required Marcus now requires Sarah. That's the thing about an empire built on everyone else's work — once you've built it, it doesn't actually need you. It just needs somebody willing to sit in the chair. Preferably somebody who won't ask too many questions about the books, because she literally can't remember the answers.

Alex has not been seen since the investigation. He's disappeared after Blake led everyone out of that warehouse where their memories were stolen from them. Chances are, Alex is still running. Only time will tell whether he can clear his good name. 

The room named a fist. The evening named a fixer. The board named a widow. Only one of them is now wanted by the law. The outsider who threatened NeurAI's very existence with his Cease & Desist letter — a legal claim that could have unwound the entire company — is on the run for Marcus' murder. And meanwhile the machine Marcus built is chugging along under new leadership.
