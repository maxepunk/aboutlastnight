The Blackwood Investigation

Marcus Blackwood Spent Five Years Robbing Alex Reeves. This Morning, She Got His Company.

He died at his own party, full of a drug he bullied his chemist into making. The room blamed the chemist and buried the evidence, and never asked why the biggest account in the building wore Alex's name.

Casper Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 22, 2027 · Article NNA-062626-26

The biggest account in that room had Alex Reeves's name on it. One point eight million dollars, paid into the same market that keeps memories from ever reaching a room like this one. By tonight, a leaked tip says, Alex is also being lined up to take Marcus Blackwood's company. Nobody in the room set those two facts side by side. I think that was the point.

Marcus is dead. Twenty people gathered this morning to rebuild his last night out of the memories he had pulled from them, on his own machine, while they were drugged. They came to decide who was to blame. I was in the room for all of it, working it alongside the man who runs the accounts. What I watched was not an investigation. It was a cleanup, and it finished early.

The Story

Marcus Blackwood was a fraud, and by the night he died almost everyone in the building could prove it. The company that made him famous, NeurAI, did not run on his code. It ran on Zia Bishara's.

The Serial Numbers Filed Off
On the left, Zia's Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. On the right, NeurAI's "proprietary" system from 2027. The data is identical. Marcus didn't even bother changing the notes. He's not just a thief, he's a sloppy thief. Maybe now Vic will stop prioritizing NeurAI and give me the time of day.
Remi Whitman

Remi Whitman did not bring that proof to mourn a rival. He brought it to trade, and the bitterness in the last line is the tell. Everyone in that house wanted something from Marcus's ruin. The theft had a paper trail older than the party: a cease and desist had already gone out under Alex Reeves's name, demanding NeurAI's board remove Marcus and stop running stolen work.

Cease and Desist
The Patchwork Law Firm has been retained by Alex Reeves in a suit against Marcus Blackwood and NeurAI regarding the theft of intellectual property in the code base of NeurAI. We insist that M. Blackwood be removed as the Chief Executive Officer of NeurAI. A. Reeves is willing to assume the role of Chief Technology Officer immediately upon his ouster. The NeurAI Board has until February 28, 2027 to comply, or the Patchwork Law Firm will initiate legal proceedings.
Alex Reeves

But Marcus took more than code. The machine that pulled the memories out of his guests was not even his. It was the Synesthesia Engine, built by Cass Zhang, who pitched it to him and signed his NDA, then watched him hide it inside the party installation Kai Andersen built and run it on the room. The same stolen tech the investigators used this morning to rebuild the night was the tech that harvested it.

The chemistry started with Quinn Sterling, who synthesized the compound and then spent months trying to bury it. Marcus would not let him.

Make It Stronger
I need you to bring me another batch of Psychotrophin-3A. The method you established works, but the side effects I'm looking for are inconsistent, and we should go up on the dosage. I know you're resistant, you didn't test levels anywhere near this high during the PT-3B safety assessments. And you can't afford to say no, can you? I'll put you on the guest list. Come find me there. Anything you want to tell me about it, you can tell me in person.
Marcus Blackwood

That is an email, recovered from Quinn's files, not a memory. It is a threat. By the night of the party Quinn was confessing to anyone who would listen, to Ezra Sullivan, to Cass Zhang, to Zia, that he had made the drug and that Marcus was forcing him to make it stronger. That is not a man planning a murder. It is a man trying to climb out from under one.

But Quinn synthesized the compound. He did not mix the batch that went into the guests. The baggies recovered from the room were a different formulation, Psychotrophin-3B, and they belonged to Sam Thorne, who had been making Marcus's party blend for years.

That's What I Do
Marcus is so high, and I just gave him my usual party blend. He should not be this far gone. He told me the BizAI algorithm was never his own work. Alex rewrote the whole thing, and Marcus still took the credit.
Sam Thorne

Behind the bar, Jamie Woods watched the supply arrive.

Just the Bartender
Marcus comes in with a stranger in a lab coat. "Ingesting extracts. Untested. Dangerous." Marcus says, "I can handle it." An envelope changes hands. The stranger leaves. Then Marcus pops the pills right there and stares at the bar, looking scared. Rich people and their top-shelf habits.
Jamie 'Volt' Woods

The room had all of this. Marcus extorted a dangerous drug, dosed himself with it in front of the bartender, and waved off every warning he was given. I cannot tell you it is what killed him. No memory in that room reaches the moment he died, and I will not pretend past the evidence. But the room did not wait for proof. Almost its first move was to decide Marcus had done it to himself, and that decision did a great deal of work. It meant no one else had to answer for anything.

Follow the Money

More than four and a half million dollars changed hands that morning, paid out by Blake to keep memories from ever reaching the room. That much is not in dispute. Riley Torres had spent the party tracing the hidden vehicles Marcus used to move money like it, building a case for the wife he was draining. The money was never the secret. The names on it were.

Most of the accounts that absorbed it wear invented ones. An account called Pikachu holds eight hundred and twenty-five thousand. One spelled Mohammed Ali, four hundred and twenty-five thousand. Three of the accounts wear real names.

The largest belongs to Alex. One point eight million dollars, more than the next two accounts combined. I do not know which memories went into it. I cannot know that, and I will not pretend otherwise. What I can tell you is how the account got its name, because I heard Alex decide it.

"You know, I'm a shit stirrer. Put our joint account under my name, you guys Ok with that?"

Alex Reeves

A second account carries Mel Nilsson's name. I heard her hand it to Blake myself.

"Put it under my name, I have nothing to hide."

Mel Nilsson

A third carries Ashe Motoko's, and I cannot tell you who opened it or whose memory is inside, because that is exactly the kind of thing the buried ledger is built to hide. None of these names is the man the room convicted. Quinn Sterling has no account at all. The chemist they blamed for Marcus's death left that market with nothing, while the partner they cleared left it with more than anyone.

The Players

Twenty people came to that party, and almost none of them came as friends.

The most important ones came as a plan. Vic Kingsley had sunk thirty million dollars into a company her own people were now calling a sham. Alex Reeves wanted the company she said was built on her stolen code, and a cease and desist with her name on it had been sitting on the NeurAI board's desk for weeks, demanding the board remove Marcus and bring her in. Remi Whitman, the rival Vic also funded, brought the proof to finish the job. By the night of the party the three of them were not waiting on a verdict. They were replacing him.

Taylor Chase had buried Marcus's story once before, killing Ashe Motoko's exposé to win the NeurAI launch exclusive. Ashe was the reporter that favor destroyed, fired from her paper after the story died. Morgan Reed was the crisis manager Marcus kept on retainer for nights exactly like this one, and by morning Morgan had turned, feeding Taylor the file to take Marcus down instead.

Sam Thorne mixed the chemistry. Flip owed dangerous people more than she could pay, and spent the night taking Sam's product to keep the edge off. Nat Francisco filmed the man he had spent years failing to save. Skyler Iyer paid the bartender to spy on the man whose memory tech she needed. Jamie Woods poured the drinks and remembered all of it.

And two people who had the most reason to be enemies refused. Sarah Blackwood was divorcing Marcus. Jess Kane was carrying his child, and the test left nothing to argue about. They found each other in a bathroom, and chose this.

"You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."

Sarah Blackwood

That was not surrender. In a building full of people protecting themselves, it was the one act of grace.

Then the room voted, and the grace did not survive it. Blake had already shut the market down ahead of the police and was pushing everyone to name a suspect. Jamie put Alex forward; her stolen code was motive enough. Blake, the man who runs the accounts, waved it off. You can't make a lot of money from a dead man, he said, and the room agreed. She drew zero votes. The first count put the no-fault theory in front, seven votes for the idea that nobody was to blame. As the clock ran down, that number fell to three. With minutes left, the room handed the death to Quinn, ten to eight over Jess. The mistress, in the end, spent her last vote on the widow.

What's Missing

For everything the room reconstructed, the most useful names never reached the board.

The board names Quinn for the drugs. It does not name Sam Thorne, whose blend filled the baggies and whose own diary admits she was the one making it. The room had the chemist who wanted out, and convicted him. It never said the name of the supplier standing in the room. Blake was asked directly who put the drugs into circulation that night. He declined to answer.

And the board has plenty about how Marcus lived and everyone he wronged to get there. It has nothing about who got rich the morning he died. Nobody asked why the largest fortune in the room sat under the name of the woman now in line to inherit his company. That part reached me later, as I was writing this: Alex Reeves is to be offered Marcus's chair at NeurAI, once things "blow over."

Closing

Quinn Sterling spent the whole morning in that room. He solved the puzzles, he sat for the questions, and long before anyone pointed a finger he told them the truth: he made the compound, he let Marcus bully him into making it stronger, he was sick about it. They convicted him anyway, ten to eight, with the police minutes from the door. A warrant issued within hours. Only then did Quinn run.

Everything he confessed to is true. What he did not do is the one thing they pinned on him. He held the formula, not the body, and he is the only suspect who left that warehouse with less than he walked in with.

The people who left with more did not need a killer. The machine did not have to kill Marcus; by every account in that room he was handling that himself. It only had to make sure that when he went down, the evidence went down with him, the company changed hands clean, and someone outside the family wore the blame. Blake and I walked the last of them out a side door and into the business next door, minutes before the police came through the front.

The verdict was the easiest loop in the room to close. Alex had spent weeks demanding in writing that the board remove Marcus and bring her in, with a deadline of February 28. The verdict beat it, and on Quinn Sterling's name it handed her more than the letter ever dared ask for. The machinery was never in danger. It only changed hands.
