Marcus Blackwood Is Dead. His Crisis Manager Is Running. His Investor Is Smiling.

Eleven people tried to find a killer this morning. They accused the fixer. But the person who steered the room may have been the one holding the knife all along.

Cassandra Nova and Ashe Motoko | NovaNews · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-041126-26

Marcus Blackwood is dead. Last night he hosted a party at a warehouse in Fremont. This morning, eleven people sat in a room with his extracted memories and tried to figure out who killed him. They pointed at Morgan Reed, the crisis manager who spent years making Marcus's problems disappear. But here is the question that kept nagging at me as I pieced this together from tips and transferred evidence: did eleven people find the truth, or did one person steer them to his preferred conclusion?

Morgan is gone. Left the country, according to sources close to the investigation. Vic Kingsley, the venture capital investor who turned on Morgan in the final vote, is not available for comment. Between the man who ran and the man who stayed silent, there is a story. I am going to tell you what I know. I am also going to tell you what is missing. The gap between those two things should trouble you.

The Story

Start with Marcus, because everyone else did. The man built NeurAI on stolen code and called it innovation. Zia Bishara's Oracle Ledger, committed in 2023, showed up in NeurAI's 'proprietary' system in 2027 with the same variable names, the same logic flow, the same comments in the margins. Marcus did not even bother changing the notes. Cass's Synesthesia Engine, sent to Marcus for a demo, appeared in Kai Andersen's party installation without a contract or a payment. Sloppy theft from two different creators, laundered through one company's inflated valuation.

The Sloppy Thief
ZIA.4 - 12:31AM - REMI hands you the laptop. You punch in the default password for the Oracle Ledger and it works. Next, you pull up a code comparison. Left: your Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. Right: NeurAI's 'proprietary' system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. MARCUS didn't even bother changing your notes. Sloppy thief. Too bad the idiot didn't catch the massive bug that you've since fixed.
Zia Bishara

But stolen code was just the foundation. Marcus was also blackmailing Quinn Sterling into continuing development of Psychotrophin-3A, a memory drug with dangerous side effects that Quinn had buried the safety assessments for. He was self-administering it. He was financially gutting his wife while she defended him to his enemies. He was running shell companies with his own crisis manager. The man had built an empire of leverage, and last night, every piece of it was in that warehouse at the same time.

And the people who knew him best? They knew all of it. The Stanford Four, Sam Thorne, Mel Nilsson, Nat Francisco, and Marcus himself, had been circling this reckoning for years. A recovered memory from early in the evening shows Sam texting Marcus in a panic after overhearing Vic Kingsley and Morgan Reed discussing the possibility of going to jail because of him. Marcus did not even look up.

"You Must Have Misheard"
SAM.1 - 8:25PM - SAM texts MARCUS: we need to talk NOW. Just heard VIC and MORGAN chatting about going to jail bc of you?? / wtf man. you told me your new co is the best place for me… / cmon man seriously answer me. I can't afford to have my biz go down the drain just cuz you need me for your unicorn… / I swear to god if you don't answer me… / if I don't hear from you in the next 20 min, I'm going home. You have fun making special memories at the party without me and my special memory drugs. MARCUS: It's all good man. You must have misheard.
Sam Thorne

You must have misheard. That is Marcus Blackwood in four words. Deny, deflect, keep building. Sam did not mishear. Sam knew exactly what was happening, because Sam was the chemist making Psychotrophin-3A in a secret laboratory in an abandoned warehouse. Sam handed over a laptop to Remi Whitman without pausing to consider how many secrets were on it. All Remi had to do was open the client spreadsheet. The most dangerous kind of enabler is the reluctant one, the person who sees everything, worries constantly, and hands over the evidence by accident.

By 12:50 AM last night, Mel had called an emergency meeting of the Stanford Four minus Marcus. The memory shows them gathering with the weight of years of complicity. Mel brought the paternity news and secret bank accounts. Sam had already mailed the paternity test to Sarah Blackwood. But the real revelation was what Sam and Nat had figured out about the scope of the stolen code. Jess Kane, meanwhile, was nearby. She had overheard Sam and Nat discussing Psychotrophin-3A earlier that evening. She knew Marcus was experimenting on himself with it, because she knew for a fact that he was. She was carrying his child and carrying dangerous knowledge, and no one in that circle realized how much she had heard.

While the Stanford Four reckoned with their friend's crimes, Riley Torres was already building a case. A recovered memory from 10:12 PM shows Riley confronting Morgan Reed about the shell companies and insider trading he had been profiting from with Marcus. This could have been a career-making case for Riley. Years of research. Instead, she used it as bait. She promised to keep Morgan's name out of it if he would help her track the money for Sarah. She gave up the case to help her best friend.

Sarah needed the help. The memory shows her discovering that Marcus had already cleared out their shared bank account. He had corporate-shielded everything. He financially planned to leave her destitute. She had stopped working because he promised to support her no matter what, and he lied through his teeth. Mel Nilsson, her divorce attorney and friend, confirmed it: Sarah was caught completely unaware, face tear-stained, describing a nearly empty account and no independent income.

Financial Annihilation
SARAH.2 - 10:10PM - You both agreed that it was fine for you to stop working. He made plenty of money, and promised to support you no matter what. But he lied through his teeth. Even though he just found out you were leaving him, he'd already cleared out your shared bank account. He's been preparing for this day. Marcus must have corporate-shielded everything. He financially planned to leave you destitute. You know that divorces are never a walk in the park. But you can't believe that he'd do you so dirty.
Sarah Blackwood

And then came the bathroom at 1:36 AM. Sarah and Jess Kane, face to face, both crying. Jess expecting rage. Getting something else entirely.

"We Both Do"
JESS.4 - 1:36AM - Bathroom. Both crying. SARAH knows. You know she knows. She says: "The baby's MARCUS's?" You nod. Expect rage. Get: "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do." You're not enemies. You're both his victims.
Jess Kane

That line redefines every relationship in this story. Not enemies. Both his victims. Every arc converges on Marcus as the absent center, the man who stole code without changing the comments, who blackmailed a pharmacologist, who emptied his wife's bank account, who told his crisis manager to take care of it, just like you always do.

Remi Whitman turned Sam's laptop around at 1:08 AM so that Vic Kingsley and Alex Reeves could both see the code comparison. The stolen Oracle Ledger. The identical data. Remi was making a pitch, trying to convince Vic to stop prioritizing NeurAI and fund AIBioComp instead. But what Remi was really doing, without knowing it, was handing Vic the ammunition he needed. Vic did not stumble onto Marcus's fraud. He was handed it. And he knew exactly what to do with it.

The Intelligence Transfer
REMI.4 - 1:08AM - You turn SAM's laptop around so that VIC and ALEX can both see the code comparison. On the left: ZIA's Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. On the right: NeurAI's 'proprietary' system from 2027. the data is identical. MARCUS didn't even bother changing the notes. He's not just a thief, he's a sloppy thief. Next you roll the demo. That's even sloppier. Maybe now VIC will stop prioritizing NeurAI and give you the time of day.
Remi Whitman

Ashe's reporting from inside the room paints a picture. Vic worked the room all morning. Quick conversations with Morgan in shadowy corners. Quiet huddles with Sam and Mel. Vic and Sam were seen in what looked like heavy negotiations early on. Remi was overheard asking Mel, 'So do you want to trade a little?' Mel's response: 'I don't know if I should trust you.' Then the two of them made a beeline to a memory scanner together. Vic told Remi at one point, 'I need BOTH of your companies to succeed.' Think about what that means from an investor who was watching NeurAI's founder self-destruct. He was not mourning. He was repositioning.

Then came the confrontation. Vic raised his voice to Morgan. 'I can't believe you would take such actions!' Before the final deliberations, the alliance shattered publicly. Every whispered conversation between them now reads differently. Not partnership. Reconnaissance.

The group accused Morgan. The evidence is real. A recovered memory from 11:22 PM shows Morgan on a hammock with Marcus, presenting proof of bribes, fraudulent reports, shell company records, stolen IP. Morgan gave Marcus an ultimatum: go public about the insider trading or the evidence goes to Senator Walsh. Marcus responded the way Marcus always responded.

"Just Like You Always Do"
MORGAN.3 - 11:22PM - You're both on the hammock. You show him proof: bribes, fraudulent reports, shell company records, stolen IP... You say: "Go public about the insider trading and take full responsibility or I give all of this damning evidence to Senator Walsh." His face says he's considering it—but there's something else there. Instead of answering, he reaches into his bag and hands you a generous bribe. MARCUS: "Take care of it, will you? Just like you always do," then leaves without giving you a chance to respond.
Morgan Reed

Morgan did not take care of it. Not this time. By 12:40 AM, Morgan was sitting down with Taylor Chase and a recorder, spilling everything. The world leader requests. The insider trading. The entire long list. But Riley Torres had already laid the groundwork hours earlier.

The Deal That Built a Case
RILEY.2 - 10:12PM - These documents represent years of research. This could have been a career-making case for you. But instead of taking your evidence to court, you're using it to bait MORGAN into giving you the info you really need. You confront them about the shell companies and insider trading they've been profiting off of with MARCUS. They don't even try to deny it. You promise to keep their name out of it if they'll help you track the money down for SARAH. MARCUS has no idea what's about to go public.
Riley Torres

Morgan tried to force accountability. Got a bribe instead. Leaked to Taylor Chase. Then, this morning, became the primary suspect. But the path to that accusation was not clean.

The final deliberations began with two competing witness statements. Riley submitted evidence that Morgan and Vic had colluded to eliminate Marcus, covering up years of blackmail and financial crimes. Nat and Sam had a different theory: that Vic and Sarah had been the ones colluding, that Marcus's erratic behavior had begun to threaten everything they had built together, and that he had to be taken out of the picture. Vic was the common thread in both accusations. His defense was blunt: he had no reason to want Marcus dead. He was invested in Marcus. Why would he waste his own investment? He claimed the room had been trying to throw him to the sharks from the beginning, just because he controlled some money.

Then the room devolved. Accusations started flying in every direction. Sarah, Vic, and Morgan were each named as individual suspects. Even Nat and Sam's motives were questioned. And in the final minute before the police arrived, Vic pointed the finger at Morgan. Morgan was shocked. An argument broke out. Morgan claimed they had an agreement. It did not matter. The final vote went through, and Morgan had the most fingers pointed at him. The crisis manager who finally broke became the easiest person to blame, especially once the room's most powerful voice decided to blame him. Morgan ran. Vic stayed. And that is the part of this story that keeps me up.

Follow the Money

Three accounts dominated Blake's evidence market this morning. An account registered as 'Marcus friend' moved nine hundred and thirty thousand dollars in exposed evidence — more than any other. An account under Remi Whitman's name moved eight hundred thousand. A third, registered as 'Cass,' moved six hundred and seventy-five thousand. Total market activity across the investigation: two point four million dollars. Forty-one memories exposed. Zero buried.

The amounts tell one story. The fingerprints tell another. The 'Marcus friend' account carried transaction prints from multiple contributors — someone created it early, and people close to Marcus fed evidence through it as if it were a communal drop. The 'Remi' account shows the same pattern: multiple hands, one name. Remi Whitman is either a remarkably prolific source, or someone opened an account under his name to make it look that way. If someone was manufacturing a paper trail under Remi's name while Remi was busy handing Vic proof that NeurAI's code was stolen, the question is who benefits from muddying a competitor's credibility — and who had the relationships to coordinate it.

Vic poured twenty million dollars into NeurAI. The company whose code turned out to be identical to Zia Bishara's 2023 commit. The company whose founder was blackmailing a pharmacologist, self-administering experimental drugs, and emptying his wife's bank account. As an investor, Vic needed NeurAI to survive Marcus. A dead founder with a controllable successor is an asset. A live one spiraling out of control is a liability. Follow the investment, not just the evidence.

The Players

Riley Torres and Sarah Blackwood were inseparable this morning. While everyone else negotiated, these two built. Riley's deal with Morgan, detailed above, gave Sarah the financial trail she needed. Sarah's collaboration with Alex gave them both the technical proof. Between the two of them, they assembled more of the case than anyone else in the room — and neither of them was angling for power when they did it.

Alex Reeves came in with a cease and desist letter and years of stolen work behind it. She engineered the code that became BizAI. Marcus took credit and pushed her out. She watched him build NeurAI on what she believed was her intellectual property, and she came to this investigation to prove it. She collaborated with Sarah during the morning, two people Marcus had wronged finding common cause. Every memory Alex helped expose was aimed at the system that took her life's work.

Sam Thorne and Mel Nilsson carried years of complicity into this morning. Mel called the emergency meeting of the Stanford Four. Sam had already mailed the paternity test to Sarah. They finally acted. The question is not whether they acted, but why it took a death to make them.

Ashe Motoko's name is on this article for a reason. She worked the investigation from the inside, pairing with Jamie 'Volt' Woods, the bartender who saw everything and said nothing until it mattered. Marcus had destroyed Ashe's career once before, burying her expose through Taylor Chase. This morning she came back with a co-reporter's credential and a bartender who knew where the bodies were buried. Jamie unlocked puzzles, provided key insights, and remained under the radar. Together they contributed a significant portion of the evidence that ended up on my desk. The investigative engine of this story ran on their partnership.

Jess Kane operated under the radar for most of the investigation. She buried evidence under her own name. I do not know what was in it. I know she has been carrying more secrets than anyone should have to, carrying Marcus's child while carrying knowledge of his memory extraction experiments. She told Nat about how Marcus had used Psychotrophin-3A on her, calling it an acting gig. She took the drugs and let him run his experiment. He was elated. She was a test subject who loved him.

And then there is Vic Kingsley. Every partnership described above — Ashe and Jamie exposing evidence, Riley building the case, Alex proving the code theft, Sam and Mel reckoning with the Stanford Four's legacy — all of it flowed through a room that Vic was working. He talked to everyone. He committed to no one. His final-vote betrayal of Morgan was the investigation's most consequential act, and he has not said a word about it since. Vic was not available for comment after the investigation.

What's Missing

What happened between Morgan's leak to Taylor Chase and Marcus's death? The timeline has a gap that no exposed memory fills. Morgan fled the country. That is not in dispute. But flight can mean guilt or fear. If Vic engineered the accusation, Morgan may have run from a frame, not a crime.

Quinn Sterling was blackmailed into developing Psychotrophin-3A and buried the safety assessments. The exposed memories confirm it: Marcus discovered Quinn's off-the-books research and leveraged it, forcing Quinn to keep producing a drug with dangerous side effects that Quinn had tried to bury. Quinn eventually confessed to Ezra, and later to Cass and Zia, but by then Marcus had been self-administering the compound for months. Sam knew how to make the drug. Sam knew Marcus was self-administering dangerous doses. The Stanford Four discussed intervention at 12:50 AM. How far did that intervention go? And Vic's motive is the clearest and least discussed. No memory places him near Marcus at the time of death. He did not need to be. That is what makes him dangerous.

The manufactured account patterns described above did not happen by accident. Someone coordinated them. And throughout the morning, Vic, Sam, and Mel were seen in quiet conversations together multiple times. Vic and Sam were in heavy negotiations early on, the content of which no source was able to capture. The Stanford Four's reckoning with their dead friend may have been real. But it happened in the orbit of the man who would ultimately decide who to blame.

The shape of the silence here is not random. It is strategic. The gaps all point in the same direction: toward the drug research Quinn suppressed, toward the Stanford Four's final conversation with Marcus, toward whatever Vic knew and when he knew it. The group accused Morgan. The holes in the story suggest the truth is larger and darker than one man's guilt.

Eleven people tried to find a killer this morning. They accused Morgan Reed. The evidence supports it. The blackmail on the hammock, the leak to Taylor Chase, the shell companies, the flight from the country. But Vic Kingsley's last-minute pivot shaped the outcome more than any single piece of evidence. The accusation is credible. It is also convenient. For Vic.

Here is what I keep returning to. Vic walked into that room with a twenty-million-dollar investment in a company built on stolen code and a founder who was self-destructing. He walked out with the founder dead, the crisis manager blamed and gone, and himself positioned to inherit whatever remains. Every quiet conversation with Sam, every huddle with Mel, every negotiation whose content no one captured — that was not grief. That was restructuring.

Sam looked the other way for years. Mel called the meeting too late. Nat urged intervention that never came. But their reckoning did not happen in isolation. It happened in Vic's orbit, in whispered conversations with the one person who had the capital to decide how the story ended. The Stanford Four watched their friend become a monster. When they finally acted, their action flowed straight to the man who would use it. The system does not just produce Marcus Blackwoods. It produces Vic Kingsleys — people for whom everyone else's survival instinct is a resource to be managed.

I was not in that room this morning. I am piecing this together from tips slipped under my door and extracted memories transferred to my desk. Someone opened an evidence account under Remi's name with fingerprints that were not all his. Someone built the 'Marcus friend' account knowing exactly who would contribute. The infrastructure designed to expose truth was used to shape a narrative. I cannot tell you which evidence to trust, because the system that produced it is the same system that was being exploited. They accused Morgan Reed. The investor is not running. He does not need to.
