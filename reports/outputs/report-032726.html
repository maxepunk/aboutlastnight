They Accused Quinn Sterling of Murder, But He Wasn't Even in the Room

Five people investigated Marcus Blackwood's murder this morning. Blake's network was paying for every memory they were willing to bury. But not everyone was selling.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews | Senior Investigative Correspondent · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-0327-26

I wasn't in the warehouse this morning. I was receiving a live feed from sources inside when the first data came through — extracted memories, financial transactions, the raw evidence of twenty lives turned inside out at a party the night before. I expected a story about a tech company cutting corners. What my sources sent me was a fraud empire, a murder accusation, and $1.7 million in silence purchased before the police arrived.

Five people stayed for the investigation: Sarah Blackwood, Vic Kingsley, Remi Whitman, Sam Thorne, and Alex Reeves. They accused Quinn Sterling, a researcher Marcus had been blackmailing over a dangerous drug called Psychotrophin-3A. Quinn was not in the room. The accused killer has no financial footprint on the ledger. $1.7 million does.

The Accusation

Start with the blackmail, because that's where the group started. A recovered memory from 9:59 PM shows Quinn Sterling under Marcus Blackwood's thumb.

The Blackmail
QUINN.2 - 9:59PM - Somehow, MARCUS found out about the unusual side effects of Psychotrophin3A, and is obsessed with its ability to induce neural plasticity in large doses. Why did you bury the PT-3A safety assessments? Now he's got you by the balls and is blackmailing you to continue with your experiments to develop it. This drug is dangerous, and should not be in the wrong hands. But MARCUS wants you to bring him another batch of it tonight. You're to meet him at the bar at 10.
Quinn Sterling

Two minutes later, Jamie "Volt" Woods was near the service door when Marcus walked out with a stranger in a lab coat. The stranger warned him the extracts were untested and dangerous. Marcus took them anyway.

The Handoff
JAMIE.2 - 10:01PM - Voices behind the service door. Then MARCUS enters with a stranger wearing a lab coat. STRANGER: "Ingesting extracts—untested. Dangerous." MARCUS: "I can handle it." Envelope exchanges. Stranger leaves. Then MARCUS pops some pills and stares vacantly at the bar for a while. Looks scared.
Jamie "Volt" Woods

The group weighed Quinn's motive against other suspects. Vic pushed hard for Sam Thorne, and the room spent real time building that case before others pulled it back. Sam, Remi, and Vic were all considered. They landed on Quinn. A recovered memory of Quinn confessing to friends became the clinching evidence:

The Confession That Convicted
QUINN.4 - 12:52AM - You, CASS, and ZIA. It's the first time you've been together since that infamous conference call when you burned it all down. You finally confess to your off-the-books research that MARCUS uncovered, and his blackmail that's threatening your career. CASS hugs you so hard. You watch as ZIA releases their deep-seated grudge in front of your eyes. The truth is incredibly freeing.
Quinn Sterling

"The truth is incredibly freeing." A confession meant to unburden became the evidence that convicted. If Quinn killed Marcus to escape blackmail, the proof of that blackmail is still sitting in the public record. And Quinn was not even in the room this morning to respond to the accusation.

The Story

The recovered memories tell two parallel stories from last night. One ran in the shadows. One ran in the open. Together, they dismantled NeurAI.

At 9:37 PM, Remi Whitman followed Marcus through a secret door in the warehouse. The memory shows Marcus so high he could barely form sentences. He smiled when he saw Remi. Welcomed her in. Told her to help herself to anything. She grabbed a thumb drive and left.

Through the Secret Door
REMI.2 - 9:37PM - You saw him go through the secret door, and you didn't think. You just followed him. It looks like he's taken over the other half of this warehouse to live in. You're not sure why he's not at home with his wife, and don't care. You need to talk to him about the funding discrepancy. But the man is SO HIGH that he can barely string sentences together. He smiles when he sees you in his space. Welcomes you in. Tells you to help yourself to anything. So you do. You grab a thumb drive and leave. Now you need to find Alex.
Remi Whitman

Emails between Remi and Alex confirm this wasn't spontaneous. Remi came to that party with a plan to investigate Marcus over a funding discrepancy. The thumb drive was step one. But she needed a computer, and that's where Sam Thorne entered the story without meaning to.

The Laptop Handoff
SAM.3 - 11:17PM - REMI comes at you, looking so desperate, asking for a computer. You're in such a daze, thinking about MARCUS, that you hand over your laptop without pausing to consider how many secrets are on it. All they would have to do is open up your client spreadsheet and you'd be screwed. But they have it now. You'll just have to hope they're not the snooping sort. Then they ask if you know any hackers. So you pull out your phone and text ZIA.
Sam Thorne

Sam knew the risk. Handed it over anyway. Remi didn't open Sam's spreadsheet. She opened something much worse for Marcus Blackwood.

At 12:31 AM, Zia Bishara punched in the default password for something called the Oracle Ledger, and it worked. On the left of the screen: Zia's own code commit from 2023. On the right: NeurAI's "proprietary" system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. Marcus hadn't even changed the notes.

Then, at 1:08 AM, the second story collided with the first. Remi turned Sam's laptop around so that Vic and Alex could see.

The Moment It Collapsed
VIC004 - 1:08AM - REMI approaches you and ALEX with a laptop in hand, looking like a smug son of a bitch. At first, what you see looks familiar. The demo loops perfectly. But then ALEX points out what you're not seeing. The code doesn't match. The demo's fake. ALEX snatches the laptop to investigate while you stand there, dumbfounded. What they find is disastrous. MARCUS's "breakthrough AI" that he's been crowing about for years needs manual fixes for 90% of the inputs. Put another way... it's worthless.
Vic Kingsley

Alex spotted what Vic didn't. Snatched the laptop. Confirmed what she'd been arguing for years through the cease and desist filed by Patchwork Law Firm: NeurAI was built on stolen code that didn't even work. The biggest investment in Vic's portfolio evaporated on a laptop screen at a party.

That was last night. This morning, those memories were unlocked and exposed. What each investigator did with that information is the real story.

Follow the Money

My sources on the ground describe Sam Thorne working solo from the first minute to the last. Unlocking extracted memories. Turning every single one over to the public record. Spending every available minute before the police arrived trying to crack open more evidence. His financial footprint on the ledger is zero. In a room where $1.7 million changed hands, Sam's hands stayed empty. It's worth understanding why. Sam is not a venture capitalist or a startup CEO or an IP lawyer. He's a Stanford-trained chemist who made his living working for Marcus — his best friend since college, the man he threw raves with, the man he made the party drugs for, the man whose underground empire he kept functional. When Marcus died, Sam didn't lose an investment. He lost his best friend and his livelihood in the same night. The person with the least financial cushion in that room was the one who spent the morning giving evidence away for free.

The whiteboard had Sam flagged: drugs, memory extraction access, potential data involvement. Vic wanted Sam to take the fall. "VIC: Wants to set Sam up" — written right there on the board. Sources tell me Vic spent much of the morning talking about needing more money, having hushed conversations with Blake. Her relationships with the other investigators kept her insulated from scrutiny while she pointed at the most transparent person in the room.

When the Sam frame collapsed and the group converged on Quinn — someone who wasn't even there — Vic turned to Blake. She buried memories and collected $505,000 in payment under accounts bearing her own name. Not a pseudonym. Not a code. Her name. The woman who watched NeurAI's fraud unravel on a laptop screen last night responded this morning by suppressing evidence and pocketing the proceeds under a name so traceable it might as well be a signature.

Vic is traveling to an undisclosed location to recover from trauma. Unavailable for comment.

Sources also saw Alex Reeves in hushed conversation with Blake — one of the first investigators he pulled aside. Whatever was discussed, consider what Alex did for the rest of the morning. The code NeurAI runs on is hers. The cease and desist demands she be installed as CTO. If the company survives with Alex at the helm, she finally gets what Marcus stole from her at BizAI. Blake's network could have facilitated exactly that arrangement. A quiet deal. The rightful owner restored. NeurAI lives on under competent management.

Instead, Alex sits at zero on the financial ledger. Her cease and desist is escalating into a multimillion-dollar lawsuit — against a company whose value she helped destroy by confirming the fraud last night. That's either a rejection of whatever Blake was offering, or a very expensive bet that the truth is worth more than the inheritance. I don't know which. I know what it cost her.

Meanwhile, someone collected $1.2 million from Blake's network for burying five memories — and named their shell account "NeurAI." Five entries in fourteen minutes, all between 4:35 and 4:49 AM. The account name is a tell. Not a personal name like Vic's. A corporate name, chosen to make the payments look institutional, to throw anyone reading the ledger off the scent of whoever was actually collecting. The memories suppressed under that name all contained evidence damaging to NeurAI — stolen code, dangerous experiments, financial misconduct. Someone was being paid to bury evidence that would destroy the company's value, and they named their payment account after the company they were protecting.

What's Missing

A recovered memory from 1:36 AM captures Sarah Blackwood finding Jess Kane in the bathroom, crying. She put together that Marcus's baby was Jess's. What the memory shows next is a kind of grace I didn't expect from this investigation.

Grace Under Devastation
SARAH.4 - 1:36AM - You find JESS in the bathroom, crying, and suddenly put it together. You squeak out between unexpected tears: "MARCUS's baby is yours?" She's sobbing so hard now that she can't even speak, so she nods. You thought you'd be outraged, but seeing her here in front of you, you just feel resigned. You say: "You can have him. But that baby deserves better. We both do."
Sarah Blackwood

"That baby deserves better. We both do." Sarah came to that party to confront her estranged husband. She left it discovering the full scope of his betrayal and chose solidarity over rage. That was last night.

Sources saw Sarah having hushed conversations with Blake this morning. During the accusation deliberations, she hung back — not driving toward any suspect, not advocating, not building a case the way Remi or Alex did. Everyone else in that room had a theory. Sarah just listened.

The recovered memories fill in why. Marcus had already cleaned out their shared bank account. Sarah had no job, no independent income. But she wasn't unprepared. Mel Nilsson, the Patchwork Law Firm attorney, was building her divorce case. Riley Torres had traced Marcus's shell companies and insider trading schemes — all of it for Sarah. She was better positioned than anyone in that room knew.

Now consider what happens to Sarah's divorce claim if NeurAI's fraud goes public. The company collapses. The code Alex proved worthless is the only significant asset in the marital estate. Every shell company Riley traced leads to a company that no longer has value. Alex's honesty last night is devastating for everyone, but it is financially catastrophic for Sarah specifically.

I cannot confirm who named their shell account "NeurAI" and collected $1.2 million for burying evidence. But I can observe who was seen in hushed conversations with Blake this morning, who went quiet during accusations, and who had the clearest motive — not just to suppress evidence that would tank the company's value, but to collect $1.2 million while doing it. Sarah had no income. Marcus had cleaned out their accounts. She was facing destitution. Blake's network was paying.

Vic is accounted for. Traveling, unavailable, but located. Sarah Blackwood's whereabouts have been unknown since the investigation concluded.

Marcus Blackwood is dead. I reported the group's accusation of Quinn Sterling. The motive is documented. I'm reporting their verdict. I'm not endorsing it. The accused wasn't in the room.

Blake was busy this morning. Sources saw multiple investigators approach him in private. What happened after those conversations is its own kind of evidence. Vic wrote checks under her own name and tried to frame the most transparent person in the room before the group accused someone who couldn't fight back. Alex walked away from Blake with nothing on the ledger — at real cost to her own claim on the company she built. Sarah, who showed a stranger grace in a bathroom at 1:36 AM, went quiet when the money started moving and disappeared when the investigation ended. Remi followed a high man through a secret door last night and came back with the proof that toppled an empire.

And then there's Sam. His best friend is dead. His livelihood died with him. Blake's network was paying good money this morning for every memory an investigator was willing to bury. Sam had more reason than anyone to take that deal — no income, no patron, no safety net. He spent the morning giving evidence away instead. For free. While the venture capitalist who tried to frame him collected half a million dollars under her own name. While someone collected $1.2 million under NeurAI's name for burying the evidence that would have destroyed the company Sarah needs intact for her divorce. While the group Sam helped inform accused someone who wasn't there to defend themselves.

Sam is the only investigator being called in by police for further questioning. Not Vic, who collected $505,000 and left for an undisclosed location. Not Sarah, who disappeared entirely. Sam — the one who lost his best friend, lost his livelihood, refused to sell, and gave everything away.

That's not a broken system. That's the system working as designed. Blake's network paid for silence this morning and found willing sellers. The person who couldn't be bought is the one still answering questions.
