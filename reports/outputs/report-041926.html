A Chemist, A Blackmail, And Six Woken-Up Partygoers: How The Accusation Against Quinn Sterling Landed

Marcus Blackwood is dead. His memory-extraction protocol ran on six of his own partygoers before the night ended. The group who woke up in the warehouse settled on the drug chemist he had been blackmailing. Three other names almost got there first.

Cassandra Nova | NovaNews · February 21, 2027 · Article NNA-041926-26

Quinn Sterling is accused of murder. Not as a cold killer. As someone who saw what was coming and decided to stop it.

Six people woke up in a warehouse this morning with no memory of how they got there. Marcus Blackwood was dead. The drug that stripped their recall had been formulated by Quinn Sterling, the chemist Marcus had spent the last of his life blackmailing, and manufactured by Sam Thorne, one of the six people in the warehouse. Before the group landed on Quinn, the deliberation nearly landed on three of those six. Sarah Blackwood. Jess Kane. Sam herself. Each had a motive on the whiteboard. Only one had her own memories in the public record to corroborate or refute it.

I have been tracking Marcus Blackwood and NeurAI for years. I was not at the party. The tips came through this morning. What follows is what the record shows, what the record will not show, and the $6.44 million shaped hole in the middle of it.

The Story

Start with the accusation the group settled on, because everything else orbits it. Quinn Sterling had buried Psychotrophin-3A safety research years before the party. They buried it because the drug induced neural plasticity in doses that were not safe in any hands, let alone Marcus Blackwood's. Somehow Marcus found out anyway. At 9:59 PM, according to Quinn's own recovered memory, he was blackmailing them for another batch. The meeting was set for ten o'clock at the bar.

The Blackmail
Somehow, MARCUS found out about the unusual side effects of Psychotrophin-3A, and is obsessed with its ability to induce neural plasticity in large doses. Why did you bury the PT-3A safety assessments? Now he's got you by the balls and is blackmailing you to continue with your experiments to develop it. This drug is dangerous, and should not be in the wrong hands. But MARCUS wants you to bring him another batch of it tonight. You're to meet him at the bar at 10.
Quinn Sterling

What Quinn did next is where the sympathetic framing was built. At 12:02 AM, they sought counsel from Ezra Sullivan. Ezra is the closest thing Silicon Valley has to a moral compass, and Quinn had to explain the situation three times before they took off their rose-colored glasses. At 12:52 AM, Quinn confessed the whole arrangement to Cass Zhang and Zia Bishara, the collaborators they had burned years earlier to bury PT-3A in the first place. Zia forgave them. Cass hugged them. In the hour after that confession, Marcus ran the extraction.

The crime itself sits in the victim's own recovered memory. Marcus at 1:50 AM, past the point of control, trying to walk drugged attendees through the codes that would seal each extracted memory to its owner. Sam was among the drugged. He commands her by name to take her own extracted memory and lock it. She wanders off mid-instruction. Sarah, also drugged, stands at a wall with tears streaming. His AI never worked. The protocol he built to cover for that failure did.

The Protocol
The extractions worked. The instructions were simple: your memory is now outside your body, sealed with a code only you know. Lock it. Keep it. Protect it however you can. But the drugged attendees wander off mid-instruction. Marcus tries to hand Sam her own extracted memory to lock. She walks away. Sarah stands at a wall, tears streaming. It is chaos. No one is holding their own code anymore. Drugged people make terrible assistants.
Marcus Blackwood

Before the group landed on Quinn, they nearly landed on three other names. The deliberation shows how close the accusation came.

Jess Kane first. At 8:17 PM she caught Marcus's eye, wanting two minutes of his attention about a relationship that was not a relationship anymore, carrying a child he had already decided not to acknowledge. He walked past her without a word.

The Dismissal
You managed to catch his eye. All you wanted were 2 minutes alone with MARCUS to talk about your relationship. You need to know whether it's over for good. But MARCUS just walked right by, without a word. He's a man who's used to actions without consequences. You're no longer worthy of his attention. Suddenly, you can't hold the tears back. A stranger leans over and offers to buy you a cocktail. HA! That's the last thing you need right now.
Jess Kane

Sam Thorne next. At 9:42 PM, Sam told Nat Francisco that she had been spending her days making an experimental memory drug for Marcus in an abandoned warehouse with weird lighting. Nat did not know whether to be sympathetic or angry. The account Nat left behind shows Sam panicking about Marcus over-using the compound. The anxiety is not about Marcus as a person. It reads, on review, like exposure. Sam enabling Marcus meant Sam adjacent to Marcus when the structure collapsed.

The Entanglement
SAM just told you that they've been spending their days making this experimental memory drug in a secret laboratory with weird lighting in an abandoned warehouse. It all feels plucked from some fictional, "evil genius" storyline. MARCUS has been taking the drug, and you don't know whether to be sympathetic or angry at SAM for enabling MARCUS, and then worrying about him. But these are two grown adults, so you decide to tell SAM to go find MARCUS and talk it out.
Nat Francisco

Then Sarah Blackwood. The claim raised against Sarah during deliberation was that Marcus had stolen from her. It came up on the whiteboard. It did not come up in any of the 44 recovered memories the group brought to the detective log, because none of Sarah's own memories did. My research indicated that Sarah Blackwood had had at least 4 memory tokens extracted from her. Zero reached the public record. The strongest support Sarah had in the log was built from other people's recovered memories. Riley Torres left a voicemail at 11:43 PM mapping Marcus's shell companies and telling Sarah the money was hers. Mel Nilsson had the paternity test and the infidelity evidence. The case for Sarah the victim was large. The case for Sarah the suspect lived and died in the whiteboard margin.

The whiteboard held the theories. The ledger held the transactions. The two told different stories, and only one could be verified.

An account bearing Sam's name opened the morning's main burst of burial activity at 8:36 AM. Six transactions in sixty seconds. $1.35 million, the session's third-largest shell. During deliberation, Sam denied operating it. This reporter cannot verify or refute that. What the ledger shows is the name on the account and the clock.

Sam's denial is the contested version of what Jess's admission is the confirmed version of. At 9:00 AM, a different account closed the burial window with three transactions in sixty seconds. Total: $825,000. The account was named Jess. In the deliberation, Jess stood up and said the account was hers. She said she had buried her own memories under her own name because she is carrying a child and wanted resources for it. Of five observable accounts and $6.44 million in buried evidence, she is the only operator who named herself.

The recovered record also shows why that admission lands harder than it first reads. At 11:04 PM Nat Francisco documented that Jess had already been a subject of an earlier extraction. Her memory of that experience was wiped in the process.

Already Extracted
JESS tells you that they know about SAM's memory drug. It's called Psychotrophin-3A, and MARCUS is using it, as well as some environmental controls, to extract human memories from willing subjects. Jess experienced it themselves, but can't tell you much because their memory was wiped in the process.
Nat Francisco

Vic Kingsley had been running a triangulation strategy. His VC firm was funding two competing AI companies. NeurAI, Marcus's. And AIBioComp, run by Remi Whitman, another of the six people who woke up in the warehouse. A January 29th email from Remi to Vic, recovered at the venue, made the pitch explicit: Marcus's codebase was outdated, his grasp of his own technology was thin, and Vic's $20 million was backing the wrong horse. At 9:55 PM, Vic steeled himself for a bar meeting in which he would resolve the problem a different way. Alex Reeves, Marcus's former BizAI cofounder, sitting on a cease-and-desist letter she had never fired, was the replacement he had picked. At 11:39 PM, Jamie Woods overheard the deal close after a fistfight. Sarah's name threaded through it. Marcus was out. Three of the six people in this warehouse had motives on the whiteboard against him. Two more had working alternatives ready to plug in.

The Coup Plan
You steel yourself before you head to the bar for your meeting. This is not going to be a fun party. But you are going to rip off the band-aid, spill the beans, AND kill two birds with one stone. ALEX is in, MARCUS is out. No more C & D. No more potential criminal conspiracy charges. You've just got to do this, quiet like and ASAP, so that NeurAI remains a viable investment.
Vic Kingsley

The morning's highest-earning shell account, at $1.835 million, used none of these names. It was called Memory. Nine transactions spread across nine minutes starting at 8:50 AM. Most concentrated in the opening burst, with stragglers at 8:57 and 8:59. The director observed Vic spending the morning making deals to keep his name out of the various evidence piles. That is observable behavior. The connection to the Memory account is not. I can note the pattern. I cannot close the loop.

Follow the Money

Five accounts. $6.44 million in buried extracted memories. The burial window opened at 8:22 AM with a single transaction to an account called Group, then exploded at 8:36 AM and stopped at 9:00 AM. Thirty-eight minutes end to end. The bulk of it compressed into the middle twenty-four. In that window, the session's entire market activity concentrated itself and then stopped. The record shows who acknowledged their account, who denied theirs, and which accounts named no one at all.

The Jess account closed burial at 9:00 AM with an acknowledged operator. The Sam account opened the main burst at 8:36 AM with a denied one. The Memory account sat in the middle with the largest total and the most anonymous name a person can pick. Group, at $1.53 million, ran a different pattern entirely: repeat visits to the market across three separate clusters, eight extracted memories across thirty-five minutes rather than a single consolidating trip. Morgan, at $900,000, points to a name no roster player carries. The financial record of this session is not a list of the guilty. It is a map of what the system allows people to hide.

The Players

Six people woke up as victims of a crime and spent the morning building a case against the man who had done it to them. That is not a neutral starting position. The record shows who pushed the hardest into the public evidence pile and who made other choices with the same morning.

Alex Reeves and Sam Thorne contributed the most to the public record. The director observed both of them at the evidence board repeatedly, pushing memories into the log rather than into an account. Remi Whitman worked the paper evidence with Sarah during the mid-session document hunt and cracked the memory-lockbox codes alongside Sam. The unglamorous investigative labor was theirs. None of that is dramatic. All of it is why the group had anything to deliberate over at all.

Sam is the session's most photographed player, the denier of the contested shell, and the player whose recovered memory at 9:42 PM shows her already panicking about being pulled under with Marcus. She also put in the hours at the board. More than one thing can be true.

Remi Whitman was the sixth person in the warehouse and the least visible in the recovered memory record. What the memory log does not carry, the paper evidence does. Remi is CEO of AIBioComp, one of the two AI companies Vic was funding in competitive tension. A January 29th email recovered at the venue shows Remi making the pitch to Vic directly: Marcus's codebase was outdated, his grasp of his own technology was thin, redirect the capital. That is a motive with a paper trail and a business plan attached. Remi spent the morning cracking memory-lockbox codes alongside Sam and working the paper-evidence hunt with Sarah. Whatever Remi wanted out of Marcus's death, she put her hands into the public record to pursue it. She did not hide her motive. She filed it with a VC.

Vic Kingsley arrived with a criminal-conspiracy concern and spent the morning managing his exposure to it. The director observed him making multiple deals to keep his name out of the evidence piles. That is a VC who understood exactly where his risk was and worked the room accordingly. It is not a scandal. It is a strategy. Jess Kane is the only player who stood up and named herself. She said she buried memories under her own name for her child. That takes a different courage than denial.

Sarah Blackwood is the fourth character in the deliberation's standoff, and the one the record treats most strangely. She appeared in Marcus's own final memory at 1:50 AM, a drugged attendee staring at a wall. She appeared in Riley's voicemail as the beneficiary of the whole shell-company paper trail. She appeared on Mel's paternity-test evidence as the wife Marcus had cheated on. Every recovered memory of Sarah's own experience during the party, all four of them, was buried. None reached the detective log. The whiteboard said Marcus had stolen from her. The public record could neither confirm nor refute that, because Sarah's public record did not exist. She was the loudest silence in the room.

What's Missing

The record has edges. Here is where they are.

The theft accusation against Sarah surfaced in deliberation and this reporter has been unable to find any corresponding evidence in the recovered memory record. The whiteboard noted it. No exposed memory supports it. The claim lived in the deliberation and died there.

An account called Sam absorbed $1.35 million in six burials over one minute. Sam denied it. The account's name is Sam. The operator is unknown. Both sentences are accurate at the same time. The highest-earning account of the morning, Memory, took $1.835 million across nine transactions over nine minutes. The director observed Vic making deals that morning. The pattern is observable. The connection is inference.

Three of the six people in the warehouse were nearly accused before Quinn's name was circled. Each one's exposure in the public record looks different. Jess owned her shell. Sam denied hers. Sarah has none, not because she lacked motive on the whiteboard but because every one of her own memories from the party was buried. The ledger shows $6.44 million of silence across thirty-eight minutes. One acknowledged operator. One denied. Three accounts naming no one. And one roster member whose four recovered memories the detective will never read. The buried market did not just move money. It moved motive.

What This Means

The group accused Quinn Sterling. Not as a cold killer, but as the one person in range of Marcus who understood what Psychotrophin-3A was doing to him and who had something concrete to withhold. That is the verdict they arrived at. My job is to record it, not revise it.

The harder question is the one the accusation does not touch. Marcus's AI did not work. The whiteboard had it boxed and undisputed. What worked was the memory-extraction protocol, formulated by Quinn, manufactured by Sam, borrowed from Cass Zhang's Synesthesia Engine without payment or consent, and pushed into execution by Skyler Iyer's midnight ultimatum. It was delivered on six drugged attendees whose only crime was accepting an invitation. Quinn withheld the drug at the end. Cass identified the stolen code. Kai Andersen found the match. Ezra Sullivan sat for an interview with Ashe Motoko they have declined to give any reporter for forty years. The people who saw what was happening and tried to stop it, or to document it, were in that warehouse. So were the people who bought silence in minute-long bursts.

Three of those six had motives for Marcus on the whiteboard. Jess acknowledged her shell and her reason. Sam denied hers. Sarah's own four memories were buried so completely that the deliberation had nothing to corroborate or refute the theft accusation against her. No record at all of what Sarah had seen, wanted, or lost during the party. The case had to land somewhere. The only suspect left was the one who had not walked into the warehouse this morning. Quinn Sterling was convenient because Quinn was not in the room to defend themselves. Quinn was safe because accusing Quinn required nobody in the deliberation to turn on the person sitting beside them. Six people walked out of that warehouse with the same answer. That was the point.

Jess Kane bought her child's future with three burials and told the room why. Sarah made her motives disappear without saying a word. The market treats both moves the same: a name on an account, a number on a screen, a payment received. That is the system Marcus built, and that the system around Marcus rewarded until the morning it killed him. Marcus is dead. The machinery that made him rational, the machinery that lets six survivors of a memory crime decide collectively to point at someone who is not there, is not.
