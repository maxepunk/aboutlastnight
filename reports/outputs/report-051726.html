Marcus Blackwood's Death Investigation Decided NeurAI's Next CEO

Eighteen people gathered to investigate Marcus Blackwood's death. By the time they left, the verdict had named the one suspect who had already fled. His widow was on the path to inherit his company.

Cassandra Nova | Senior Investigative Reporter || Ashe Motoko | Guest Reporter · February 22, 2027 · Article NNA-051726-26

Eighteen people gathered in a warehouse this morning to investigate Marcus Blackwood's death. I watched from inside the room. Twelve voted Zia Bishara guilty in retaliation for stolen tech. Three for Vic Kingsley. One for Morgan Reed. The room had been tied between Vic and Zia until Vic stood up and gave a speech that moved the vote.

By the time I sat down to write, word had reached me from inside NeurAI. The company is preparing to name Sarah Blackwood its next CEO once the noise dies down. Meanwhile, I was unable to get a comment from Vic Kingsley. Her assistant tells me she is on her way to the Cayman Islands to recover from the trauma of last night. Alex Reeves' lawsuit against NeurAI is still active. Zia's whereabouts remain unknown.

I want to walk you through what the room concluded. Then I want to walk you through what the verdict required to be true. They are not the same picture.

The Story

The whiteboard case for Zia is clean. Marcus stole Zia Bishara's memory-indexing algorithm and rebuilt it under the NeurAI name. Zia had proof. Zia had a motive. Zia, conveniently, was not in the warehouse to argue. The verdict hinged on this IP theft. But what exactly did Marcus steal, and from whom?

Start with Alex Reeves. At 10:44 PM the night before, Sam Thorne caught Marcus high enough to admit on the record that the BizAI algorithm Marcus took credit for was Alex's work. Patchwork Law had already served NeurAI with a cease and desist on Alex's behalf. This morning Alex approached me directly to report it in person, with the addendum that the company's marquee product does not actually work. I overheard Vic's reply to Alex's claims: 'the way you kids talk these days.' The dismissal would matter later.

12:31 AM. Same variable names. Same comments.
REMI hands you the laptop. You punch in the default password for the Oracle Ledger and it works. Next, you pull up a code comparison. Left: your Oracle Ledger commit from 2023. Right: NeurAI's 'proprietary' system from 2027. Same variable names. Same logic flow. Same comments. MARCUS didn't even bother changing your notes. Sloppy thief. Too bad the idiot didn't catch the massive bug that you've since fixed.
Zia Bishara

Zia's case is the most damning single piece of IP-theft evidence the morning produced. By 11:10 PM the night before, Zia had told Ashe Motoko on the record that Marcus had 'borrowed temporarily' and never returned the code. Hours later, Zia had the proof. By the time the room found it, Zia was gone. Twelve hands went up for the person who could not add a single sentence in their own defense.

Then Cass Zhang. At 12:38 AM the night before, Kai Andersen had matched the code Marcus insisted she install in her party rig against the demo Cass had sent him. Identical. Marcus had installed Cass's Synesthesia Engine without paying, without signing, and intended to run it on his guests as live research subjects. A third stolen technology. A third documented victim. Yet the whiteboard did not list Cass as a suspect.

12:38 AM. Identical.
Code comparison. Left: the Synesthesia Engine code you sent MARCUS for your demo. Right: The last minute code MARCUS insisted KAI incorporate into their installation for tonight's party. Identical. MARCUS is secretly using the Synesthesia Engine before signing the contract and paying you for it. What's more, he wants KAI to run it tonight on an unsuspecting party audience. Why would he want to do that? What's his agenda here?
Cass Zhang

A fourth pattern made the prior three easier to overlook. Taylor Chase had handed Marcus the leverage to bury a Silicon Valley Business Journal expose on his memory experiments. The reporter who wrote it lost her job. In exchange, Taylor got the NeurAI launch exclusive. The reporter was Ashe Motoko. The email confirming the burial is on the record; the expose excerpt survives in Nat Francisco's files. Ashe came back to Marcus's party undercover as Kai's plus-one. She was here to run her own investigation and we joined forces this morning. That is why her name is on this article's byline.

9:51 PM. 'The story's not about the truth.'
TAYLOR: 'I can't believe that ASHE is even showing their face here. What a loser. I mean, look. They had the receipts on MARCUS. But they never understood that the story's not about the truth. No sirree… I know you gotta keep some doors open. Don't get me wrong. Sucks to be ASHE - grinding away at their blog or whatever, then showing up here as some loser's plus one… It makes me appreciate my spot on the VIP list. Gotta be grateful for the small things, you know? And I did get the exclusive on NeurAI's launch out of it.'
Taylor Chase

At 11:19 PM, Skyler Iyer's attention had drifted from a Cass pitch on the Synesthesia Engine to Marcus and Morgan in angry standoff across the room.

Morgan Reed knew most of this. She did not go to the police. At 11:22 PM the night before she met Marcus at the hammock with proof of insider trading, fraudulent reports, and shell-company records, and gave him a choice: come clean publicly, or she handed everything to Senator Walsh. Marcus reached into his bag and handed her a bribe instead. Flip witnessed the cash handoff seven minutes later. Morgan took the money. This morning, I overheard Vic pull Morgan aside, saying: 'I'm really concerned about the state of our investments.' Last night, Marcus was having a similar conversation with Morgan.

11:22 PM. 'Take care of it, will you?'
You're both on the hammock. You show him proof: bribes, fraudulent reports, shell company records, stolen IP... You say: 'Go public about the insider trading and take full responsibility or I give all of this damning evidence to Senator Walsh.' His face says he's considering it, but there's something else there. Instead of answering, he reaches into his bag and hands you a generous bribe. MARCUS: 'Take care of it, will you? Just like you always do,' then leaves without giving you a chance to respond.
Morgan Reed

Then there is Marcus' crumbling personal life. By 11:43 PM, Riley Torres was leaving Sarah a voicemail with the full map of Marcus's shell companies. The money is yours. By 12:50 AM, Mel had called an emergency Stanford Four meeting and was openly weighing blackmail to recover Marcus's money for Sarah. Mel's email to his partners at Patchwork Law Firm, announcing he had taken Sarah on as a client effective immediately, had been timestamped 2:08 AM the morning of the party. Almost a day before. Sam Thorne had already mailed Sarah a paternity test naming Jess Kane as the mother of Marcus's child. This morning I watched Mel turn on Sam: 'You had these diary entries the WHOLE Time? Why didn't you tell me? Now break it down, I need like an AI summary of all this.' The marriage was over before the party was. So was the friendship.

11:43 PM. 'The money is yours.'
You call SARAH. You're not sure where she disappeared off to. No answer, so you leave a voice-mail: 'MARCUS worked with MORGAN to set up a slew of hidden investment accounts. We're talking cover organizations, liability shields, blood money, environmental degradation, insider trading... I've mapped the shell vehicles. The paper trail is clear. When you're ready, we blow this wide open. The money is yours. Love you! Also… I'm going to find Flip. Don't judge me!'
Riley Torres

I saw Riley pull Sarah aside during the investigation. I caught what he told her: 'I mean he's gone, so the best you can do is move on, and get on with your life.' At the time it sounded like grief counsel. In light of what came next, it read more like inheritance counsel. The investigation produced a verdict. The deals made with Blake produced something else entirely.

Follow the Money

While eighteen people conducted an investigation, a parallel economy ran through Blake's tablet. Approximately seven and a half million dollars in extracted memories. Thirty-two transactions across fifteen named accounts. The burial market opened at 08:13 AM, twenty minutes before the first piece of evidence reached my hands. Whoever buried first was already moving before the investigation opened.

The Ziadidit account had its only transaction at 09:04 AM. Four hundred fifty thousand dollars, transferred in the closing minutes of the investigation. The room was getting ready to name their suspects. The account itself had been registered at Blake's table under that name, Ziadidit, visible to the all who would be voting on Zia as the prime suspect minutes later. Someone was not betting on which way the room would go. Someone was telling the room which way to go.

One other account uses Morgan Reed's full name, I have no way of knowing if this was really Morgan's personal account or a shell opened up to place a target on her back.

The Jamie account is the largest haul on the ledger: one and a half million dollars across seven burials. Jamie admitted ownership openly during deliberations, citing his need for the cash and how he personally had nothing to hide. The room accepted it.

At 08:38 AM, I watched Vic Kingsley ask Blake, 'I would like to disappear this evidence,' and watched Blake peel off with her into a corner I could not hear. At 08:38 AM the Maria account opened its first burial. Maria received two more, at 09:00 and 09:07, totaling six hundred twenty-five thousand across mention-tier and party-tier values. Of Vic's memories, only the one in which she and Alex discover NeurAI's AI is worthless reached the public record. The other three are unaccounted for. I cannot tell you who walked memories to Blake. I can tell you what I watched, and I can tell you what the Maria account seemed to absorb.

The Players

Two people brought their cases directly to me this morning. Alex Reeves came to report Marcus's BizAI theft and the lawsuit already in motion. Ashe Motoko came to disclose that she was the reporter Taylor and Marcus had silenced. Both put themselves on the record before they had to. The deliberation that followed used what they brought.

Earlier in the morning I had interviewed Jamie. 'It's just speculation,' he said sheepishly, 'but I think someone killed Marcus over funding. A rival competitor perhaps.' When it came time for final deliberations, his speech was the kind of synthesis no one expects from a bartender. He laid out the documents and the people who might have moved against Marcus. Then he admitted the Jamie burial account was his and explained he needed the money. The room accepted it. But within moments, the deliberation where he laid out all that he'd seen to the gathered crowd became a settlement. The group seeking out the safest path out of the warehouse.

The suspect list was being assembled on the whiteboard by Ashe. Alex rushed up to the whiteboard moments before the deliberation began, saw his own name listed among possible suspects, and exclaimed 'Why is my name up there?!?' Marcus had stolen his code; motive enough for someone to consider it. The room moved on. His name was taken off the board. Ashe built the case against Zia. The counter-narrative arrived: someone named Vic. Remi defended her. Morgan's bribery scheme surfaced as alternate motive. The room held a brief tie. Then Vic stepped forward. She said she did not know much about Marcus's personal life, or what he may or may not have stolen. She said that given all the trouble he was in, perhaps he had killed himself. She said Morgan seemed to know a whole lot more about Marcus's life than she did. The room moved. Twelve for Zia. The pivot was the speech.

Blake called the final vote as the police closed in, then led the entire group out and left me behind to convey the findings. But it seemed to me the negotiations that shaped the result of those final deliberations were in motion much earlier this morning.

What's Missing

I cannot tell you what is in the memory token that were never recovered, but I can tell you a pattern that I saw: the people most adjacent to the morning's central threads also have the most memories that are still unaccounted for. Whether that means they sat on evidence, or someone else found their tokens and chose not to surface them, I cannot say.

Meanwhile, The Pikachu account holds nine hundred twenty-five thousand dollars across five burials. It is the largest unclaimed shell of the morning, aligned with the morning's clearest beneficiary by way of an account name no one stepped forward to claim. I am not making an attribution. I am just asking if the NeurAI board of directors offered any special benefits to whomever was willing to bury the most damning company secrets.

Twelve people voted for Zia Bishara. The evidence supports them. Zia had cause, means, and a documented grievance against Marcus Blackwood. Zia's whereabouts remain unknown. The case is clean. It is also the only verdict the room could agree on that did not require anyone present to answer for anything.

Marcus built a company on stolen memory-extraction technology. The room investigating his death used a memory market that lets you put a name on a verdict before the verdict has been named. Someone did. Four hundred fifty thousand dollars on Zia, the account named Ziadidit, sitting on the public screen before eighteen people sat down to deliberate. By the time deliberations started, the question was no longer who did it. The question was whether the room would ratify the answer that had already been deployed in the financial record. Sarah Blackwood inherits NeurAI. Vic flies to the Caymans. . Morgan still has the bribe money Marcus handed her on the hammock. Marcus's machinery did not die with him. It just changed hands.

I watched Alex and Sarah have that private conversation under the MARCUS graffiti this morning. 'Oh my god, I'm glad we agree,' he said. 'I understand we have some shared strengths,' she answered. Two of the people in the room with the most to inherit, in quiet alignment under the name of the man whose death made the inheritance available. The question remains regarding whether the Cease and Dessist case filed by Alex will continue running unimpeded after the events of last night.

I am not saying Zia did not kill Marcus. I am saying Marcus's party guests produced a verdict that exonerated those fortunate enough to have been in the room where it all happened. I am saying the financial architecture of that verdict was assembled at Blake's window before the vote was called. I am saying the company built on three stolen technologies is about to be inherited by the spouse of the man who stole them. I am saying Vic Kingsley pivoted a tie into a twelve-vote majority for Zia and then left the country.

This article carries two bylines. Ashe Motoko's prior story on Marcus Blackwood was killed by a source on the inside, coordinating with Marcus. The email documenting that burial is now on the public record. Ashe returned to Marcus's party undercover as Kai's plus-one and stayed through the morning to help build the case she could not finish alone the first time. I took the same notes everyone else did. I do not know if Zia killed Marcus. I know someone put a four hundred fifty thousand dollar account named Ziadidit on Blake's table before the room sat down to talk about Zia. They were not predicting the vote. They were placing it. The story Marcus and Taylor tried to kill came back with both our names on it. That is the only verdict I will be stamping tonight.
